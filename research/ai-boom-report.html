8 · Applications & Models

Highest reward / highest risk
The software people actually use, and the frontier labs that build the brains. Margins & ROI still unproven; valuations extreme; commoditisation risk as model makers move into apps.

7 · Cloud & Neoclouds

Landlords of compute
Rent GPUs by the hour. The hyperscalers do this at huge scale & profit; the "neoclouds" are pure-play, fast-growing, and heavily debt-financed.

6 · Servers & Systems

Integration layer
Bolt GPUs, memory & networking into racks. Real AI revenue at scale — but thin margins mean the dollars don't convert like the silicon does.

5 · Networking & Optical Interconnect

The data-movement bottleneck
Once a model spans thousands of GPUs, moving data between them is the limit. Copper breaks down past 200Gb/s → smart cables, 1.6T optics, retimers & fabric switches. Several genuine small-cap rockets here.

4 · Power, Cooling & Grid

The binding constraint
The scarcest resource in AI is now electricity. US data-centre load may double to ~150GW by 2028 with 5-year grid queues. Generation, electrical gear and liquid cooling all show 40–240% order growth.

3 · Memory & Storage

The Micron zone 💎
Each GPU needs stacks of HBM, which eats ~3× the wafer capacity of normal DRAM — so AI is starving the entire memory market. This is exactly the cyclical + secular squeeze that made the multibaggers. Also the most "late in the move."

2 · Compute — GPUs & Custom Silicon

The kingpin layer
The chips that do the math. Nvidia's merchant GPUs dominate; hyperscalers increasingly design their own ASICs (with Broadcom/Marvell) to escape Nvidia's pricing. AMD is the #2 challenger.

1 · Foundry & Equipment

The chokepoint
Everyone's chips — Nvidia, AMD, every ASIC — are made by TSMC on ASML's machines. The narrowest, lowest-substitution part of the entire boom. If you believe in AI but distrust any single chip designer, this is the hedge.

Under the hood

Two deep dives

The memory super-cycle and the ASIC-vs-Nvidia battle are the two themes that matter most for finding the next multibagger — and both are frequently misunderstood.

💾 The Memory Super-Cycle

Why Micron 3×'d, why the market is now pricing in a roll-over, and what comes next with HBM4.
  • What HBM is: High Bandwidth Memory — DRAM dies stacked in 3D on the same package as the GPU. Each H100 needs 6 stacks (~80GB). Each Blackwell GB200 needs 8. HBM3e eats ~3× the DRAM wafer capacity of standard memory.
  • Why it squeezed: Three suppliers (SK Hynix, Micron, Samsung) all converted DRAM capacity to HBM simultaneously — starving the regular DRAM market and creating a multi-year demand surplus for HBM.
  • The Micron template: Cyclical trough (DRAM was loss-making in 2023) + secular AI layer on top (HBM) + earnings inflection (GM guided from ~20% → ~81%) + starting valuation that didn't price it in = multibagger math.
  • Where we are now: HBM supply is contracted through 2026; TAM forecast ~$100B by 2028. BUT Micron crossed $1T market cap in May 2026. Fwd P/E of ~8–9 is the market screaming "cyclical peak incoming."
  • HBM4 / Vera Rubin: Jensen Huang confirmed all three suppliers shipping HBM4 in Q3 2026. New cycle extending the runway — but much is already priced.
2023Trough. Micron loses money; NAND prices collapse. MU trades at ~$50.
2024Recovery. AI drives HBM demand; SK Hynix sells out. MU re-rates to $150+.
May '26Micron crosses $1T. Sandisk +3,300%. The multibagger has already happened.
2026–27Transition. HBM4 ramp for Vera Rubin; question is whether cycle extends or rolls.

⚔️ ASIC vs. Nvidia — Who Wins?

Hyperscalers are designing their own chips to escape Nvidia's pricing. Here's what's real vs. hype.
  • Why hyperscalers build ASICs: Nvidia's GB200 costs ~$30–40K per chip with ~70% gross margins. A custom ASIC — tuned for inference, not training — can be 3–5× cheaper per token at scale. For Meta running Llama on 600K+ GPUs, the maths forces it.
  • The ASIC duopoly: Broadcom () and Marvell () design ~80% of merchant custom silicon. AVGO builds Google's TPU (Ironwood), Meta's MTIA, OpenAI's chip. MRVL anchors AWS Trainium and Microsoft's Maia.
  • Broadcom's bet: ~$100B AI revenue target by 2027. Its AI revenue was $10.8B in Q2 FY26, +143% YoY. Google now sells its AVGO-built TPU to others (Anthropic, Meta) — a stunning reversal.
  • The honest limit: ASICs are excellent at inference (running a fixed model at scale) but struggle with training (where Nvidia's CUDA ecosystem is an almost-unassailable moat). As training jobs dwarf inference today, Nvidia's share stays high.
  • The 2027 risk for Nvidia: If inference spend eclipses training spend — and model architectures stabilise — ASIC economics become devastating for Nvidia's margins. That's the long-term bear case that Michael Burry is betting on.
  • Net verdict: ASIC share grows, but Nvidia doesn't lose training dominance for years. AVGO is the cleanest play on the trend; MRVL is richer and faces more share pressure.

🔍 Google & Apple — Different Playbooks

Google goes big on external AI bets; Apple acquires quietly and builds internally.
  • Google → Anthropic: ~14% stake + up to $40B new (April 2026) at a $380B Anthropic valuation. $10B upfront, $30B contingent. No voting rights or board seats.
  • Google → Waymo: ~75% ownership, $126B valuation after $16B Series D (Feb 2026, Alphabet contributed ~$13B of it). 450K+ paid rides/week.
  • GV (Google Ventures): Most active CVC in 2024 (46 rounds). AI picks: Harvey (legal AI, $5B val), Lightmatter (photonic computing), Sierra, Synthesia.
  • Google AI Futures Fund (May 2025): early Gemini model access + Google researcher support alongside equity.
  • Apple — the quiet accumulator: 32 AI startups acquired in 2023 (most of any Big Tech); DarwinAI acquired 2024 (neural-net compression). Never takes minority equity stakes in frontier labs.
  • Apple strategy: On-device AI (Apple Silicon) + Private Cloud Compute (own servers, not Azure/AWS) + "Project ACDC" custom AI server chips. $500B US investment plan over 4 years.
  • Apple × OpenAI deteriorating: ChatGPT integrated in iOS 18 (no cash/equity exchanged) but Apple reportedly signing ~$1B/year deal with Google to use Gemini for Siri instead.
The main event

Hunting the next Micron

You asked specifically about multibaggers like MU. Here's why Micron worked — and the framework for spotting the next one before it runs.

🧬 The multibagger DNA

Micron didn't 3× because of a great story. It worked because of a precise setup you can screen for:

  1. Cyclical trough → peak swing (memory was loss-making in 2023).
  2. A secular AI layer on top so the up-cycle runs hotter and longer.
  3. Earnings inflection: Micron's fwd gross margin guided from ~20% → ~81%. EPS went vertical.
  1. A starting valuation that didn't price it in — the magic ingredient.
  2. A chokepoint position: sold-out capacity, contracted years out.
  3. Visible, immediate demand — not a 2030 promise.

✅ Metrics that flag the next one

  • Revenue acceleration — growth getting faster, not just high.
  • Margin inflection — gross margin stepping up = pricing power kicking in.
  • Backlog / RPO growth — contracted future revenue (Vertiv +109%, Oracle +325%, GE Vernova $163B).
  • Rule of 40 rising (growth% + margin% > 40, trend matters more than level).
  • Reasonable forward P/E vs. that growth — the part everyone forgets.
  • Chokepoint / sold-out — pricing power you can verify today, not in theory.

⚠️ The honest catch on memory

  • The biggest gains in this theme already happened: Sandisk +~3,300%, Micron to $1T+, WDC/Seagate +100%+ — all in the last ~12 months.
  • Micron's forward P/E of ~8–9× is the market screaming "these earnings won't last." Low forward P/E in a cyclical = late, not cheap.
  • From here it's a bet on cycle duration (does the super-cycle run into 2027+?), not discovery.
  • Memory always over-builds and crashes. HBM being contract-sold makes this cycle sturdier — but doesn't repeal physics.

Both sides, honestly

Is it a super-cycle or a bubble?

The single biggest question for your money. The honest answer is "we don't know yet" — so here's the strongest version of each case.

🟢 The super-cycle case

  • Real cash, not 1999 vapor. The rally leaders are the most cash-generative companies in history, trading at ~half the multiple of 2000's leaders. Ex-Oracle, the Big Four can fund 2026 capex from operating cash flow.
  • Demand is contracted, not hoped-for. HBM sold out through 2026; Seagate/WDC nearline allocated through 2027; Oracle RPO $553B; GE Vernova backlog $163B. That's signed revenue.
  • The bottleneck is physical. Power and memory shortages are real and slow to fix (5-yr grid queues), which sustains pricing power for the pick-and-shovel layers.
  • "Circular" can be virtuous — it's how you pre-build supply for genuinely exploding compute demand.
  • Enterprise AI is inflecting. Palantir's Rule of 40 = 145%; ServiceNow cRPO +22.5%; Snowflake NRR 126%. The revenue is beginning to arrive.

🔴 The bubble case

  • Circular financing fragility. $800B+ of interlocking deals; OpenAI losing ~$14B in 2026. One stumble can unwind the loop. Nvidia's $100B OpenAI deal already got shelved.
  • ROI is unproven. A 2026 NBER study found ~90% of firms saw no productivity impact from AI yet. If revenue doesn't catch capex by ~2027, "patience runs out."
  • Hidden depreciation. Michael Burry (now short SOXX/PLTR/NVDA) argues AI hardware's real useful life is ~2.5 years, so Big Tech is understating depreciation and overstating earnings.
  • Cash flow is turning. Buildout is shifting from self-funded to debt (>$400B hyperscaler issuance expected); Amazon's FCF reportedly fell ~95%.
  • Extreme valuations in spots: Palantir (fwd P/E ~82), Tesla, the neoclouds. Priced for perfection with no margin of safety.
Balanced bottom line: Fundamentals are materially stronger than the dot-com era, which supports a "super-cycle, not bubble" base case. But extreme valuations in the application layer, circular financing, aggressive depreciation assumptions, and unproven enterprise ROI mean the downside is real if revenue fails to catch capex by ~2027. The picks-and-shovels layers (foundry, power, memory, networking) are where demand is most visible and contracted.

🎯 The one mental model to keep

Multibaggers come from asymmetry the market hasn't priced — a small base + accelerating, contracted demand + a valuation that doesn't yet believe it. By that test, the memory names are late (huge gains already banked, low forward multiples flashing "peak"), while the interconnect, power-producer and chokepoint layers are where that asymmetry looks freshest in mid-2026. The richest application-layer names (Palantir, neoclouds) offer the biggest dreams but the thinnest safety margin. Match the layer to your risk tolerance — and respect that the whole edifice rests on capex eventually being justified by real revenue.
