The 60-second version

The whole boom on one page

The AI trade is a layered supply chain. A handful of hyperscalers (Microsoft, Amazon, Google, Meta, Oracle) are spending ~$650B+ in 2026 on data centers. That capex flows down the stack into chips, memory, networking, power and cooling — and the model labs (OpenAI, Anthropic) sit on top, often funded by the very chip & cloud vendors they buy from.

The fuel

~$650–690B of hyperscaler capex in 2026 (Gartner sees ~$2.5T total AI spend, +44% YoY). This is the single biggest number in the whole story.

The kingpin

NVDA still ~70% of AI accelerators, doing a $75B data-center quarter. But custom ASICs (Broadcom/Marvell-built) are the fastest-growing threat.

The squeeze

Memory (HBM) and power are sold out. That scarcity is what minted the Micron / Sandisk / SK Hynix multibaggers — and it's the heart of the "is it sustainable?" debate.

The catch

Much of it is circular: Nvidia funds OpenAI/CoreWeave who buy Nvidia chips; OpenAI commits $250B to Azure + $300B to Oracle. ~$800B+ of such loops. Bulls call it a virtuous cycle; bears call it a bubble.

8 · Applications & Models

Highest reward / highest risk
The software people actually use, and the frontier labs that build the brains. Margins & ROI still unproven; valuations extreme; commoditization risk as model makers move into apps.

7 · Cloud & Neoclouds

Landlords of compute
Rent GPUs by the hour. The hyperscalers do this at huge scale & profit; the "neoclouds" are pure-play, fast-growing, and heavily debt-financed.

6 · Servers & Systems

Integration
Bolt GPUs, memory & networking into racks. Real AI revenue at scale — but thin margins mean the dollars don't convert like the silicon does.

5 · Networking & Optical Interconnect

The data-movement bottleneck
Once a model spans thousands of GPUs, moving data between them is the limit. Copper breaks down past 200Gb/s → smart cables, 1.6T optics, retimers & fabric switches. Several genuine small-cap rockets here.

4 · Power, Cooling & Grid

The binding constraint
The scarcest resource in AI is now electricity. US data-center load may double to ~150GW by 2028 with 5-year grid queues. Generation, electrical gear and liquid cooling all show 40–240% order growth.

3 · Memory & Storage

The Micron zone 💎
Each GPU needs stacks of HBM, which eats ~3× the wafer capacity of normal DRAM — so AI is starving the entire memory market. This is exactly the cyclical + secular squeeze that made the multibaggers. Also the most "late in the move."

2 · Compute — GPUs & Custom Silicon

The kingpin layer
The chips that do the math. Nvidia's merchant GPUs dominate; hyperscalers increasingly design their own ASICs (with Broadcom/Marvell) to escape Nvidia's pricing. AMD is the #2 challenger.

1 · Foundry & Equipment

The chokepoint
Everyone's chips — Nvidia, AMD, every ASIC — are made by TSMC on ASML's machines. The narrowest, lowest-substitution part of the entire boom. If you believe in AI but distrust any single chip designer, this is the hedge.

The main event

Hunting the next Micron

You asked specifically about multibaggers like MU. Here's why Micron worked — and the framework for spotting the next one before it runs.

🧬 The multibagger DNA

Micron didn't 3× because of a great story. It worked because of a precise setup that you can screen for:

  1. Cyclical trough → peak swing (memory was loss-making in 2023).
  2. + a secular AI layer on top (HBM demand) so the up-cycle runs hotter and longer.
  3. Earnings inflection: Micron's forward gross margin guided from ~20% to ~81%. EPS went vertical.
  1. A starting valuation that didn't price it in — the magic ingredient.
  2. A chokepoint position: sold-out capacity, contracted years out.
  3. Visible, immediate demand — not a 2030 promise.

✅ Metrics that flag the next one

  • Revenue acceleration — growth getting faster, not just high.
  • Margin inflection — gross margin stepping up = pricing power kicking in.
  • Backlog / RPO growth — contracted future revenue (Vertiv +109%, Oracle +325%, GE Vernova $163B).
  • Rule of 40 rising (growth% + margin% > 40, trend > level).
  • Reasonable forward P/E vs. that growth — the part everyone forgets.
  • Chokepoint / sold-out — pricing power you can verify.

⚠️ The honest catch on memory

  • The biggest gains in this exact theme already happened: Sandisk +~3,300%, Micron to $1T+, WDC/Seagate +100%+ — all in the last ~12 months.
  • Micron's forward P/E of ~8–9× is the market screaming "these earnings won't last." Low forward P/E in a cyclical = late, not cheap.
  • From here it's a bet on cycle duration (does the super-cycle run into 2027+?), not discovery.
  • Memory always over-builds and crashes. HBM being contract-sold makes this cycle sturdier — but doesn't repeal physics.

Both sides, honestly

Is it a super-cycle or a bubble?

The single biggest question for your money. The honest answer is "we don't know yet" — so here's the strongest version of each case.

🟢 The super-cycle case

  • Real cash, not 1999 vapor. The rally leaders are the most cash-generative companies in history, trading at ~half the multiple of 2000's leaders. Ex-Oracle, the big four can fund 2026 capex from operating cash flow.
  • Demand is contracted, not hoped-for. HBM sold out through 2026; Seagate/WDC nearline allocated through 2027; Oracle RPO $553B; GE Vernova backlog $163B. That's signed revenue.
  • The bottleneck is physical. Power and memory shortages are real and slow to fix (5-yr grid queues), which sustains pricing power for the pick-and-shovel layers.
  • "Circular" can be virtuous — it's how you pre-build supply for genuinely exploding compute demand.

🔴 The bubble case

  • Circular financing fragility. $800B+ of interlocking deals; OpenAI losing ~$14B in 2026. One stumble can unwind the loop. Nvidia's $100B OpenAI deal already got shelved.
  • ROI is unproven. A 2026 NBER study found ~90% of firms saw no productivity impact from AI yet. If revenue doesn't catch capex by ~2027, "patience runs out."
  • Hidden depreciation. Michael Burry (now short SOXX/PLTR/NVDA) argues AI hardware's real useful life is ~2.5 years, so Big Tech is understating depreciation and overstating earnings.
  • Cash flow is turning. Buildout is shifting from self-funded to debt (>$400B hyperscaler issuance expected); Amazon's FCF reportedly fell ~95%.
  • Extreme valuations in spots: Palantir (fwd P/E ~82), Tesla, the neoclouds. Priced for perfection.
Balanced bottom line: Fundamentals are materially stronger than the dot-com era, which supports a "super-cycle, not bubble" base case. But extreme valuations in the application layer, circular financing, aggressive depreciation assumptions, and unproven enterprise ROI mean the downside is real if revenue fails to catch capex by ~2027. The picks-and-shovels layers (foundry, power, memory, networking) are where demand is most visible and contracted — and historically where the cleanest multibaggers live.

🎯 The one mental model to keep

Multibaggers come from asymmetry the market hasn't priced — a small base + accelerating, contracted demand + a valuation that doesn't yet believe it. By that test, the memory names are late (huge gains already banked, low forward multiples flashing "peak"), while the interconnect, power-producer and chokepoint layers are where that asymmetry looks freshest in mid-2026. The richest application-layer names (Palantir, neoclouds) offer the biggest dreams but the thinnest safety margin. Match the layer to your risk tolerance — and respect that the whole edifice rests on capex eventually being justified by real revenue.
