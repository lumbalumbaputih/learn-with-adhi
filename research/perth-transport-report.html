Car City or
Transit Shift?

Perth is globally famous for car dependence. But a $13 billion rail program and record public transport patronage hint at something changing. We dug through government budgets, grey reports, and peer-reviewed papers to find out what's really going on.

🚗 Modal share data 💰 Road vs rail budgets 🏙️ Urban sprawl analysis 📚 Peer-reviewed + govt sources Perth, Western Australia
The one-line answer

Still a car city — but the fastest-shifting one in Australia

Perth is structurally built for cars and statistically the most car-dependent mainland capital. But it's now also expanding its rail network faster than any other Australian city, and public transport patronage just hit an 11-year high. It's a city in genuine tension — and the tension is real.

⚠️ Why the Census overstates PT a little

The 2021 Census was conducted with Perth's Fremantle rail line closed for maintenance — reducing PT mode share on Census day. The PATHS household travel survey (which samples across the whole year, not a single day) gives the more reliable picture: 4.2% PT for all trips, not just work.

🛤️ Context on the METRONET numbers

The $4.8 billion figure is state funding for METRONET only — it excludes Commonwealth contributions ($2.9B extra), ongoing Transperth operations, bus network costs, and the Perth City Deal active transport investment. The roads figure covers the entire state including major regional freight routes (Bunbury Outer Ring Road, Great Northern Highway etc.). Like-for-like metro comparisons would narrow the gap, but roads still dominate on any honest accounting.

The historic pattern

1950s
The Hepburn-Stephenson Metropolitan Plan sets Perth on a low-density, car-oriented trajectory. Freeways and suburban blocks become the primary investment.
1970s–80s
Rail services shrink. Car ownership climbs. Perth ranked by Newman & Kenworthy as having among the world's highest car use per capita.
1991
Joondalup Line opens — the first major new rail investment in decades. PT mode share begins a slow recovery from its 9% nadir.
2007
Mandurah Line opens — the most expensive single infrastructure project in WA's history at the time. Mode share recovers to 10.4%.
2017–present
Labor government launches METRONET: 18 projects, 72 upgraded stations, $13B+ total spend. It becomes the defining infrastructure program of the era.
2022–24
Forrestfield–Airport Link (Oct 2022), Yanchep Rail Extension, and Morley–Ellenbrook Line all open. 10 of 18 METRONET projects complete by end 2024.
2026
March 2026: all-time monthly PT record — 15.7 million boardings across train, bus, and ferry. Smashes the previous record from March 2012 by 1.5 million trips.
Section 03

The city that couldn't stop sprawling 🏙️

You can't understand Perth's car dependence without understanding its shape. The urban form is the single most powerful driver of transport behaviour — and Perth's form is extreme.

Three of every five newly-built homes in Perth are on the outer urban fringe — far from rail, often without frequent buses.

47%
The official planning target: 47% of new homes as urban infill. Perth consistently misses this target.
Density target by 2050 (Perth @ 3.5M)
+50%
The WAPC's Perth and Peel @ 3.5 Million plan aims for 50% higher average density and 380,000 homes near METRONET stations.

📏 The planning gap: targets vs reality

Academic research (MDPI, 2017) found that WAPC underestimated Perth's actual sprawl by a significant margin. The official estimate suggested the boundary expanded from 637 km² to 813 km² between 2001–2012. Satellite-based measurement found the real expansion was from 747 km² to 1,050 km². Planners were optimistic; physics won.

Why density matters for transport

At 10 dwellings per hectare, running a frequent bus service is economically marginal at best — there simply aren't enough people per square kilometre to fill buses or justify stations. The spatial structure forces car use even on residents who'd prefer alternatives. CEDA analysis quantifies the efficiency loss: a freeway lane moves ~2,500 people/hour; a train corridor moves ~50,000 — 20× the throughput. Perth's shape means it defaults to the less efficient option for most of its geography.

📌 The missing link: no mode shift targets

A notable gap in Perth's policy suite: as of 2024, there are no published, binding mode shift targets connecting METRONET investment to measurable reductions in car use. The strategy documents set density and housing targets, but Perth has not committed to, say, "reduce private car journeys to work to 55% by 2035." This is a significant accountability gap that advocates and Infrastructure WA have flagged.

✅ What is working: fare policy as a lever

Perth has used fare policy aggressively and with measurable success. Free Sunday travel is now permanent. The Summer of Free Travel (Dec 2023–Jan 2024) produced a 42% increase in SmartRider boardings compared to the same period the prior year. Free school transport is expanding. This shows demand is price-elastic — when the barrier drops, people switch.

🔑 Why Perth is recovering faster than other cities

  • Western Australia had lighter COVID restrictions than the eastern states — so the baseline drop was smaller and recovery started earlier
  • Multiple new METRONET lines opened in 2022–24, adding new catchments and destinations
  • Aggressive fare policy (free Sundays, Summer of Free, student passes) reduced cost barriers
  • Strong WA economy is driving more commuter trips, and METRONET now serves many new employment corridors
Section 06

Why car dominance is so sticky 🏛️

Understanding what has kept Perth car-dependent for 70 years requires looking at the political economy, not just the planning decisions. Several structural forces make change difficult — and a few are now weakening.

🚗 Forces keeping Perth car-first

  • The Hepburn-Stephenson legacy: Perth's 1950s metropolitan plan hardwired car-oriented suburban form into 70 years of subsequent development. Spatial lock-in is almost impossible to undo by policy alone.
  • The road lobby network: Academic research identifies a "system of provision" connecting automotive, oil, road construction, concrete, steel, insurance, and motoring club industries — all with aligned interests in road investment. Road-building is framed as economic common sense across the political spectrum.
  • The fiscal feedback loop: Only 33 cents in every dollar collected from WA motorists is returned to the State for roads. This is used as an argument for more road spending rather than demand management or PT.
  • FIFO mining economy: WA's resource boom depends on fly-in-fly-out workers in remote areas where PT is physically impossible. This embeds a car-positive economic identity at the state level.
  • Low-density NIMBY coalition: Established suburban residents — who vote — consistently oppose infill density near their homes, making the land-use transformation needed to support PT politically difficult.

🚌 Forces pushing toward transit

  • Congestion costs: CEDA and Infrastructure WA estimate Perth's congestion costs are heading toward $3.6 billion per year by 2031. The economic case for PT is becoming undeniable even to conservative economists.
  • METRONET's political popularity: The program proved durable across electoral cycles — unusual for big PT investments. It survived changes in government sentiment and was increased, not cut, in successive budgets.
  • Fare policy results: Free Sunday travel and the Summer of Free Travel produced measurable, immediate ridership spikes — giving politicians concrete win stories for PT investment.
  • RAC WA's evolved position: The Royal Automobile Club — historically Perth's prototypical road lobby — now actively advocates for PT investment, active transport, and urban planning reform. The old coalition is fragmenting.
  • Climate and health pressure: Australian Transport Assessment and Planning (ATAP) guidance and WA's emissions targets are pushing planners toward active and public transport options.

The road pricing question

Infrastructure WA's Recommendation 58 called for investigating location- and time-of-day road pricing — essentially congestion charging — as a demand management tool. The WA Government responded in February 2023: Rec 58 "partially supported." EVs will pay per-kilometre from 2027. But full congestion pricing for Perth's CBD or orbital corridors remains politically untouchable — governments across the political spectrum have been unwilling to give up fuel excise revenues or face the political backlash.

Section 07 · The Verdict

Perth in 2026: A car city in the fastest transit transition in Australia

Perth is structurally car-dependent — this is not an opinion, it's what the mode share data, car ownership statistics, and urban form all say. A city where 4.2% of all trips go by public transport, 90.9% of households own a car, and the urban form stretches 271 km at 10 dwellings per hectare cannot honestly be described as transit-oriented.

But "car city" is no longer the full story. Perth is also expanding its rail network faster than any other Australian city, recording its highest PT patronage in 11 years, and making structural fare interventions (free Sundays, free school travel) that are producing measurable behaviour change. The political economy is shifting: the old road lobby has fractured, the economic cost of congestion is now impossible to ignore, and METRONET has proven politically durable in a way that previous PT investments didn't.

The honest verdict: Perth has shifted from deeply car-dependent to car-oriented with meaningful transit investment. To become a genuinely transit-supported city (15–20%+ all-trip mode share), it would need land-use transformation around METRONET stations that is planned but largely unbuilt, and demand management tools (road pricing) that remain politically untouchable. Those are decade-scale changes. For now: still a car city, but the fastest-shifting one in Australia.
