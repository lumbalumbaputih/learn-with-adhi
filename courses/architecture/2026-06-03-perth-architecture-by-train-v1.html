Module 01 · The Lens

How to Read a City

Perth is one of the most isolated cities on Earth, and you can read that story in its buildings. Before we walk the platforms, let us learn the lens – why Perth looks the way it does, and how to date a building at a glance.

What you'll learn
  • The four forces that shaped every Perth building: isolation, climate, boom-and-bust, and Country
  • A quick visual vocabulary – how to date a building from the footpath
  • The six eras this course follows, on one timeline
  • Why the train network is the perfect way to read the city

Why Perth Looks the Way It Does

Every city is an argument between its people and its place, frozen in stone, brick, and concrete. To read Perth, hold four forces in mind. Once you can name them, you will see them in almost every building.

🌏
Isolation
Perth is closer to Jakarta and Singapore than to Sydney. Materials, styles, and skilled labour arrived late and by ship. Local stone – limestone, jarrah, brick – did the heavy lifting, giving early Perth a homemade, hand-built character.
☀️
Climate
A hot, dry Mediterranean climate with fierce summer sun off the Indian Ocean. Deep verandahs, shaded arcades, thick walls, and (later) sun-hoods and louvres are climate machines, not just decoration.
💰
Boom & Bust
Perth grows in violent surges – the 1890s gold rush, the 1960s–70s mineral boom, the 2000s resources boom. Each leaves a layer of ambitious buildings. Quiet decades leave almost nothing. The skyline is a graph of commodity prices.
〰️
Country
The city sits on Whadjuk Noongar Country, beside the Derbarl Yerrigan (Swan River). For 65,000+ years this was a living cultural landscape. Today's best buildings are learning to design with that Country, not over it.
A useful myth to drop: Perth was not founded as a convict settlement. The Swan River Colony of 1829 was a free settlement of British migrants. Convicts only arrived in 1850 – invited later, to supply the labour the struggling colony desperately lacked. That 21-year gap explains a lot about what got built, and when.

Dating a Building From the Footpath

You do not need to be an architect to date a building. You need three habits: look up (above the shopfronts, where the original facade survives), look at the material, and look at the ornament. Here is the cheat sheet we will fill in over the next six modules.

Why Read Perth by Rail

Here is the lovely accident: Perth's railway is itself a heritage object, and its lines thread through exactly the places where the city's history piled up. The Fremantle line – opened in stages from 1881, when the Fremantle–Guildford railway became the colony's first public railway – still runs past the gold-boom port, the colonial gaol towns, and the beach suburbs. The newer underground lines drop you under the contemporary waterfront.

So this course has two halves. Modules 2–7 build your eye, era by era, tagging every landmark with its nearest station. Module 8 turns that knowledge into actual train itineraries you can do on a weekend with a SmartRider and a flat white. By the end, a station platform becomes a doorway into a particular decade.

Field habit: every landmark in this course carries a STATION tag showing the nearest train stop and its line colour. Collect them as you go – Module 8 reassembles them into routes.

Module 02 · 1829–1890

Convict Stone & Colonial Gothic

Perth's oldest buildings are small, tough, and hand-made – limestone and brick raised by a struggling colony and, after 1850, by convict labour. This is the bedrock layer, and almost all of it is a short walk from a station.

What you'll learn
  • The two men who built early Perth: Reveley and Jewell
  • Why so many early buildings are Gothic – and how to spot it
  • The convict story written into Fremantle Prison and the Town Hall
  • The five oldest buildings you can still visit, and their stations

A Colony Built by Hand

For its first 20 years the Swan River Colony was poor, under-populated, and chronically short of skilled labour. Buildings were small and practical, made from what was underfoot: soft coastal limestone, locally fired brick, and the magnificent local hardwood, jarrah. The dominant style was Gothic Revival – the fashionable "Christian" and institutional look back in Britain – scaled down to colonial means: pointed arches, steep gables, and battlemented parapets that made a modest stone box look like a little fortress.

Two figures shaped this era. Henry Willey Reveley, the colony's first civil engineer, designed its very first public buildings. Then Richard Roach Jewell arrived and, as the government's go-to architect for decades, gave Perth most of its surviving colonial landmarks – the Town Hall, Government House, the Cloisters, the Old Perth Boys School.

The convict turn (1850–1868): Desperate for labour, the colony asked Britain to send convicts. Around 9,700 arrived. They quarried stone and built the roads, bridges, the Fremantle Prison, and – uniquely – much of the Perth Town Hall. It is often described as the only town hall in Australia built by convicts.

How to Spot Colonial Gothic

The Round House gives us the era's purest diagram. Built in 1830–31 as the colony's first gaol, it is a 12-sided panopticon – cells arranged around a central yard so a single guard at the centre could watch every door. The form is the idea: surveillance made architecture.

The Round House, Fremantle (1831) – Panopticon Plan

Module 03 · 1890–1914

Gold! The Boom Years

In the 1890s, gold was found in the eastern goldfields and Perth exploded. The wealth poured into grand, confident buildings – domes, columns, iron-lace verandahs – that still give the city centre and Fremantle their richest streetscapes.

What you'll learn
  • How gold turned a sleepy colony into a building site
  • The Federation styles – and how to read a boom-era facade
  • George Temple Poole, the architect of public Perth
  • Fremantle's West End: the best gold-boom precinct in Australia

The City That Gold Built

Gold strikes at Coolgardie (1892) and Kalgoorlie (1893) detonated a population boom. Perth's people, port, and tax base multiplied, and a frenzy of construction ran right up to the outbreak of war in 1914. Where the colonial era whispered in limestone, the gold era shouted in render and pressed brick: tall parapets to hide the roof and carry the building's name, classical columns and pediments borrowed from banks and palaces, and across the suburbs, the deep filigree verandahs dripping with cast-iron lace.

This is the moment Perth grew up. It coincides with Federation (1901), and the architecture of the period takes that name. You will hear sub-styles – Federation Free Classical for grand public and commercial buildings, Federation Filigree for the iron-lace verandahs, Federation Queen Anne for the red-brick-and-terracotta houses with their turrets and tall chimneys.

Reading a Boom-Era Facade

Boom buildings are designed to be read from the street as a stack of three. Train your eye to separate them and you can date almost any commercial building from this era at a glance.

Anatomy of a Gold-Boom Commercial Facade
The golden rule of street-reading: the ground floor lies. Shopfronts are remodelled every generation, so a 1900 building can wear a 2025 café below. The real age lives in the upper floors and the parapet. Always look up.

George Temple Poole, Architect of Public Perth

George Temple Poole ran the colony's Public Works Department through the boom and gave Perth a suite of dignified public buildings in brick and stone. His work bridges the refined end of the colonial era and the swagger of the gold years, and much of it still anchors the cultural heart of the city around the old Museum and Art Gallery precinct in Northbridge.

The era's showpiece, though, is a theatre. His Majesty's Theatre (1904), designed by William G. Wolf, is a lavish Edwardian Baroque pile – Perth's last grand Edwardian theatre still working, all curved pediments and exuberant plasterwork. Locals just call it "The Maj."

Module 04 · 1918–1939

Tudor Fantasy & Art Deco

Between the wars Perth split in two moods: a nostalgic, mock-Tudor cosiness, and a sleek, machine-age Art Deco optimism. They sit a block apart in the city – and learning to tell them apart is one of the most satisfying tricks in the whole course.

What you'll learn
  • London Court – Perth's beloved mock-Tudor arcade and its gold-baron backstory
  • How Art Deco arrived in 1934 and what it borrowed from ships, machines, and Egypt
  • The Deco motif kit: zigzags, sunbursts, speed-lines, portholes
  • Where to find the best inter-war buildings, by station

London Court – A Tudor Daydream

In 1937, the flamboyant gold financier Claude de Bernales built a shopping arcade between Hay Street and St Georges Terrace and dressed it head-to-toe as Tudor-and-Elizabethan England: timber-look gables, leadlight oriel windows, a St George-and-the-dragon clock at one end and Big Ben at the other. London Court is not "real" history at all – it is 1930s fantasy, built four centuries after the style it imitates. But it is gloriously done, and it tells you something true about inter-war taste: between two world wars and a Depression, people craved cosy, story-book nostalgia.

Reading tip: revival styles are honest about being copies once you know the dates. If a "medieval" building has shop windows, electric lighting and a 1937 plaque, it is Revival – a deliberate costume, not a survivor. London Court and Government House both wear Tudor, but a century apart and for opposite reasons.

Art Deco Arrives, 1934

The other inter-war mood looked forward, not back. Art Deco reached Western Australia around 1934 and swept through cinemas, hotels, shops and apartment blocks in a post-Depression surge of optimism. Where Tudor revival was hand-made and fussy, Deco was streamlined and modern, borrowing its glamour from ocean liners, aeroplanes, cars, and the recently opened tomb of Tutankhamun. Local architects like Marshall Clifton, William Leighton and Harold Krantz worked it in black glass, chrome, and coloured render.

Deco has an unmistakable kit of motifs. Once you can name them you will spot Deco from across the street – often hiding in plain sight above a suburban shopfront or a 1930s block of flats.

Module 05 · 1945–1980

Concrete & Optimism

After the war, Perth threw off ornament entirely. A hosting of the 1962 Commonwealth Games and a 1960s–70s mineral boom gave the city its first modern towers, its boldest Brutalist landmarks, and a homegrown master of the concrete block.

What you'll learn
  • What Modernism rejected – and why buildings suddenly went plain
  • Council House: Perth's best Modernist building and its sun-hood facade
  • Perth Concert Hall and the white-concrete drama of Brutalism
  • Iwan Iwanoff, the émigré who made concrete blocks sing

Less, But Sunnier

Post-war Modernism made a clean break: no parapets pretending to be palaces, no Tudor gables, no iron lace. The new buildings were honest grids of concrete, steel and glass, their beauty coming from proportion, structure, and the play of light – not applied decoration. In Perth's harsh sun, Modernism found a local accent: the sun-hood, a projecting concrete shade over every window, turning a climate problem into the whole look of a building.

The catalyst was civic pride. Perth hosted the 1962 British Empire and Commonwealth Games, and the city dressed up for the world. The young firm Howlett & Bailey won the commissions that would define Perth Modernism – first Council House, then, a decade later, the Concert Hall.

Council House (1963) – The Sun-Hood Facade

Brutalism: Honest, Heavy, White

By the 1970s Modernism had grown muscular. Brutalism – from the French béton brut, "raw concrete" – celebrated mass, structure, and the texture of bare concrete straight from the timber moulds. Perth's masterpiece is the Perth Concert Hall (1973), again by Howlett & Bailey: the first concert hall built in Australia after the war, poured in crisp white off-form concrete, and still rated among the country's finest rooms for sound. People love or loathe its "wedding-cake" bulk – but everyone agrees the acoustics are sublime.

Brutalism's reputation has swung from despised to adored. Many of Perth's best examples now carry heritage protection precisely because a younger generation reads their boldness as beautiful, not brutal.

Iwan Iwanoff, Sculptor in Concrete Block

No Perth modern story is complete without Iwan Iwanoff (1919–1986), a Bulgarian-born architect who settled here in 1950 and turned the humblest material in the builder's yard – the grey concrete block – into sculpture. His suburban houses, especially the dazzling Marsala House in Dianella, are covered in bold geometric block patterns: a one-man fusion of Modernism, Brutalism, and pure imagination.

Train honesty: Iwanoff's best houses are scattered through the northern and north-eastern suburbs – wonderful, but not next to a station. Treat them as a "graduation field trip" by car or bus once your eye is trained. The two buildings you can reach on foot from a platform – Council House and the Concert Hall – are both in the city, near Elizabeth Quay.

Module 06 · 1990–now

Glass, Water & the New City

The 2000s resources boom and a wave of public projects rebuilt Perth's edges: a reconnected waterfront, a sunken railway turned into a square, a museum that wraps its own history, and a stadium you reach by train. This is the layer you walk through today.

What you'll learn
  • The contemporary moves: glass towers, adaptive reuse, and reconnecting the city to its river
  • Elizabeth Quay, Yagan Square, the WA Museum and Optus Stadium – who built them and when
  • "Façadism" vs. genuine adaptive reuse – and how to tell
  • How the new lines drop you right into this layer

Three Contemporary Moves

Read today's Perth and you will see the same three strategies again and again:

🏙️
The Glass Tower
Resources money built the skyline. Central Park (1992) has stayed the tallest for decades; later towers like the Brookfield Place tower (2012) cluster at the river end of St Georges Terrace.
♻️
Adaptive Reuse
Instead of demolishing heritage, the best new projects keep and repurpose it – the State Buildings reborn as COMO The Treasury (2015), or old facades stitched into new towers.
〰️
Back to the Water
A generation of public works has reconnected the CBD to the Swan River and across the rail line – Elizabeth Quay, Yagan Square, the Matagarup Bridge to the stadium.
Façadism vs. adaptive reuse: when only a heritage skin is propped up with a tower jammed behind it, critics call it "façadism" – keeping the costume, losing the body. Genuine adaptive reuse keeps real rooms and gives the old building a real new life. The State Buildings are the gold standard; watch the towers around them and decide for yourself which is which.

Module 07 · The Deepest Layer

Reading Country

Long before 1829, this place was Boorloo, on Whadjuk Noongar Country, beside the Derbarl Yerrigan. That story is the oldest architectural layer of all – and the newest buildings are finally learning to design with it. Learn to read it and the whole city changes.

What you'll learn
  • Whose Country Perth sits on, and the names that carry it
  • What "designing with Country" means in practice
  • The six Noongar seasons – and where they shaped a building
  • How to "see" Country in the contemporary city

Boorloo, on Whadjuk Country

The land Perth occupies is Boorloo, part of the territory of the Whadjuk people, one of fourteen groups of the Noongar nation of Australia's south-west. The Swan River is Derbarl Yerrigan; Kings Park is Kaarta Gar-up; Heirisson Island, beside the stadium, is Matagarup. These are not just translations – they record a cultural landscape of seasons, songlines, and gathering places that is tens of thousands of years old, and that the colonial grid was laid straight over the top of.

For most of Perth's built history this layer was ignored or erased. The shift in the last decade – visible in Yagan Square, the WA Museum, Optus Stadium and Elizabeth Quay – is one of the most important things happening in the city's architecture, and it gives you a richer way to read every site.

Designing With Country

Designing with Country means starting from the land and its First People's knowledge rather than treating a site as a blank rectangle. In practice, recent Perth projects have done this through genuine collaboration with Whadjuk elders and artists, and it shows up in four readable ways:

  • Story & naming – buildings carry Noongar names and meanings (Boola Bardip, "many stories"; Yagan Square, for the Noongar resistance leader Yagan).
  • Water & sightlines – plans that re-open views and movement to the river, the way the land always pulled people toward Derbarl Yerrigan.
  • Material & colour – earthy, local palettes and artwork that reference Country rather than imported imagery.
  • Seasons & cycles – design organised around the six Noongar seasons rather than the European four.
The Six Noongar Seasons – woven into Optus Stadium's design
Why it matters for reading: the European calendar gives four seasons; the Noongar year has six, tied to wind, heat, rain and flowering. When Optus Stadium's designers organised parts of the project around these seasons, they were letting the building keep local time. Notice it and a stadium stops being generic – it becomes of this place.

How to See Country in the City

You do not need special access to read this layer. Stand at Elizabeth Quay and notice how the design pulls you toward the water. Read the Noongar names on plaques and station signage. Look for artwork and material that references the wetlands, the river, the six seasons. Visit Kings Park / Kaarta Gar-up, the great escarpment over the city, and feel why this high ground above the river mattered for thousands of years. The colonial city is only the top 0.3% of the timeline – Country is the other 99.7%.
