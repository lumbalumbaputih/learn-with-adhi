The Rumah Harmonis Philosophy

Why Three Frameworks?

Most Indonesian families already live inside all three of these philosophies simultaneously - they just haven't had a language to unite them. The Rumah Harmonis approach says: you don't have to choose. A well-designed Indonesian home can be climatically intelligent, energetically balanced, and spiritually aligned at the same time.

Each framework brings something the others lack. Tropical design gives you science: sun angles, rain patterns, material properties, ventilation physics. Feng Shui gives you a spatial grammar: how energy flows between rooms, which directions carry which qualities, how furniture placement shapes mood and relationship. Islamic values give you purpose: why the home exists, who it shelters, what it expresses about the family's faith and hospitality.

The Indonesian Family Context

Indonesia sits between 6°N and 11°S latitude - entirely within the tropics. The climate is warm and humid year-round, with a wet and dry season. The country is 87% Muslim, meaning most families have specific spatial needs: a musholla (prayer room), wudu areas, gender-separated guest spaces, and a home that expresses modesty to the outside while being generous on the inside.

At the same time, Chinese-Indonesian cultural influence has made Feng Shui deeply embedded in Indonesian building practice - often blended with local Javanese and Balinese cosmological thinking that shares many of the same principles.

How Conflicts Get Resolved

The three systems sometimes point in different directions. When they do, this course applies a clear priority order:

  1. Islamic obligation first - qibla orientation, mahram privacy, and wudu access are non-negotiable.
  2. Tropical physics second - a hot, badly ventilated house causes real health and comfort harm.
  3. Feng Shui as refinement - within the space left by the first two, Feng Shui guidance improves flow and harmony.
Key insight: Most of the time, the three frameworks agree. A south-facing main bedroom (tropical: avoid direct afternoon sun) also happens to be a Feng Shui command position, and faces Mecca from most of Java. The conflicts are rarer than the convergences.

Module 02 · Tropical Design

Building With the Climate, Not Against It

Indonesia's tropics are demanding. Master the sun, wind, rain, and heat - and your home becomes naturally comfortable without heavy air conditioning.

  • Cross-ventilation and stack effect strategies
  • Rain, humidity, and flood elevation design
  • The best tropical building materials and why

The Sun Problem

At Indonesian latitudes (roughly 0–8° South), the sun passes almost directly overhead at noon. The dangerous solar exposure comes from the west and east facades in the morning and afternoon - not the south as in temperate climates. A west-facing wall absorbs heat all afternoon, making the rooms behind it unbearably hot by 3–4pm.

Sun Path - Java (6°S) · Key Orientations

The practical rule: orient your main living spaces and bedrooms to face north or south. North and south facades receive the least direct solar radiation in equatorial Indonesia, staying cooler through the day. Reserve east and west facades for service areas, wet areas, or add deep overhangs and louvers to shade them.

Cross-Ventilation & The Stack Effect

Before air conditioning, tropical architecture solved heat with two natural systems:

💨
Cross-Ventilation
Place inlet openings on the windward side, outlet openings on the leeward side. Prevailing winds in Java blow from the south-east (May–October) and from the west (November–April). Design for both seasons.
🔥
Stack Effect
Hot air rises. High vents or a raised roof ridge allow hot air to escape upward, drawing cooler air in from low openings near the floor. The taller the space, the stronger the stack effect.
🌳
Vegetation Buffer
Trees on west and east sides block solar radiation before it hits the wall. A tree canopy can reduce wall surface temperature by 8–12°C. Choose tall species with high canopies that don't block ground-level breezes.

Rain, Humidity & Elevation

Indonesia receives 2,000–4,000mm of rain per year in most urban areas. This creates two design imperatives that Feng Shui and Islamic aesthetics will later reinforce: deep overhangs (protecting walls from rain-driven moisture) and elevated floors (keeping the home above flood and splash-back zones).

Traditional wisdom meets physics: The Javanese joglo roof, the Batak rumah adat, and the Minangkabau rumah gadang all feature extreme roof overhangs - 60–120cm beyond the wall line. This is not decoration; it is a moisture management system validated over centuries.

Module 03 · Feng Shui

Feng Shui: Flow, Balance, and the Family Map

Feng Shui is a spatial grammar that describes how energy (Qi) moves through a home and how that movement affects the wellbeing of everyone inside.

  • The Five Elements and their material/color correlations
  • Command positions for beds, desks, and cookers

What Is Qi?

Qi (pronounced "chee") is the Chinese concept of life-force energy that flows through everything - people, land, buildings. Good Feng Shui arranges a home so that Qi flows gently and continuously, like a slow river: not stagnant (which leads to lethargy and poor health) and not rushing (which causes stress and financial drain).

For practical purposes, think of Qi as a stand-in for airflow, light, sound, and human attention - the tangible things that make a room feel alive or dead. When a Feng Shui master says "Qi is blocked here," you can often translate that directly to "this corner has no natural light, poor airflow, and nobody's attention reaches it." The fix is often the same either way.

Islamic note: Feng Shui does not require belief in supernatural forces to be useful. Many Indonesian Muslim families apply it purely as an ergonomic and spatial design system. The Prophet Muhammad ﷺ said: "There is no disease that Allah has created, except that He also has created its treatment." Practical wisdom from any tradition can be used if it doesn't contradict aqeedah.

The Bagua Map

The Bagua divides any space into 9 zones, each governing an aspect of life. In the BTB (Black Sect) school most popular in Indonesia, you align it to the front door - the main entrance is always at the bottom of the map.

Bagua Map - aligned to front door at bottom

The Five Elements

Feng Shui uses five elements - Wood, Fire, Earth, Metal, Water - to describe the qualities of materials, colors, and directions. Each room benefits from having all five present, but with the element of its Bagua zone dominant.

Command Position

The command position is the most important practical rule in Feng Shui: wherever you spend focused time (sleeping, working, cooking), position yourself so you can see the door without being directly in front of it. Your back should be to a solid wall.

Why it works physically: The command position reduces subconscious vigilance - your nervous system relaxes when it doesn't need to monitor for threats behind you. This directly improves sleep quality and concentration. It also aligns with the hadith preference for sleeping with your head toward the foot of the room, not the door.

Module 04 · Islamic Home Values

The Home as Sacred Space

In Islam, the home (bayt) is not merely shelter - it is a place of worship, family formation, and the expression of moral values. Its design must serve these purposes.

What you'll learn
  • Sitr - the principle of modesty and visual privacy
  • Mahram zones and non-mahram reception areas
  • Qibla orientation for the musholla and bedrooms
  • Wudu space design integrated into the home
  • Hospitality (diyafa) as a spatial design requirement

Sitr - Privacy as Architecture

Sitr means covering or concealing - in Islamic ethics, protecting people's dignity by shielding them from unwanted exposure. In architectural terms, sitr requires that a home not expose its interior or its women to the gaze of non-mahram visitors, passers-by, or neighbours.

This is not seclusion - Islam also commands generous hospitality. The solution is layered space: a public reception zone near the entrance, then semi-private family areas, then private sleeping quarters. Each zone requires a visual break - a wall, a screen, a change of level - before the next.

Islamic Privacy Zones - Layered Floor Plan Logic

The Musholla - Heart of the Home

Every Muslim home benefits from a dedicated musholla (prayer space). This doesn't need to be a separate room - a dedicated corner with a sajadah, a qibla marker, and a wudu-accessible bathroom nearby is sufficient. But where budget and space allow, a proper musholla should be considered the most important room in the house.

🧭
Qibla Orientation
From Java, the qibla (direction of Mecca) is approximately 295°–300° (west-northwest). The prayer wall should face this direction. Mark it permanently with an embedded tile pattern or niche (mihrab).
🚿
Wudu Access
The musholla must be within easy reach of a wudu station - a low basin with foot-washing capability. Placing it adjacent to the kamar mandi (bathroom) is common. Keep the path from wudu to prayer mat clean and dry.
🤫
Sound Separation
The musholla should be acoustically separated from the TV room and kitchen - areas of household noise. Place it against a bedroom wall or at the end of a corridor rather than adjacent to the main living area.

Hospitality (Diyafa) as Spatial Logic

The Prophet ﷺ commanded generosity to guests. Architecturally, this means the public zone of the house - the teras (porch) and ruang tamu (reception room) - must be spacious, comfortable, and easy to reach without entering the private family areas. Guests should never need to pass through bedrooms or the musholla to reach seating.

Practical implication: The toilet for guests should open off the public zone, not require a walk through the family kitchen. A dedicated WC near the front of the house serves both diyafa (hospitality) and sitr (privacy) simultaneously.

Sleeping Direction (Qibla & Sunnah)

A common misunderstanding: in Islam, the head should not point toward the toilet (to avoid impurity), and the body should ideally be oriented on the right side facing qibla when possible. This does not mean the bed head must always face qibla - it means the bed placement should avoid the worst positions (head toward WC, feet toward qibla direction).

The Big Convergences

These are the design decisions where Tropical, Feng Shui, and Islamic guidance all point to the same answer. Build your home around these first - they represent the easiest, highest-value moves.

Deep roof overhangs ✅ Rain & sun protection ✅ Provides shelter; contained Qi, not exposed ✅ Provides teras for guests (diyafa)

The Top 5 Conflicts & How to Resolve Them

1. Front Door Facing West (Feng Shui favorable) but West = Max Heat Gain (Tropical bad)

Resolution: Prioritize tropical. Add a deep teras with minimum 1.5m overhang, perforated screen walls, and vegetation to block afternoon sun. Then place a water feature (FS: career water element) just inside the entrance to "cool" the westward Qi symbolically and literally.

2. Feng Shui places the "Wealth" zone (SE) where Islamic modesty requires a private area

Resolution: Islamic modesty takes priority. Activate the Wealth zone with plants, good lighting, and a beautiful piece of art or Quranic calligraphy - rather than with an open, exposed space. Qi activation doesn't require visibility from outside.

3. Water feature at entrance (FS good) conflicts with wudu cleanliness requirement

Resolution: A small internal fountain in the courtyard, or a decorative water vessel inside the teras, satisfies Feng Shui's Water/Career activation without creating a wet splash zone that makes wudu approach messy. Outdoor ponds facing the entrance are fine if elevated and clean.

4. Qibla direction conflicts with "best" tropical window orientation

Resolution: Qibla is fixed. Orient the musholla wall correctly. For the bedroom, the bed position takes guidance from both - if qibla is WNW (as in Java), and the best bedroom face is south, the bed head against the south wall means the sleeper faces north. This is acceptable in Islamic jurisprudence.

5. Feng Shui's open-plan preference vs. Islamic need for zone separation

Resolution: These are more compatible than they seem. Feng Shui screens, shoji-style panels, and partial walls allow Qi to flow without full visibility. Islamic sitr requires visual separation - a translucent or perforated screen can provide visual separation while preserving Qi flow.

The three-question test: For any design decision, ask: (1) Does it hurt the climate performance? (2) Does it violate Islamic obligation? (3) Does it block Qi unnecessarily? If all three answers are no, do it. If one is yes, adjust. If two are yes, reconsider the whole approach.

Room Guide

🏠 Teras (Porch)
Public Zone 1
Tropical: Minimum 1.5m deep overhang; face north or south ideally; cross-breeze inlet position.
Feng Shui: Keep clear of clutter; Career/Helpful People zone activation; no dead plants or broken fixtures.
Islamic: Wide and welcoming; visible from the street signals hospitality; shoe storage that keeps entry clean.
🛋 Ruang Tamu (Reception)
Public Zone 1
Tropical: High ceiling (3m+) for stack effect; operable louvres; east or south-facing for morning light without afternoon heat.
Feng Shui: Host seat in command position (back to solid wall, facing door); no beam above seating; bright, yang energy.
Islamic: Visual screen before family area; WC nearby for guests; modest decor - calligraphy, geometric patterns over figurative art.
👨‍👩‍👧 Ruang Keluarga (Family Room)
Semi-Private Zone 2
Tropical: Central to house plan; courtyard-adjacent if possible; natural cross-ventilation prioritised over air-con.
Feng Shui: Earth centre zone; square or near-square room shape ideal; plants activate Wood (family); avoid TV directly opposite sofa across a long axis.
Islamic: Physically separated from ruang tamu by at least one visual break; TV position doesn't face qibla direction.
🍳 Dapur (Kitchen)
Semi-Private Zone 2
Tropical: At rear of house; cross-ventilated with exhaust above cooker; avoid west wall placement to prevent oven + sun double-heating.
Feng Shui: Fire element zone; cooker in command position; sink and cooker should not be directly adjacent (Water vs Fire conflict) - separate with a wooden chopping block.
Islamic: Not visible from ruang tamu; clean and organised - cleanliness is half of faith (hadith); halal and non-halal storage clearly separated if needed.
🛏 Kamar Tidur Utama (Master Bedroom)
Private Zone 3
Tropical: South-facing preferred; cross-ventilated; avoid west-facing for afternoon heat; high-mass walls on east and west for temperature buffering.
Feng Shui: Bed in command position; solid headboard wall; no mirror facing bed; no ceiling beam directly over body; Love/Relationship zone ideally in SW.
Islamic: Head doesn't point toward WC; bed faces don't directly face qibla (facing qibla to sleep is acceptable and encouraged); door opens to reveal bed gradually, not immediately.
🕌 Musholla (Prayer Space)
Private Zone 3
Tropical: Quiet, thermally comfortable; east-facing if possible (morning light); high ceiling for spiritual expansiveness and thermal comfort.
Feng Shui: Knowledge zone (NE) of Bagua is ideal - governs wisdom and inner development; clean, uncluttered, yin-balanced energy.
Islamic: Qibla wall marked clearly; sajadah aligned; Quran accessible; wudu station within 10 steps; no images/photos on walls; azan audible (window or near external wall).
🚿 Kamar Mandi + Wudu
Service - All Zones
Tropical: Ventilated naturally (louvre or high window); tiled floor with proper drainage gradient; humidity managed with airflow not just air-con.
Feng Shui: Keep door closed at all times; don't place bathroom in the centre (Earth zone) - it drains wealth Qi; don't place above the kitchen.
Islamic: Wudu basin separate from toilet area if possible; enter with left foot, exit with right; toilet seat not facing or back-to qibla (rotate 90°); istinja area designed for easy right-hand-free use.
🌿 Halaman (Garden/Yard)
Transition Zone
Tropical: West and east trees for shade; avoid dense ground cover on west that retains heat; rain garden or biopori holes for absorption.
Feng Shui: Keep front garden open and welcoming; place healthy plants - no thorny plants near entrance; Water feature on north or east activates Career/Family.
Islamic: Garden is sadaqah - a gift to nature and community; fruit trees are sunnah; ensure visual privacy from street for family area; avoid statues or figurative sculpture.

The Harmonis Color Palette

Pure Feng Shui color prescriptions can conflict with Islamic aesthetics - bold red fame walls and golden wealth corners can feel gaudy in a modest Muslim home. The resolution is to use tinted naturals: warm earthy hues as base, with Feng Shui color expressed as accents in furnishings, ceramics, textiles, and art rather than wall paint.

Lighting the Harmonis Home

Islamic atmosphere in the home leans toward warm, diffuse light - not harsh overhead fluorescents. Feng Shui agrees: bright, yang spaces for active areas; soft, yin light for rest and prayer. Tropical design adds a third voice: maximize natural light from skylights and high windows before any artificial layer.

🌅
Musholla Lighting
East-facing clerestory window for morning prayer (Fajr) light. Warm-white LED 2700K for evening prayers. No harsh downlights - they create hard shadows that disturb concentration.
💡
Living Area
Layered: ambient ceiling (3000K), task lamp for reading, accent for art or calligraphy. Dimmer on all circuits. Feng Shui: bright = yang activation for career and social zones.
🌙
Bedroom
Warm 2700K maximum. Avoid overhead centre light - use bedside lamps instead. FS: a bedside lamp on both sides activates the Love zone symmetrically for couples.

What to Avoid

Avoid: Mirrors Facing Bed Feng Shui strongly warns against mirrors facing the sleeping person. Islamic practice also avoids reflective surfaces in bedrooms that expose the private body. Both agree: bedroom mirrors should face a wall or be covered at night.
Avoid: Exposed Drains Near Musholla Water draining symbolically "takes away" Qi in Feng Shui. Practically, exposed drain sounds during prayer distract. Islam requires cleanliness near worship space. Enclose all pipes near the musholla.

Briefing Your Architect

Most Indonesian architects are comfortable with tropical design - it's fundamental to their training. Far fewer have integrated Feng Shui and Islamic spatial thinking. Your brief should do three things: establish the three-framework approach, give non-negotiables, and give refinements.

Non-negotiables to state upfront: (1) Musholla on qibla axis with wudu access. (2) Three-zone privacy separation: public / semi-private / private. (3) Guest toilet accessible from ruang tamu without entering family zone. (4) No west-facing bedroom windows without shading. (5) Cross-ventilation path designed before walls are fixed.

Budget Tiers - What to Prioritise

TierBudget AllocationWhat to Lock In
Tier 1 - Non-negotiableFirst 60% of budgetStructure, orientation, roof form, musholla, privacy zones, ventilation paths, drainage elevation. These cannot be fixed cheaply after the fact.
Tier 2 - High ReturnNext 25% of budgetDeep overhangs, quality windows (operable louvres), hardwood flooring in bedrooms, wudu station, quality front door and teras finish.
Tier 3 - RefinementLast 15%Feng Shui Bagua zone accents, color palette, lighting dimmer system, water feature, landscaping for shade, calligraphy art.

12-Point Pre-Build Checklist

  • Qibla direction confirmed with digital compass and marked on site plan
  • Musholla location decided with wudu station adjacency confirmed
  • Three privacy zones drawn on floor plan with visual breaks at each transition
  • Guest toilet position accessible from Zone 1 only
  • Bedroom(s) facing south or north - not west-facing without 1.5m+ overhang
  • Cross-ventilation path drawn: inlet on SE/S, outlet on NW/N or upward
  • Roof overhang minimum 90cm on all sides; 150cm on east and west
  • Floor level minimum 45cm above surrounding ground (flood + cleanliness)
  • Kitchen at rear of house, not adjacent to ruang tamu
  • No bathroom directly above kitchen or musholla
  • Bed positions sketched for all bedrooms - command position confirmed
  • Materials list checked: thermal mass for east/west walls, breathable plaster

Common Mistakes to Avoid

❌
Musholla as Afterthought
Many families add a musholla after the house is built - it ends up tucked in a leftover corner with no qibla alignment and no wudu access. Design it first.
❌
Open-Plan That Violates Sitr
Copying Western open-plan layouts means the kitchen and living area are immediately visible from the front door. This works in a secular context; it conflicts with Islamic home hospitality ethics.
❌
Maximum Glass on West Wall
Following minimalist architectural trends with full-height west glazing creates an oven by 3pm. Shading is non-optional in the tropics.
❌
Feng Shui Symbols Without Understanding
Placing a Bagua mirror or dragon figurine "for luck" without understanding the system is neither effective Feng Shui nor aligned with Islamic aqeedah. Use Feng Shui as a spatial grammar, not superstition.
❌
Sealed, Air-Conditioned Box
Designing a hermetically sealed house then relying on AC is expensive to run, unhealthy over time (poor air quality), and loses all Qi and spiritual atmosphere. Design for natural air first.
❌
Single Bathroom for Entire House
For a family of 4–6 with prayer obligations, one bathroom creates wudu bottlenecks at Fajr and Maghrib. Budget for a second WC/wudu station adjacent to the musholla.

Phasing Construction on a Budget

If building in phases, this is the recommended sequence:

  1. Phase 1: Structure, roof, all openings, drainage, musholla, master bedroom, one bathroom with wudu. Everything that determines orientation, ventilation, and Islamic practice.
  2. Phase 2: Finish ruang tamu, ruang keluarga, dapur, guest toilet. The social and family zones.
  3. Phase 3: Additional bedrooms, landscaping, water features, refined Feng Shui zone activation. The refinement layer.
Final reminder: The Rumah Harmonis is not a formula - it is a way of thinking about your home. Every site is different, every family is different. Use this framework to ask better questions, not to follow rigid rules. The best home is the one that serves your family's ibadah, comfort, and love for each other - and these three frameworks are just tools to help you get there.
