Module 01What a token actually is

Before you can save tokens, you need to see what they are. This is the single concept that reframes everything else.

TL;DR
A token is roughly 3–4 characters of English, or about ¾ of a word. Claude reads and writes in tokens, and you pay (in dollars or in rate-limit quota) for every token that enters or leaves the model. This means long rambling prompts, huge pasted files, and 40-turn conversations all cost real money — even when you're not asking anything new.

Tokens, not words

Claude doesn't read English. It reads a sequence of small chunks called tokens. A common word like the is one token. A rare word like photobioreactor might be 3–5 tokens. Code, JSON, and non-English text (including Indonesian) are usually token-heavier per character than plain English.

Tokenization is not a 1:1 mapping to words. Technical and rare words cost more.

Input vs output tokens

Every Claude call has two sides: what you send in (input) and what Claude writes back (output). Both cost, but they're priced differently — output is usually several times more expensive than input, because generation is the expensive compute step. Practical consequence: a short prompt that causes Claude to write a 4,000-word essay can cost more than a long prompt that asks for a one-line answer.

Mental model
Think of Claude as a photocopier that charges per page going in and per page coming out, where the outgoing pages cost 5×. The cheapest call is: small input, small output. The most expensive: huge input and huge output.

Why this matters for your actual work

Take your master's research paper. If you paste a 40-page PDF into the chat and then ask five follow-up questions, every single follow-up re-sends the entire paper as input. By turn 5 you've paid for that paper 5×. We'll fix this in Module 5.

Or your clownfish colour study with 59 fish rated across 4 treatments: dumping the raw CSV into chat every time you want a stats question answered is expensive. Analysing it once from a file and keeping results in a small summary is cheap.

Module 02The context window: your real budget

Tokens cost money, yes. But the harder limit is the context window — the finite memory Claude has for any one conversation.

TL;DR
Every Claude session has a maximum context (currently 200K tokens for most Claude models, and larger for some). Once you fill it, old turns fall out, performance degrades, and every new message pushes you closer to a wall. The real cost of a bloated session isn't just money — it's that Claude gets worse the fuller it gets.

What lives in the window?

When you send a message, Claude doesn't just see that message. It sees, in this order:

Every turn re-sends everything above it. Bar 4 is the one that grows without bound.

The "fullness" tax

Two things degrade as the window fills up:

Cost per message rises linearly. On turn 1, you might send 2K tokens. On turn 30 of the same conversation, you might be sending 80K tokens every time you hit enter — because the whole history is re-sent. This is why people get rate-limit-throttled deep into a long session.

Attention gets diluted. Claude is better at using the beginning and the end of its context than the middle. A 180K-token conversation with your critical instruction buried 60K in will often see Claude "forget" it, even though technically it's still there.

Rule of thumb
If a conversation has gone past ~30 turns or ~80K tokens, you're paying 3–10× more per message than you were at the start, and the answers are getting worse. That's the moment to start a fresh session with a summary file.

Seeing your usage

In Claude.ai you don't see a token counter natively, but long-conversation warnings will appear. In Cowork and Claude Code, the CLI/UI often shows context usage. In the API, every response includes exact input_tokens and output_tokens. Get in the habit of noticing when a session feels sluggish or repetitive — that's usually context pressure, not a "bad day" for the model.

Module 03Prompting habits that save tokens

Most token waste isn't from big files — it's from prompts that invite long answers when you wanted short ones, or answers you immediately have to redo.

TL;DR
Be specific about scope, format, and length up front. Ask one thing at a time. Constrain the output. Say what you don't want. A 40-word prompt that prevents a 4,000-word wrong answer is worth more than a 10-word prompt that triggers it.

Habit 1 — State the output shape first

Burns tokens

"Tell me about material recovery in Australian demolition."

Result: 1,500-word essay you didn't need. You'll ask for a shorter version next, paying for both.

Tight

"In 5 bullet points, list the main barriers to material recovery in Australian demolition. Peer-reviewed sources only."

Result: 300 tokens, first try.

Habit 2 — Constrain length explicitly

"Keep it under 150 words." "One paragraph." "Max 5 items." Claude respects these. Without them it defaults to "thorough", which is expensive.

Habit 3 — Say what you don't want

Based on your writing prefs: "No bullet points. No em dashes. Cohesive prose. Chicago 17th author-date in-text. Don't include a conclusion section." Putting these negatives in the prompt stops Claude from writing the wrong thing, and you from paying for the rewrite.

Habit 4 — Batch related questions, but only related ones

Three separate calls
Q1: "Summarise this paper."
Q2: "What's the methodology?"
Q3: "What are the limitations?"
Each call re-sends the paper. 3× input cost.
One call
"From this paper, give me:
1. 3-sentence summary
2. Methodology in one paragraph
3. Limitations as 3 bullets"
Paper sent once.

Habit 5 — Don't be polite at the model's expense

"Can you please, if it's not too much trouble, maybe consider helping me with..." adds tokens on every turn. Claude doesn't need softeners to do its job. Be direct. (It's also just faster.)

Habit 6 — Examples beat adjectives

"Write in a professional academic tone" is vague and invites Claude to use many words to guess your taste. A one-line example of the style you want does 10× more work in 1/10 the tokens.

Bad: "Write in a good academic style for my master's paper"
Good: "Match this voice: 'Material recovery from building
deconstruction remains poorly quantified in Australia
(Minunno et al. 2021). This gap limits...' "

Habit 7 — Use structure markers

Wrapping blocks in <instructions>, <source>, <example> XML-ish tags is a Claude-native habit. It costs a handful of tokens and dramatically reduces ambiguity, which is the real source of re-work.

<task>Rewrite the paragraph below in IELTS 7.0 register.</task>

<constraints>
- No em dashes
- Retain citations exactly
- Max 120 words
</constraints>

<paragraph>
...the text...
</paragraph>

Habit 8 — Prompt for the verdict, not the reasoning (when you just want the verdict)

"Is X better than Y for Z? Just the answer plus one-line reason." will save you 800 tokens of weighing-the-options prose you were going to skim anyway.

Brutal honesty, per your prefs
You personally write fairly long prompts in Indonesian/English mixed. That's fine for clarity, but: Indonesian tokenises less efficiently than English. If token cost matters on a given session, prompting in English with Indonesian quotes where specificity matters will cost less.
Which prompt is likely cheapest and most useful?

Module 04Session hygiene: when to clear, compact, reset

The single biggest leak is a conversation that should have ended three tasks ago.

TL;DR
Start a fresh chat for a new topic. Compact or summarise long sessions before they cross ~80K tokens. In Claude Code, use /compact or /clear. In Cowork and Claude.ai, end the chat and start a new one with a tight handover summary. Treat a session like a workbench, not a filing cabinet.

The three-state rule

StateSignalAction
FreshUnder ~15 turns, one clear topicKeep going
Saturating20–40 turns, Claude starts repeating itself or losing early contextCompact: ask for a summary, save it, start new
BloatedAnswers noticeably slower; Claude forgets instructions from the topHard reset. Do not rescue.

How to "compact" manually

Before ending a long session, send this:

Summarise this entire conversation as a handover for a
fresh Claude session. Include:
- What we're trying to accomplish
- Decisions made (with rationale)
- Open questions
- Any file paths or references we rely on

Max 400 words. No preamble.

Save that output to claude output/Personal/2026-04-18-handover-X.md, open a new session, and start the new session with: "Read this handover and continue from step 3: <paste or attach>". You've just compressed a 90K-token session into ~2K tokens with no loss of decisions.

In Claude Code specifically

CommandWhat it doesWhen
/clearWipes context completelyNew unrelated task
/compactAuto-summarises the session and starts fresh with the summarySame task, context getting heavy
/resumeRe-opens a prior sessionYou closed it by accident

In Cowork

No explicit /compact, but the principle holds: end the chat, save the handover to claude output/, start a new one. Your CLAUDE.md already auto-loads, so role/preferences don't need re-explaining. That's a major token saving already.

In Claude.ai

Projects let you pin reference files so they don't have to live in conversation history. Move repeated references (your CV, your writing style sample, your research outline) into Project files, then each chat in that Project starts lean.

Anti-pattern
Using one giant chat as a journal for a whole week of work. Every morning you're paying for a week of stale context before Claude even reads today's question. Daily fresh chats + handover notes is cheaper and sharper.

Module 05Files over chat: the biggest lever

If you only apply one thing from this course, make it this one. It's where most real token waste lives and it's where most real savings are.

TL;DR
Keep data, drafts, and references in files. Let Claude read them when needed instead of re-sending them as chat context. In Cowork, write outputs to claude output/. In Claude Code, work in the repo. In Claude.ai, use Project files. Chat is for instructions; files are for content.

The two mental models

Paste-in-chat mindset

Every turn, everything you want Claude to know is re-sent. The 40-page PDF, the 500-row CSV, the 12 reference quotes — all of it, every message. You pay for the paper 20 times over a conversation.

File-based mindset

You point Claude at a path. Claude reads that file only when it needs to. The summary lives in the conversation; the raw data stays on disk. You pay for the paper once, maybe twice.

Cowork — the workflow you already have

This is exactly why your claude output/ convention exists. Apply it deliberately:

  1. Put source materials (PDFs, CSVs, notes) in the project folder.
  2. Tell Claude the path. Claude uses Read/Grep instead of ingesting them into chat context.
  3. Claude writes drafts to claude output/<project>/.
  4. Revisions happen via file Edit, not re-pasted chat.

For Edit-based revisions specifically: Edit only sends the diff, not the whole file. A 10,000-word essay with one paragraph rewrite costs a few hundred tokens, not 15K.

Claude Code — even stricter

Claude Code is file-native. Best practices:

  • Don't paste code into prompts. Point to the file. It'll read what it needs.
  • Narrow searches. Use Grep with specific patterns, not open-ended "read the whole repo".
  • CLAUDE.md at the project root. Put conventions, stack, do/don't rules there so every session is pre-briefed without you re-typing.
  • Commit progress. Claude can diff against git to see what changed instead of re-reading the world.

Claude.ai — use Projects

Projects give you a shared "reference bundle" across chats. Put into Project files:

  • Your CV (already in your memory system)
  • Your writing samples — one per register (formal academic, IELTS 6.5, email, casual)
  • Your research paper outline and literature notes
  • Style rules (Chicago 17th, no em dashes, etc.)

Every chat in that Project gets this context without you spending tokens re-explaining.

Attachments vs pasted text

Attaching a file and pasting its contents are not the same. Attachments let Claude decide what to load (often a summarised/chunked view). Pasting dumps the entire raw text into the prompt, every turn. For anything over a page: attach, don't paste.

Module 06Tools, MCPs, and agents

Tools make Claude powerful, but each connected tool adds tokens to every single turn — even when you don't use it. Managing that is an efficiency skill.

TL;DR
Every loaded tool schema costs context. Having 80 MCP tools loaded "just in case" can eat 20K+ tokens of every turn. Connect only what you need, per project. For heavy/sprawling work, spawn subagents so the main conversation doesn't eat all the raw search/exploration output.

Tool schemas live in your context

When an MCP like Gmail or Google Calendar is connected, Claude doesn't magically know how to use it. The tool's full schema — parameters, descriptions, examples — is loaded into the context of every message. Five connected MCPs can easily mean 15–25K tokens of overhead per turn before you've said anything.

Each disconnected MCP frees up tokens for actual work.

The deferred-tool pattern (advanced)

Cowork and some Claude deployments now use a "deferred tools" system: tools are listed by name only, and their schemas are only loaded on demand via a ToolSearch call. That's why you sometimes see system reminders about deferred tools. The benefit: you can have hundreds of tools "available" while only paying schema-cost for the few you actually use. If you're building your own agent, copy this pattern.

Agents: delegate, don't marinate

A subagent is Claude spawning a secondary Claude to do a bounded piece of work and report back only the result. This matters for tokens because:

  • The subagent's messy middle (50 tool calls, file reads, dead ends) stays in its context, not yours.
  • You only pay — in your main session — for the final summary it returns.
No agent

"Search for 30 circular economy papers published after 2020, check each for relevance, and summarise." → main chat balloons to 60K tokens of raw search results before you get your answer.

Agent

Spawn a research subagent with that task. It reads, filters, writes notes to a file. Main chat only receives: "Here are the 8 relevant papers with 2-sentence summaries each."

Rules of thumb for MCPs

  • Connect MCPs per project, not globally. A research session doesn't need Klaviyo. An e-commerce session doesn't need Gong.
  • If you haven't used an MCP in the last 7 days, disconnect it. Re-adding is cheap.
  • Before a heavy exploration task, consider whether a subagent should do it.
  • Tool results also live in your context. A huge tool output (e.g. 500-row spreadsheet dump) is just as expensive as a huge pasted prompt. Ask tools to return only what you need (e.g. specific columns, first 20 rows).

Applied to your setup

Looking at your available plugins (brand-voice, sales, legal, data, marketing, operations, finance, engineering, customer-support, productivity, pdf-viewer, cowork-plugin-management): that's a lot. You probably don't need all of them loaded for the same session. A 2-minute pre-session audit — "what am I doing today, what do I actually need?" — is worth 30K tokens of breathing room.

Module 07Skills, memory, and caching

Three mechanisms that make Claude remember and act consistently across sessions — without you re-explaining, and without you paying full freight each time.

TL;DR
Skills package repeatable workflows. Memory (CLAUDE.md, Claude.ai memory, Projects) avoids re-explaining who you are. Prompt caching (used automatically in Cowork/API) makes large stable prefixes dramatically cheaper on repeat calls. Understand each so you stop paying for stability that should be free.

Skills

A Skill is a small packaged recipe: a SKILL.md plus any helper files. When you invoke it ("make a docx of this"), Claude loads that skill's instructions and follows them. Benefits:

  • Replaces a 400-word prompt with a single skill name.
  • Versioned: your team can improve the skill without every user re-learning the prompt.
  • Skill files only load when invoked — they don't permanently bloat context.

For you: skills like anthropic-skills:docx, :pdf, :xlsx, :pptx save you from re-explaining "use these fonts, these margins, don't break tables" every time you make a document. Use them instead of typing the rules out.

Memory — your existing system

You already have a sophisticated memory setup: CLAUDE.md at the workspace root + a memory/ directory. This is genuinely excellent and I'll be honest — most users don't do this. Advantages it gives you:

  • Every session starts with your role, preferences, people, terms, projects already loaded.
  • No "hi I'm Ris, I'm doing a master's..." wasted tokens.
  • Claude cites Chicago 17th automatically; doesn't use em dashes; writes in your register.
Keep it lean
A 30K-token CLAUDE.md starts every session 30K deep. Periodically run the consolidate-memory skill: merge duplicates, prune stale facts. Aim for CLAUDE.md under ~5K tokens; put detail into files under memory/ that load on demand.

Prompt caching

This is the one most people don't know about. When you send the same big prefix repeatedly (system prompt, long document, tool schemas), the API can cache that prefix. Subsequent calls that start with the same prefix are charged at a much lower rate and run faster.

How it affects you:

  • In Cowork and Claude.ai, caching happens automatically — you don't control it directly, but your habits still help. Stable prefixes cache well. If you put a large reference into a Project file and then ask 10 questions across 10 chats in that Project, later chats benefit from cache.
  • If you're using the API: explicitly mark cache breakpoints for big stable chunks (system prompt, uploaded doc). Repeated calls within a short TTL get massive discounts.
  • Chaotic prompts that keep mutating the prefix never hit cache. Stable structure = real savings.

Putting it together

A well-set-up workflow looks like this, with each layer handling a different kind of repetition:

LayerHandlesCost profile
CLAUDE.md / memoryWho you are, persistent prefsLoaded every session, so keep lean
Project filesStable reference bundle for a topicCache-friendly; read by Claude on demand
SkillsRepeatable task recipesOn-demand load; near-zero idle cost
ChatToday's questionShould be small; growing means misusing the above

Module 08Model selection & extended thinking

Not every task needs the biggest, smartest, most expensive model. Picking the right one is one of the easiest cost cuts.

TL;DR
Opus is for hard reasoning and high-stakes writing. Sonnet is the workhorse for most tasks. Haiku is for fast/cheap work (triage, reformatting, classification). Extended thinking (more tokens spent on internal reasoning) costs more — use it for hard problems, not for "rewrite this email".

The current lineup

As of mid-2026, the Claude family you'll interact with includes Opus 4.6, Sonnet 4.6, and Haiku 4.5. Naming can change, so when in doubt check Anthropic's docs. What doesn't change is the shape of the trade-off:

ModelBest forRelative costYour use case
OpusHard reasoning, nuanced writing, long-horizon coding, research synthesisHighestThesis chapters; methodology design; risk analysis
SonnetGeneral work, coding, writing, analysisMidMost of your daily work
HaikuClassification, extraction, reformatting, quick lookupsLowest"Turn these notes into bullet points"; tagging CSV rows; short email drafts

Extended thinking

Newer Claude models can "think" before answering — they produce internal reasoning tokens before the final answer. This genuinely helps on hard problems (complex math, multi-step debugging, legal analysis) but it adds tokens. On easy tasks, it's pure waste.

Rule of thumb: if the question has a clear right answer a human expert could give in under 30 seconds, you don't need extended thinking. If it needs whiteboarding, you do.

Practical routing for your work

TaskPickWhy
Rewriting an emailSonnet or HaikuMechanical
Designing a research methodologyOpus, thinking onMulti-factor, needs nuance
Formatting a CV to a job adSonnetGood balance
Classifying 59 fish into colour binsHaikuRepetitive, structured

In Claude Code specifically

You can override the default model per task. For a long refactor, invoking Opus might cost more per token but finish in fewer iterations — that's often cheaper overall. For a batch of dozens of boilerplate edits, Haiku is faster and cheaper. Don't default; decide.

Pricing caveat
Exact $/million-token prices change. Check Anthropic's pricing page when it matters. The relative ordering (Opus > Sonnet > Haiku) has held across generations but absolute numbers don't.

Module 09Anti-patterns: how tokens actually get burned

A catalogue of the specific ways people hemorrhage tokens, ranked by how often they happen. Brutal version.

1. The eternal chat

Using one conversation for a whole week. Every message re-sends a week of history. Fix: daily fresh chats + handover notes.

2. Paste-the-PDF-in-chat

Dumping entire documents into the prompt instead of attaching or putting in a folder. Fix: attach, or put in Project files, or reference by file path in Cowork/Code.

3. The "one more thing" trap

You asked for a summary. You got a summary. Now you ask "and also can you..." and "actually can you also..." for 8 turns. Each one re-sends everything prior. Fix: batch upfront. "Produce: (1) summary, (2) critique, (3) 3 follow-up questions."

4. "Be thorough"

Prompts that reward length. "Give me a complete, exhaustive, detailed analysis of..." will get you a 4,000-word answer when 400 would do. Fix: constrain length.

5. Re-asking instead of refining

Claude gives a near-right answer. Instead of saying "change paragraph 3 to X", you say "try again with a different angle" and get a fresh 1,500-word output, paying full price for 80% identical content. Fix: ask for targeted edits.

6. Leaving every MCP connected

Covered in Module 6. 30K+ of schemas loaded on every turn for tools you won't use today.

7. Using Opus for email drafts

Premium model, trivial task. Fix: Sonnet or Haiku for mechanical work.

8. Extended thinking for easy asks

Thinking on, question is "what's a synonym for 'utilise'". Waste. Fix: reserve thinking for hard problems.

9. The unstructured dump

"Here's everything I've done this semester, what should I do next?" You've just sent 40K tokens of context to ask a 50-token question. Fix: summarise first (even in your own head), then ask.

10. Not using skills when you should

Re-typing "make a Word doc with 1-inch margins, Times New Roman 12, double-spaced, ..." every time, instead of invoking the docx skill that already knows this.

11. Chatty roleplay during real work

Not wrong in itself, but every "ok perfect, thank you so much, now can you..." turn adds up. If cost matters, trim ceremony.

12. Ignoring the handover

Starting a fresh chat with "continue where we left off" — but Claude doesn't remember, because chats are stateless. You end up paying for a long explanatory preamble that a 3-line handover file would have handled for free.

The meta anti-pattern
Treating Claude like a search engine + therapist + notepad + archivist all in one chat. Each role wants different context. Mixing them means every role pays for the others' context. Split by purpose.

Module 10Your personal playbook

Everything above, condensed into actions you can do this week, tailored to your actual projects.

Daily discipline (5 minutes)

  1. Start a fresh chat per topic. Not per day — per topic.
  2. Before a complex session, audit which MCPs/plugins you actually need. Disconnect the rest.
  3. Know up front: what format, what length, what you don't want. Put it in the prompt.
  4. If the chat passes 30 turns, ask for a handover summary and start fresh.
  5. End each work session by saving anything important to claude output/. You already have this habit — trust it.

Weekly maintenance (15 minutes, Fridays)

  1. Run the consolidate-memory skill on CLAUDE.md. Prune stale items.
  2. Review your claude output/. Archive finished work. Keep active projects lean.
  3. For job hunting / research, make sure memory/cv.md and lit-notes.md are current — they're your cache-friendly references.

Signs you're doing it right

  • Most of your chats are short (under 20 turns).
  • Your claude output/ tree is growing — chat history isn't.
  • You rarely re-paste the same content twice.
  • You can resume any project by opening one file and a fresh chat.
  • You feel the difference when you accidentally leave a heavy MCP loaded.

Signs you've drifted

  • You're scrolling up to find "where did I say that last week" in one giant chat.
  • Claude is responding slower than usual at the end of sessions.
  • You're re-pasting your CV, your paper outline, or your preferences into new prompts.
  • You can't remember which plugins are connected.
