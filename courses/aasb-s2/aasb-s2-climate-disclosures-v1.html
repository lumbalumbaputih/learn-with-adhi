AASB S2 Decoded
Chapter 01 / 10 · The Big Picture

Why This Standard Exists

Imagine a rule that forces big Australian companies to tell their investors, in writing, exactly how a hotter, weirder climate could mess with their money. That rule has a name: AASB S2 Climate-related Disclosures. This chapter is your map of the whole thing before we dive into the detail.

What you'll learn
  • What AASB S2 actually is, and the problem it was built to solve
  • Who has to comply, and from when (the three start dates)
  • The Objective and Scope — what's in, what's out
  • The four pillars every later chapter hangs off
  • How AASB S2 relates to its global cousin IFRS S2, and to the voluntary AASB S1

The one-sentence version

AASB S2 requires an entity to disclose information about its climate-related risks and opportunities that is useful to investors and lenders when they decide whether to put money into the entity. Objective ¶1

That's it. Everything else in the 59-page standard is just spelling out what to disclose, how, and when. The information has to be useful to the people the standard calls primary users — existing and potential investors, lenders and other creditors — the folks deciding whether to give the entity resources.

🩺 Think of it like a health check-up

A company's annual financial report is a check-up of its money. AASB S2 adds a new test to that check-up: a "climate stress test." The patient (the company) must honestly report any symptoms — like a factory in a flood zone, or revenue that dries up as the world switches to clean energy — so the people funding it can decide whether it's a healthy bet.

The problem it solves

Before standards like this, companies talked about climate in a thousand different, fluffy, un-comparable ways — if at all. Investors kept asking for consistent, complete, comparable and verifiable information. In 2023 the Australian Government confirmed it would make climate disclosures mandatory for large businesses and financial institutions, and in September 2024 Parliament passed the law (the Treasury Laws Amendment (Financial Market Infrastructure and Other Measures) Act 2024) to make it happen, by amending the Corporations Act 2001.

The Australian Accounting Standards Board (AASB) wrote AASB S2 to support that decision. The goal: better quality and comparability of climate information across companies and sectors, so investors can make more informed calls.

Who has to do it — and when

The standard itself applies to annual reporting periods beginning on or after 1 January 2025, and you're allowed to start early. But which entities are forced to comply (and exactly when) is set by the Corporations Act, which phases it in by company size across three start dates: Preface · ¶AusC1.1

PhaseFinancial years beginning on or afterRoughly who
Group 11 January 2025The largest entities
Group 21 July 2026Large entities
Group 31 July 2027Smaller (but still sizeable) entities

Heads up AASB S2 doesn't decide who's caught — the law does, based on thresholds for things like size, revenue and emissions. AASB S2 decides what you disclose once you're caught.

Scope: what's in, what's out

AASB S2 applies to: Scope ¶3

  • Climate-related risks the entity is exposed to, split into two flavours:
    • Physical risks — the climate itself doing damage (storms, floods, drought, heatwaves, rising seas).
    • Transition risks — the shift to a low-carbon economy doing damage (new carbon laws, new tech, changing customer tastes, reputation hits).
  • Climate-related opportunities available to the entity (e.g. new revenue from greener products).

The boundary line: only risks and opportunities that could reasonably be expected to affect the entity's prospects — meaning its cash flows, its access to finance, or its cost of capital, over the short, medium or long term. Anything that couldn't reasonably be expected to affect those things is out of scope. ¶2, ¶4

The magic phrase You'll see "climate-related risks and opportunities that could reasonably be expected to affect the entity's prospects" everywhere. It's the standard's way of saying: we only care about climate stuff that's financially relevant to investors. This is "financial materiality," not "save the planet for its own sake."

The S2 / S1 / IFRS family tree

This trips everyone up, so let's make it simple. There are three relatives:

NameWhat it isMandatory?
IFRS S2The global climate standard from the International Sustainability Standards Board (ISSB). AASB S2 is built on it, keeping the same paragraph numbers.Depends on country
AASB S2The Australian version — IFRS S2 with Australian tweaks (the "Aus" paragraphs). This is the one we're studying.Yes (for caught entities)
AASB S1A broader standard for all sustainability topics (not just climate).No — voluntary

Here's the clever bit. Globally, IFRS S2 (climate) was designed to be used together with IFRS S1 (general rules). But in Australia, AASB S1 is only voluntary. So the AASB couldn't rely on it. Instead, they copied the essential general rules from S1 into Appendix D of AASB S2. That makes AASB S2 a complete, standalone standard — you never have to open AASB S1 to comply. (We cover Appendix D in Chapter 8.) Preface · ¶BC20

The main Australian tweaks

When the AASB localised IFRS S2, it made a handful of deliberate changes. The big ones:

  • Added "Aus" paragraphs for the commencement date, effective date and other Australia-specific bits.
  • Dropped the industry-based disclosures. IFRS S2 asks entities to also report industry-specific metrics (from SASB guidance). The AASB removed that requirement for now (it deleted ¶12, 23, 28(b), 32, 37, B65(d), B67 and the "disclosure topic" definition). More on why in Chapter 10.
  • Consolidated-group choice. Under the Corporations Act, a parent can report for either the whole consolidated group or just the parent — so AASB S2 says your climate disclosures must cover the same reporting entity as your financial statements (unless the law allows otherwise).
  • Not-for-profit accommodations. NFP entities use the Framework for the Preparation and Presentation of Financial Statements for terms like "primary users."
  • Pinned document versions. Because of the Legislation Act 2003, AASB S2 names the exact version of outside documents it references (like the Paris Agreement and the GICS classification).
Where the words have force AASB S2 is set out in paragraphs 1–Aus37.2 plus Appendices A–D. All paragraphs have equal authority, and the ones in bold in the standard state the main principles. Terms defined in Appendix A appear in italics the first time they show up. The Basis for Conclusions and the accompanying guidance, by contrast, are not part of the standard — they're background (Chapter 10).

Chapter 02 / 10 · Pillar 1 of 4

Governance — Who's in Charge

Before anyone trusts a company's climate numbers, they want to know: who at the top is actually watching this, and do they know what they're doing? That's governance — the shortest of the four pillars, but the one that sets the tone for everything else.

What you'll learn
  • The objective of governance disclosures
  • Everything you must say about the oversight body (the board)
  • Everything you must say about management's role
  • The "avoid duplication" rule and where it bites

The objective

The point of governance disclosures is to let users understand the governance processes, controls and procedures the entity uses to monitor, manage and oversee climate-related risks and opportunities. ¶5

In plain terms: show us the chain of command for climate, and prove it's a real one. The standard splits this into two characters — the oversight body (think board or committee) and management.

🚢 Think of a ship

The oversight body is the captain and senior officers who set the course and make sure the ship is seaworthy. Management is the engine-room crew actually steering and tending the engines day to day. AASB S2 wants to see both — and proof they talk to each other.

Part A — The oversight body (the board)

First, you must identify the body or individual responsible for oversight of climate risks and opportunities (a board, a committee, or an equivalent). Then disclose information about: ¶6(a)

  1. Responsibilities in writing. How climate responsibilities are reflected in the body's terms of reference, mandates, role descriptions and policies.
  2. Skills & competencies. How the body decides whether the right skills are available — or will be developed — to oversee the climate strategy.
  3. How and how often informed. How, and how frequently, the body is told about climate risks and opportunities.
  4. How it's factored into decisions. How the body takes climate into account when overseeing strategy, major transactions and risk-management processes — including whether it weighed the trade-offs.
  5. Oversight of targets & pay. How the body oversees the setting of climate targets and monitors progress — including whether climate performance is built into remuneration policies.

Part B — Management's role

Then you describe management's role in the processes, controls and procedures used to monitor and manage climate risks, including: ¶6(b)

  • Whether the role is delegated to a specific position or committee, and how oversight is exercised over them.
  • Whether management uses controls and procedures to support this oversight — and if so, how those are integrated with other internal functions.
Notice the pattern The standard never asks "are you doing a good job?" It asks "describe your process." It's about transparency, not grading. Users form their own judgement once you've shown them the machinery.

The "don't repeat yourself" rule

When preparing governance disclosures, an entity must avoid unnecessary duplication. If a company oversees all its sustainability risks together on an integrated basis, it should give one integrated governance disclosure rather than a separate one for climate, another for water, another for human rights, and so on. ¶7

The Australian "Aus" paragraph clarifies when this matters most: particularly if the entity has voluntarily chosen to also apply AASB S1 to disclose other sustainability topics alongside climate. ¶Aus7.1

Why this exists Imagine a board that oversees climate, nature and social risks in one quarterly meeting. Forcing three near-identical governance write-ups would just bury the reader. The rule keeps reports readable — a theme you'll see again in the risk-management pillar (Chapter 5) and in the "connected information" rules (Chapter 8).

Chapter 03 / 10 · Pillar 2 of 4 (part 1)

Strategy I — Spotting Risks & Opportunities

Strategy is the biggest pillar, so we split it in two. This half is about identification: naming the climate risks and opportunities, sorting them by type and time horizon, and explaining how they ripple through your business model and your plans. The next chapter handles the money and the stress-testing.

What you'll learn
  • The five things the strategy pillar must let users understand
  • How to describe each risk/opportunity, its type, and its time horizon
  • What "short / medium / long term" really means here
  • Business model & value chain effects — and where risk concentrates
  • Strategy, decision-making and the all-important transition plan

The objective, and the five sub-topics

The strategy pillar exists so users can understand the entity's strategy for managing climate-related risks and opportunities. ¶8 To get there, the entity must disclose enough for users to understand five things: ¶9

1

The risks & opportunities

What climate risks and opportunities could reasonably affect the entity's prospects? (this chapter, ¶10–12)

2

Effects on business model & value chain

Current and anticipated. (this chapter, ¶13)

3

Effects on strategy & decision-making

Including the transition plan. (this chapter, ¶14)

4

Effects on financial position, performance & cash flows

Now and anticipated. (next chapter, ¶15–21)

5

Climate resilience

Tested with scenario analysis. (next chapter, ¶22)

Describing the risks & opportunities

For the risks and opportunities that could reasonably affect its prospects, the entity must: ¶10

  • Describe them.
  • Classify each risk as either a physical risk or a transition risk.
  • State the time horizon(s) — short, medium or long term — over which each could reasonably be expected to occur.
  • Explain how it defines "short term", "medium term" and "long term", and how those link to the planning horizons it uses for strategic decisions.

In identifying these, the entity must use all reasonable and supportable information available without undue cost or effort — including past events, current conditions and forecasts of future conditions. ¶11 (You'll meet "reasonable and supportable… without undue cost or effort" constantly — it's the standard's built-in reality check, so smaller entities aren't asked to do the impossible.)

Deleted by the AASB Paragraph 12 (which in IFRS S2 pointed to industry-based disclosure topics) was deleted in the Australian version. So there's no requirement here to consult SASB industry topics. ¶12 [Deleted]

⏱️ Why time horizons are entity-specific

For a fast-fashion retailer, "long term" might be 5 years. For a company building a 40-year gas pipeline, "short term" might be longer than the retailer's "long term"! The standard refuses to set fixed numbers — it makes each entity define its own horizons and tie them to how it actually plans. That's why it must explain its definitions.

Business model & value chain

Next, users need to understand the current and anticipated effects of climate risks and opportunities on the entity's business model and value chain. The entity discloses: ¶13

  • A description of the effects (current and anticipated) on the business model and value chain.
  • Where in the business model and value chain those risks and opportunities are concentrated — for example, particular geographic areas, facilities, or types of assets.

("Value chain" is a defined term — the full range of interactions, resources and relationships from conception to end-of-life, upstream and downstream. We unpack it in the Chapter 9 dictionary.)

Strategy, decision-making & the transition plan

Now the meaty bit. Users must understand how climate affects the entity's strategy and decision-making. The entity discloses how it has responded, and plans to respond — including how it plans to hit any climate targets it has set or is legally required to meet. Specifically: ¶14(a)

  • Changes to the business model, including resource allocation — e.g. plans to manage or decommission carbon-, energy- or water-intensive operations; shifts from supply/demand changes; spending on R&D; acquisitions or divestments.
  • Direct mitigation & adaptation — e.g. changing production processes or equipment, relocating facilities, workforce changes, new product specs.
  • Indirect mitigation & adaptation — e.g. working with customers and supply chains.
  • Any climate-related transition plan, including the key assumptions used in developing it and the dependencies it relies on.
  • How it plans to achieve any targets, including greenhouse gas emissions targets (described per ¶33–36, covered in Chapter 7).

And two more pieces: how the entity is resourcing (and plans to resource) those activities ¶14(b), and quantitative and qualitative progress on plans it disclosed in previous periods ¶14(c) — so you can't quietly drop a plan and hope nobody notices.

What's a transition plan? A defined term: an aspect of the entity's overall strategy that lays out its targets, actions or resources for its transition towards a lower-carbon economy — like cutting emissions. It's not a separate document the standard mandates; it's whatever plan you have, disclosed honestly (including the assumptions holding it up).
