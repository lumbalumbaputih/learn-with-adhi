AASB S2 Decoded
Chapter 01 / 10 · The Big Picture

Why This Standard Exists

Imagine a rule that forces big Australian companies to tell their investors, in writing, exactly how a hotter, weirder climate could mess with their money. That rule has a name: AASB S2 Climate-related Disclosures. This chapter is your map of the whole thing before we dive into the detail.

What you'll learn
  • What AASB S2 actually is, and the problem it was built to solve
  • Who has to comply, and from when (the three start dates)
  • The Objective and Scope — what's in, what's out
  • The four pillars every later chapter hangs off
  • How AASB S2 relates to its global cousin IFRS S2, and to the voluntary AASB S1

The one-sentence version

AASB S2 requires an entity to disclose information about its climate-related risks and opportunities that is useful to investors and lenders when they decide whether to put money into the entity. Objective ¶1

That's it. Everything else in the 59-page standard is just spelling out what to disclose, how, and when. The information has to be useful to the people the standard calls primary users — existing and potential investors, lenders and other creditors — the folks deciding whether to give the entity resources.

🩺 Think of it like a health check-up

A company's annual financial report is a check-up of its money. AASB S2 adds a new test to that check-up: a "climate stress test." The patient (the company) must honestly report any symptoms — like a factory in a flood zone, or revenue that dries up as the world switches to clean energy — so the people funding it can decide whether it's a healthy bet.

The problem it solves

Before standards like this, companies talked about climate in a thousand different, fluffy, un-comparable ways — if at all. Investors kept asking for consistent, complete, comparable and verifiable information. In 2023 the Australian Government confirmed it would make climate disclosures mandatory for large businesses and financial institutions, and in September 2024 Parliament passed the law (the Treasury Laws Amendment (Financial Market Infrastructure and Other Measures) Act 2024) to make it happen, by amending the Corporations Act 2001.

The Australian Accounting Standards Board (AASB) wrote AASB S2 to support that decision. The goal: better quality and comparability of climate information across companies and sectors, so investors can make more informed calls.

Who has to do it — and when

The standard itself applies to annual reporting periods beginning on or after 1 January 2025, and you're allowed to start early. But which entities are forced to comply (and exactly when) is set by the Corporations Act, which phases it in by company size across three start dates: Preface · ¶AusC1.1

PhaseFinancial years beginning on or afterRoughly who
Group 11 January 2025The largest entities
Group 21 July 2026Large entities
Group 31 July 2027Smaller (but still sizeable) entities

Heads up AASB S2 doesn't decide who's caught — the law does, based on thresholds for things like size, revenue and emissions. AASB S2 decides what you disclose once you're caught.

Scope: what's in, what's out

AASB S2 applies to: Scope ¶3

  • Climate-related risks the entity is exposed to, split into two flavours:
    • Physical risks — the climate itself doing damage (storms, floods, drought, heatwaves, rising seas).
    • Transition risks — the shift to a low-carbon economy doing damage (new carbon laws, new tech, changing customer tastes, reputation hits).
  • Climate-related opportunities available to the entity (e.g. new revenue from greener products).

The boundary line: only risks and opportunities that could reasonably be expected to affect the entity's prospects — meaning its cash flows, its access to finance, or its cost of capital, over the short, medium or long term. Anything that couldn't reasonably be expected to affect those things is out of scope. ¶2, ¶4

The magic phrase You'll see "climate-related risks and opportunities that could reasonably be expected to affect the entity's prospects" everywhere. It's the standard's way of saying: we only care about climate stuff that's financially relevant to investors. This is "financial materiality," not "save the planet for its own sake."

The four pillars (memorise these)

Every disclosure in AASB S2 lives under one of four headings, called the core content. They come straight from the globally-recognised TCFD framework, and they're the spine of this whole course:

🛡️

📊

The S2 / S1 / IFRS family tree

This trips everyone up, so let's make it simple. There are three relatives:

NameWhat it isMandatory?
IFRS S2The global climate standard from the International Sustainability Standards Board (ISSB). AASB S2 is built on it, keeping the same paragraph numbers.Depends on country
AASB S2The Australian version — IFRS S2 with Australian tweaks (the "Aus" paragraphs). This is the one we're studying.Yes (for caught entities)
AASB S1A broader standard for all sustainability topics (not just climate).No — voluntary

Here's the clever bit. Globally, IFRS S2 (climate) was designed to be used together with IFRS S1 (general rules). But in Australia, AASB S1 is only voluntary. So the AASB couldn't rely on it. Instead, they copied the essential general rules from S1 into Appendix D of AASB S2. That makes AASB S2 a complete, standalone standard — you never have to open AASB S1 to comply. (We cover Appendix D in Chapter 8.) Preface · ¶BC20

The main Australian tweaks

When the AASB localised IFRS S2, it made a handful of deliberate changes. The big ones:

  • Added "Aus" paragraphs for the commencement date, effective date and other Australia-specific bits.
  • Dropped the industry-based disclosures. IFRS S2 asks entities to also report industry-specific metrics (from SASB guidance). The AASB removed that requirement for now (it deleted ¶12, 23, 28(b), 32, 37, B65(d), B67 and the "disclosure topic" definition). More on why in Chapter 10.
  • Consolidated-group choice. Under the Corporations Act, a parent can report for either the whole consolidated group or just the parent — so AASB S2 says your climate disclosures must cover the same reporting entity as your financial statements (unless the law allows otherwise).
  • Not-for-profit accommodations. NFP entities use the Framework for the Preparation and Presentation of Financial Statements for terms like "primary users."
  • Pinned document versions. Because of the Legislation Act 2003, AASB S2 names the exact version of outside documents it references (like the Paris Agreement and the GICS classification).
Where the words have force AASB S2 is set out in paragraphs 1–Aus37.2 plus Appendices A–D. All paragraphs have equal authority, and the ones in bold in the standard state the main principles. Terms defined in Appendix A appear in italics the first time they show up. The Basis for Conclusions and the accompanying guidance, by contrast, are not part of the standard — they're background (Chapter 10).

Chapter 02 / 10 · Pillar 1 of 4

Governance — Who's in Charge

Before anyone trusts a company's climate numbers, they want to know: who at the top is actually watching this, and do they know what they're doing? That's governance — the shortest of the four pillars, but the one that sets the tone for everything else.

What you'll learn
  • The objective of governance disclosures
  • Everything you must say about the oversight body (the board)
  • Everything you must say about management's role
  • The "avoid duplication" rule and where it bites

The objective

The point of governance disclosures is to let users understand the governance processes, controls and procedures the entity uses to monitor, manage and oversee climate-related risks and opportunities. ¶5

In plain terms: show us the chain of command for climate, and prove it's a real one. The standard splits this into two characters — the oversight body (think board or committee) and management.

🚢 Think of a ship

The oversight body is the captain and senior officers who set the course and make sure the ship is seaworthy. Management is the engine-room crew actually steering and tending the engines day to day. AASB S2 wants to see both — and proof they talk to each other.

Part A — The oversight body (the board)

First, you must identify the body or individual responsible for oversight of climate risks and opportunities (a board, a committee, or an equivalent). Then disclose information about: ¶6(a)

  1. Responsibilities in writing. How climate responsibilities are reflected in the body's terms of reference, mandates, role descriptions and policies.
  2. Skills & competencies. How the body decides whether the right skills are available — or will be developed — to oversee the climate strategy.
  3. How and how often informed. How, and how frequently, the body is told about climate risks and opportunities.
  4. How it's factored into decisions. How the body takes climate into account when overseeing strategy, major transactions and risk-management processes — including whether it weighed the trade-offs.
  5. Oversight of targets & pay. How the body oversees the setting of climate targets and monitors progress — including whether climate performance is built into remuneration policies.

Part B — Management's role

Then you describe management's role in the processes, controls and procedures used to monitor and manage climate risks, including: ¶6(b)

  • Whether the role is delegated to a specific position or committee, and how oversight is exercised over them.
  • Whether management uses controls and procedures to support this oversight — and if so, how those are integrated with other internal functions.
Notice the pattern The standard never asks "are you doing a good job?" It asks "describe your process." It's about transparency, not grading. Users form their own judgement once you've shown them the machinery.

The "don't repeat yourself" rule

When preparing governance disclosures, an entity must avoid unnecessary duplication. If a company oversees all its sustainability risks together on an integrated basis, it should give one integrated governance disclosure rather than a separate one for climate, another for water, another for human rights, and so on. ¶7

The Australian "Aus" paragraph clarifies when this matters most: particularly if the entity has voluntarily chosen to also apply AASB S1 to disclose other sustainability topics alongside climate. ¶Aus7.1

Why this exists Imagine a board that oversees climate, nature and social risks in one quarterly meeting. Forcing three near-identical governance write-ups would just bury the reader. The rule keeps reports readable — a theme you'll see again in the risk-management pillar (Chapter 5) and in the "connected information" rules (Chapter 8).

Chapter 03 / 10 · Pillar 2 of 4 (part 1)

Strategy I — Spotting Risks & Opportunities

Strategy is the biggest pillar, so we split it in two. This half is about identification: naming the climate risks and opportunities, sorting them by type and time horizon, and explaining how they ripple through your business model and your plans. The next chapter handles the money and the stress-testing.

What you'll learn
  • The five things the strategy pillar must let users understand
  • How to describe each risk/opportunity, its type, and its time horizon
  • What "short / medium / long term" really means here
  • Business model & value chain effects — and where risk concentrates
  • Strategy, decision-making and the all-important transition plan

The objective, and the five sub-topics

The strategy pillar exists so users can understand the entity's strategy for managing climate-related risks and opportunities. ¶8 To get there, the entity must disclose enough for users to understand five things: ¶9

1

The risks & opportunities

What climate risks and opportunities could reasonably affect the entity's prospects? (this chapter, ¶10–12)

2

Effects on business model & value chain

Current and anticipated. (this chapter, ¶13)

3

Effects on strategy & decision-making

Including the transition plan. (this chapter, ¶14)

4

Effects on financial position, performance & cash flows

Now and anticipated. (next chapter, ¶15–21)

5

Climate resilience

Tested with scenario analysis. (next chapter, ¶22)

Describing the risks & opportunities

For the risks and opportunities that could reasonably affect its prospects, the entity must: ¶10

  • Describe them.
  • Classify each risk as either a physical risk or a transition risk.
  • State the time horizon(s) — short, medium or long term — over which each could reasonably be expected to occur.
  • Explain how it defines "short term", "medium term" and "long term", and how those link to the planning horizons it uses for strategic decisions.

In identifying these, the entity must use all reasonable and supportable information available without undue cost or effort — including past events, current conditions and forecasts of future conditions. ¶11 (You'll meet "reasonable and supportable… without undue cost or effort" constantly — it's the standard's built-in reality check, so smaller entities aren't asked to do the impossible.)

Deleted by the AASB Paragraph 12 (which in IFRS S2 pointed to industry-based disclosure topics) was deleted in the Australian version. So there's no requirement here to consult SASB industry topics. ¶12 [Deleted]

⏱️ Why time horizons are entity-specific

For a fast-fashion retailer, "long term" might be 5 years. For a company building a 40-year gas pipeline, "short term" might be longer than the retailer's "long term"! The standard refuses to set fixed numbers — it makes each entity define its own horizons and tie them to how it actually plans. That's why it must explain its definitions.

Business model & value chain

Next, users need to understand the current and anticipated effects of climate risks and opportunities on the entity's business model and value chain. The entity discloses: ¶13

  • A description of the effects (current and anticipated) on the business model and value chain.
  • Where in the business model and value chain those risks and opportunities are concentrated — for example, particular geographic areas, facilities, or types of assets.

("Value chain" is a defined term — the full range of interactions, resources and relationships from conception to end-of-life, upstream and downstream. We unpack it in the Chapter 9 dictionary.)

Strategy, decision-making & the transition plan

Now the meaty bit. Users must understand how climate affects the entity's strategy and decision-making. The entity discloses how it has responded, and plans to respond — including how it plans to hit any climate targets it has set or is legally required to meet. Specifically: ¶14(a)

  • Changes to the business model, including resource allocation — e.g. plans to manage or decommission carbon-, energy- or water-intensive operations; shifts from supply/demand changes; spending on R&D; acquisitions or divestments.
  • Direct mitigation & adaptation — e.g. changing production processes or equipment, relocating facilities, workforce changes, new product specs.
  • Indirect mitigation & adaptation — e.g. working with customers and supply chains.
  • Any climate-related transition plan, including the key assumptions used in developing it and the dependencies it relies on.
  • How it plans to achieve any targets, including greenhouse gas emissions targets (described per ¶33–36, covered in Chapter 7).

And two more pieces: how the entity is resourcing (and plans to resource) those activities ¶14(b), and quantitative and qualitative progress on plans it disclosed in previous periods ¶14(c) — so you can't quietly drop a plan and hope nobody notices.

What's a transition plan? A defined term: an aspect of the entity's overall strategy that lays out its targets, actions or resources for its transition towards a lower-carbon economy — like cutting emissions. It's not a separate document the standard mandates; it's whatever plan you have, disclosed honestly (including the assumptions holding it up).

Chapter 04 / 10 · Pillar 2 of 4 (part 2)

Strategy II — Money & Resilience

Now we follow the money. How does climate hit the financial position, performance and cash flows — today and in the future? And could the whole strategy survive a hotter, stricter world? That last question is answered with the standard's most demanding tool: scenario analysis.

What you'll learn
  • Current vs anticipated financial effects (¶15–16)
  • The relief valves — when you can skip quantifying (¶17–21)
  • Climate resilience and what scenario analysis is (¶22)
  • The "commensurate with circumstances" principle (¶B1–B18)
  • How often you must run and refresh the analysis

Financial effects: now and later

Users need to understand two things: ¶15

  • Current financial effects — how climate risks and opportunities affected the entity's financial position, performance and cash flows this reporting period.
  • Anticipated financial effects — how they're expected to affect those things over the short, medium and long term, given how climate is factored into financial planning.

Specifically, the entity discloses quantitative and qualitative information about: ¶16

  • How climate affected its position, performance and cash flows this period.
  • Which of those risks/opportunities carry a significant risk of a material adjustment to the carrying amounts of assets and liabilities within the next year.
  • How it expects its financial position to change (given investment/disposal plans and planned funding sources).
  • How it expects its financial performance and cash flows to change (e.g. more revenue from low-carbon products; costs from physical damage; adaptation/mitigation expenses).

Single number or a range? When giving quantitative information, the entity may disclose a single amount or a range. Climate is uncertain — the standard accepts that. ¶17

The relief valves: when you can skip the numbers

The standard is realistic: sometimes you genuinely can't put a credible number on a future effect. So it builds in escape hatches.

When preparing anticipated financial-effect disclosures, the entity must use all reasonable and supportable information available without undue cost or effort, and an approach commensurate with its skills, capabilities and resources. ¶18

An entity need not provide quantitative information about current or anticipated effects if: ¶19

  • the effects are not separately identifiable; or
  • the measurement uncertainty is so high that the number wouldn't be useful.

And it need not quantify anticipated effects if it simply doesn't have the skills, capabilities or resources to do so. ¶20

But — and this matters — if you use these reliefs, you can't just go silent. You must: ¶21

  1. Explain why you didn't provide quantitative information;
  2. Provide qualitative information about the financial effects, including which line items, totals and subtotals in the financial statements are likely affected; and
  3. Provide quantitative information about the combined effects with other risks/opportunities and factors — unless even that wouldn't be useful.
The spirit of the reliefs The standard would rather get honest "we can't reliably quantify this yet, but here's where it bites and why" than a fake-precise number. The reliefs prevent companies being punished for the inherent uncertainty of climate — while still forcing them to say something useful.

Climate resilience & scenario analysis

The grand finale of the strategy pillar. The entity must disclose information letting users understand the resilience of its strategy and business model to climate changes, developments and uncertainties. And to assess that resilience, it must use climate-related scenario analysis, with an approach commensurate with its circumstances. ¶22

🎮 Scenario analysis = "what-if" playthroughs

Scenario analysis isn't a prediction. It's like running your business through several different "future worlds" — one where the world transitions fast to clean energy, one where it barely transitions and the planet heats a lot — and seeing whether your strategy survives each. You're stress-testing the plan, not forecasting one outcome.

What you disclose about resilience

Two big buckets: ¶22(a)–(b)

(a) Your resilience assessment as at the reporting date, so users understand:

  • the implications for your strategy and business model, including how you'd need to respond to what the analysis surfaced;
  • the significant areas of uncertainty you considered;
  • your capacity to adjust or adapt over short/medium/long term — including the availability and flexibility of your financial resources; your ability to redeploy, repurpose, upgrade or decommission assets; and the effect of your current and planned investments in mitigation, adaptation and resilience.

(b) How and when the scenario analysis was carried out, including the inputs:

  • which scenarios you used and their sources;
  • whether you used a diverse range of scenarios;
  • whether they relate to transition risks or physical risks;
  • whether you used a scenario aligned with the latest international agreement on climate change (the Paris Agreement);
  • why the chosen scenarios are relevant to your resilience;
  • the time horizons and the scope of operations covered;
  • the key assumptions (climate policies, macroeconomic trends, national/regional variables, energy usage & mix, technology developments); and
  • the reporting period in which the analysis was carried out.

"Commensurate with circumstances" — the proportionality engine (¶B1–B18)

Appendix B spends 18 paragraphs explaining how to pick an approach that fits you. The logic:

  • Assess your circumstances: your exposure to climate risk/opportunity, and the skills, capabilities and resources you have. ¶B2–B7
  • The more exposed you are, the more sophisticated (quantitative) your analysis should be. Highly exposed + well-resourced entities are required to use a more advanced quantitative approach. ¶B4, B17
  • A less-exposed entity, or one just starting out, may use a simpler qualitative approach (scenario narratives) and build up over time through an iterative learning process. ¶B7, B15, B17
  • Use scenarios that are publicly and freely available from authoritative sources where you can — those count as available "without undue cost or effort." ¶B11–B12

How often?

Clever split: ¶B18

  • You may run the scenario analysis itself in line with your strategic planning cycle — even every 3–5 years. So the ¶22(b) "how we did it" disclosures can stay unchanged between full analyses.
  • But the resilience assessment — the ¶22(a) results — must be updated every reporting period to reflect the latest insight.
Australian note When preparing the ¶13–22 disclosures, an entity must refer to and consider the cross-industry metric categories described in ¶29 (Chapter 7). ¶Aus23.1 (IFRS S2's ¶23, which also pointed to industry-based metrics, was deleted by the AASB.)

Chapter 05 / 10 · Pillar 3 of 4

Risk Management — The Machinery

Governance is who oversees climate risk. Strategy is what it means for the plan and the money. Risk management is the machinery in between: the actual processes for finding, sizing, ranking and tracking climate risks — and whether that machine is bolted into the company's main risk system or sitting off to the side.

What you'll learn
  • The objective of risk-management disclosures (¶24)
  • The full checklist for risk and opportunity processes (¶25)
  • Why "integration" is the headline word
  • The duplication rule again (¶26 & ¶Aus26.1)

The objective

Risk-management disclosures exist so users can understand the entity's processes to identify, assess, prioritise and monitor climate-related risks and opportunities — and crucially, whether and how those processes are integrated into the entity's overall risk management. ¶24

Those four verbs — identify, assess, prioritise, monitor — are the heartbeat of this pillar. Memorise them.

What you disclose about the process

For climate risks, disclose the processes and related policies you use to identify, assess, prioritise and monitor them, including: ¶25(a)

  • the inputs and parameters used (e.g. data sources, scope of operations covered);
  • whether and how you use scenario analysis to identify risks;
  • how you assess the nature, likelihood and magnitude of effects (qualitative factors? quantitative thresholds? other criteria?);
  • whether and how you prioritise climate risks relative to other risk types;
  • how you monitor the risks;
  • whether and how the process has changed since last period.

For climate opportunities, disclose the processes used to identify, assess, prioritise and monitor them — including whether and how you use scenario analysis to spot opportunities. ¶25(b)

And the headline: the extent to which, and how, these processes are integrated into and inform the entity's overall risk management process. ¶25(c)

🏭 The smoke detector test

Is your climate-risk process a smoke detector wired into the building's central alarm system — or a standalone gadget on a shelf that nobody checks? ¶25(c) is asking exactly that. Investors want climate risk handled by the same machinery that handles every other serious business risk, not quarantined into a "sustainability" silo.

Avoid duplication (you've seen this before)

Just like governance, when preparing risk-management disclosures the entity must avoid unnecessary duplication (per Appendix D ¶B42(b)). If sustainability oversight is integrated, give integrated risk-management disclosures rather than a separate write-up per topic. ¶26

And the Australian clarification echoes Chapter 2: this matters particularly if the entity voluntarily also applies AASB S1 to disclose other sustainability topics. ¶Aus26.1

The four-pillar connection Notice how the pillars interlock: scenario analysis (a Strategy tool) feeds Risk Management's identification step (¶25); Risk Management's output feeds Governance's oversight; and the risks it surfaces drive the Metrics & Targets numbers in the next two chapters. The standard isn't four separate boxes — it's one connected loop. (Chapter 8's "connected information" rules make that explicit.)

Chapter 06 / 10 · Pillar 4 of 4 (part 1)

Metrics & Targets I — Counting Carbon

Time for the numbers everyone's heard of: Scope 1, 2 and 3 greenhouse gas emissions. This is the most technical part of the standard, so we'll take it slowly — what the scopes mean, how to measure them, and the surprisingly deep rules for the trickiest one, Scope 3.

What you'll learn
  • The objective of metrics & targets (¶27–28)
  • Scope 1, 2 and 3 — what each captures (¶29(a))
  • The GHG Protocol, CO₂-equivalent and global warming potential (¶B19–B29)
  • Location-based Scope 2 and contractual instruments (¶B30–B31)
  • The Scope 3 measurement framework — prioritising data quality (¶B32–B57)

The objective

Metrics & targets disclosures let users understand the entity's performance on climate risks and opportunities, including progress towards any targets it set or is legally required to meet. ¶27 To achieve that, the entity discloses: ¶28

  • information relevant to the cross-industry metric categories (¶29–31 — greenhouse gases plus six more, covered across this chapter and the next); and
  • its targets (¶33 onwards — Chapter 7).

Deleted IFRS S2's ¶28(b) — which required industry-based metrics — was deleted by the AASB. Australian entities don't report industry-based metrics (for now). ¶28(b) [Deleted]

The three scopes

The headline metric is absolute gross greenhouse gas emissions for the reporting period, expressed in metric tonnes of CO₂ equivalent, split into three scopes: ¶29(a)(i)

Direct

Scope 1

Emissions from sources the entity owns or controls. E.g. fuel burned in its own vehicles, furnaces, on-site boilers.

Indirect · energy

Scope 2

Indirect emissions from the purchased electricity, steam, heating or cooling the entity consumes. The emissions physically happen at the power plant.

Indirect · value chain

Scope 3

All other indirect emissions across the value chain — upstream & downstream. Suppliers, business travel, use of sold products, etc. Usually the biggest and hardest.

🍕 The pizza-shop way to remember scopes

Scope 1 = the gas flame in your oven. Scope 2 = the electricity you buy to run the lights and fridge (burned at the power station, not your shop). Scope 3 = everything else in the story — the emissions from growing the wheat and cheese (upstream), the customer driving to pick up the pizza, and the waste from the box (downstream).

How to measure: the GHG Protocol

Emissions must be measured using the Greenhouse Gas Protocol: A Corporate Accounting and Reporting Standard (2004) — unless a jurisdictional authority or the exchange you're listed on requires a different method (then you may use that one). ¶29(a)(ii), ¶B23–B25

You also disclose your measurement approach, inputs and assumptions — what approach you used, why, and any changes you made during the period. ¶29(a)(iii), ¶B26–B29 Under the GHG Protocol you use the equity share or control approach, and you disclose which, and why. Emission factors must be the ones that best represent the entity's activity. ¶B27, B29

CO₂-equivalent & global warming potential

There are seven greenhouse gases (the Kyoto Protocol list — see Chapter 9). They warm the planet by different amounts, so the standard converts them all into one common unit: CO₂ equivalent (CO₂e), using global warming potential (GWP) values. ¶B20–B22

  • If you measure directly, convert gases to CO₂e using GWP values on a 100-year time horizon from the latest IPCC assessment available at the reporting date.
  • The Australian standard pins "latest IPCC assessment" to the Sixth Assessment Report (AR6). ¶AusB22.1
  • If you use emission factors that already convert gases to CO₂e, you don't have to recalculate them. ¶B22

Two more details on Scope 1 & 2

  • Disaggregation. For Scope 1 and 2, split emissions between (1) the consolidated accounting group (parent + consolidated subsidiaries) and (2) other investees (associates, joint ventures, unconsolidated subsidiaries). ¶29(a)(iv)
  • Scope 2 is location-based. You disclose location-based Scope 2 emissions, and provide information about any contractual instruments (e.g. renewable energy contracts) that help users understand your Scope 2 — you may also disclose a market-based figure. ¶29(a)(v), ¶B30–B31

Scope 3: the hard one (¶B32–B57)

Scope 3 is everything indirect across your value chain. There are 15 categories (purchased goods, capital goods, business travel, use of sold products, investments, etc. — full list in Chapter 9). You must consider all 15 and disclose which categories are included in your number. ¶29(a)(vi), ¶B32–B33

It's mostly estimation, prioritised by quality

Scope 3 almost always involves estimation, not direct measurement. The standard gives a measurement framework: prioritise inputs and assumptions using these characteristics: ¶B40

1

Direct measurement over estimation

All else equal, prioritise directly measured data. ¶B43–B45

2

Primary over secondary data

Data from specific activities in your value chain (meter readings, supplier data) beats industry averages. ¶B46–B49

3

Representative & timely data

Prioritise data that reflects the right technology, the right jurisdiction, and the right time period. ¶B50–B52

4

Verified data

Prioritise data that's been internally or externally verified. ¶B53–B54

You disclose how you prioritised — including the extent to which Scope 3 is measured using inputs from specific value-chain activities and the extent to which it's verified. ¶B55–B56

Reassess on big changes On a significant event or change in circumstances (e.g. a merger, a major supplier change, a new emissions regulation), you must reassess which Scope 3 categories and value-chain entities to include. ¶B34–B35

The "impracticable" backstop The standard presumes Scope 3 can be estimated using secondary data and industry averages. In the rare case it's genuinely impracticable, the entity must instead disclose how it is managing its Scope 3 emissions. ("Impracticable" = can't do it after every reasonable effort.) ¶B57

One more Scope 3 wrinkle — financed emissions for banks, insurers and asset managers — is big enough that we give it its own section in the next chapter (¶B58 onwards).

The other six cross-industry metrics (¶29(b)–(g))

Beyond greenhouse gases, the entity discloses information relevant to: ¶29(b)–(g)

MetricWhat you disclose
(b) Transition riskThe amount and % of assets or business activities vulnerable to transition risks.
(c) Physical riskThe amount and % of assets or business activities vulnerable to physical risks.
(d) OpportunitiesThe amount and % of assets or business activities aligned with climate opportunities.

Financed emissions (¶B58–B63)

If an entity does asset management, commercial banking or insurance, its biggest climate exposure is often the emissions of the companies it funds — its "financed emissions" (part of Scope 3, Category 15: investments). So such entities must disclose additional, specific information. ¶29(a)(vi)(2), ¶B58–B60

🏦 Why a bank's own office emissions miss the point

A bank's Scope 1 & 2 (its offices, its servers) might be tiny. But if it lends billions to coal projects, that's where its real climate exposure lives. Financed-emissions disclosure drags that exposure into the light, so investors can see it.

For each of the three activity types, the disclosures are tailored: ¶B61–B63

  • Asset management: absolute gross financed emissions split by Scope 1/2/3; the total assets under management (AUM) included; the % of AUM covered (with explanations for any exclusions); and the methodology used (including how emissions are allocated by investment size).
  • Commercial banking & insurance: financed emissions by Scope, broken down by industry and by asset class (loans, bonds, equity, undrawn commitments, etc.); gross exposure by industry/asset class; the % of gross exposure covered; and the methodology. Industry is classified using the GICS 6-digit code (the version incorporating amendments to August 2024). ¶AusB63.1

Climate-related targets (¶33–36)

An entity discloses the quantitative and qualitative targets it has set (and any it's legally required to meet), including greenhouse gas emissions targets. For each target, disclose: ¶33

  • (a) the metric used to set it;
  • (b) the objective (mitigation, adaptation, or conformance with science-based initiatives);
  • (c) the part of the entity it applies to (whole entity? one business unit? one region?);
  • (d) the period over which it applies;
  • (e) the base period progress is measured from;
  • (f) any milestones and interim targets;
  • (g) if quantitative, whether it's an absolute or intensity target;
  • (h) how the latest international agreement on climate change (and jurisdictional commitments under it) informed the target.

Absolute vs intensity An absolute target = a total amount (or change in total), e.g. "cut total emissions to 1 million tonnes." An intensity target = a ratio to a business metric, e.g. "cut emissions per tonne of product by 30%." A company can grow and still hit an intensity target while total emissions rise — which is why the distinction must be disclosed. ¶B66

You also disclose your approach to setting and reviewing each target: whether the target (and its methodology) was validated by a third party; your review processes; the metrics used to monitor progress; and any revisions (with reasons). ¶34 Plus your actual performance against each target and an analysis of trends or changes. ¶35

Greenhouse gas emissions targets — the extra rules (¶36)

For each GHG emissions target, also disclose: ¶36

  • (a) which gases it covers;
  • (b) whether Scope 1, 2 or 3 are covered;
  • (c) whether it's a gross or net target — and if net, you must also separately disclose the associated gross target (a net figure can't hide the gross one); ¶B68–B69
  • (d) whether it was derived using a sectoral decarbonisation approach;
  • (e) your planned use of carbon credits to offset emissions toward a net target.

Carbon credits — no hand-waving (¶36(e), ¶B70–B71)

If you plan to use carbon credits to hit a net target, you must clearly show how much you're relying on them, plus: ¶36(e)

  • which third-party scheme(s) will verify or certify the credits;
  • the type of credit — nature-based vs technological, and reduction vs removal;
  • any other factors needed to judge the credibility and integrity of the credits (e.g. assumptions about permanence).
Why the carbon-credit rules are strict "Net zero" is easy to claim and hard to verify. By forcing disclosure of how much a target leans on offsets — and how solid those offsets are — the standard makes the difference between a real emissions cut and a paper one visible to investors.

Australian wrap-up ¶37 (industry-based metrics for targets) was deleted; instead ¶Aus37.1 says when identifying target metrics you refer to the cross-industry metrics in ¶29. And the whole legislative instrument formally commenced on 31 December 2024. ¶Aus37.1, ¶Aus37.2

Chapter 08 / 10 · The General Rules

The Reporting Rulebook (Appendix D)

The four pillars tell you what to disclose. Appendix D tells you how to actually prepare and present it — the conceptual foundations, what "material" means, where to put the disclosures, when, how to handle comparatives, and what to do about judgements, uncertainties and errors. It's the rules-of-the-game appendix, copied from the voluntary AASB S1 so AASB S2 can stand alone.

What you'll learn
  • Why Appendix D exists and how its numbering works
  • Fair presentation, materiality and the reporting entity
  • Connected information, location and timing of disclosures
  • Comparatives, the statement of compliance, and the two exemptions
  • Judgements, measurement uncertainty and errors
  • The qualitative characteristics of useful information

Why this appendix exists

Recall from Chapter 1: globally, IFRS S2 leans on IFRS S1 for general rules. In Australia, AASB S1 is voluntary — so the AASB lifted the necessary general requirements into Appendix D. To comply with AASB S2 you must satisfy all of Appendix D plus the rest of the standard. Only then can you make an explicit and unreserved statement of compliance. App D intro

Numbering quirk Appendix D keeps AASB S1's paragraph numbers (so the two line up for entities using both), and marks paragraphs "[Not included]" where S1 content was left out. Don't confuse Appendix D's ¶B-numbers with the main standard's ¶B-numbers — they're different series.

Conceptual foundations & fair presentation

For climate information to be useful it must be relevant and faithfully represent what it claims to (the two fundamental qualities), and ideally be comparable, verifiable, timely and understandable (the enhancing qualities). ¶10

A complete set of disclosures must present fairly all climate risks and opportunities that could reasonably affect the entity's prospects. Faithful representation means a complete, neutral and accurate depiction. Fair presentation also requires additional information when the specific requirements aren't enough for users to understand the effects. ¶11–16

Materiality — the master filter

You must disclose material information. Information is material if omitting, misstating or obscuring it could reasonably be expected to influence the decisions of primary users. ¶17–18

  • Materiality is entity-specific — the standard sets no fixed thresholds. ¶B19
  • You consider both quantitative and qualitative factors, and assess information in the context of the disclosures as a whole. ¶B21
  • For uncertain future events, weigh the possible outcomes and their likelihood — and note that low-probability, high-impact outcomes can be material, individually or combined. ¶B22–B24
  • You need not disclose immaterial information, even if a requirement lists it as a "minimum." ¶B25
  • Don't obscure material info (e.g. burying it in boilerplate, scattering it, or over-aggregating). Reassess materiality each reporting date. ¶B27–B28

Aggregation Combine information that shares characteristics; separate (disaggregate) things that don't — e.g. split water drawn from abundant sources vs water-stressed areas if lumping them would hide something material. ¶B29–B30

Reporting entity & connected information

Your climate disclosures must be for the same reporting entity as the related financial statements, unless the law permits otherwise. ¶Aus20.1, ¶AusB38.1 (This is the consolidated-group choice from Chapter 1.)

And you must present information so users can see the connections: ¶21–24, ¶B39–B44

  • between the items themselves (e.g. how different risks relate);
  • between your disclosures (governance ↔ strategy ↔ risk management ↔ metrics);
  • between your climate disclosures and your financial statements.

Data and assumptions used must be consistent — to the extent possible — with those in the related financial statements, and you use the same presentation currency. ¶23–24

Location & timing

  • Location: disclosures go in the general purpose financial reports. You may put them alongside other info (e.g. regulator-required info), as long as the climate disclosures stay clearly identifiable. You can include info by cross-reference to another report, under conditions (¶B45–B47). ¶60–63
  • Timing: report climate disclosures at the same time as, and covering the same period as, the related financial statements (normally 12 months). Disclose changes if you use a longer/shorter period, and update for new information about end-of-period conditions or significant events after the reporting period. Interim reporting rules per ¶B48. ¶64–69

Comparatives, compliance & exemptions

  • Comparatives: disclose prior-period comparatives for all amounts (and for narrative info where useful). If you get new evidence about a prior estimate, revise the comparative and explain. Special handling for forward-looking, redefined, or newly introduced metrics — unless impracticable. ¶70, ¶B49–B54
  • Statement of compliance: only claim compliance with an explicit and unreserved statement, and only if you meet every requirement. ¶72
  • Two exemptions that don't break your compliance claim: ¶73, ¶B33–B37
    • Prohibited by law: you may omit info that law/regulation forbids you to disclose (identify the type and the source of the restriction).
    • Commercially sensitive opportunities: you may omit info about a climate opportunity if it's not already public, disclosure would seriously prejudice the economic benefit, and you can't disclose it even at an aggregated level. This never applies to risks, and can't be a basis for broad non-disclosure.

Judgements, uncertainties & errors

⚖️

Judgements

Disclose the judgements (other than estimations) with the most significant effect on the disclosures — e.g. which risks to identify, which guidance to apply, what's material. ¶74–75

🌫️

Measurement uncertainty

Identify amounts subject to high uncertainty and disclose the sources of uncertainty and the assumptions made. Estimates are normal and don't undermine usefulness if well-explained. ¶77–81

🛠️

Errors

Correct material prior-period errors by restating comparatives, unless impracticable. Errors (maths mistakes, misapplied definitions, fraud) differ from changes in estimates. ¶83–86, ¶B55–B59

Appendix A — the defined terms

These aren't decoration — they're part of the standard with equal authority, and they appear in italics the first time they're used in the body. Here are the ones that carry the most weight:

climate-related physical risks
Risks from climate change that are event-driven (acute) — storms, floods, drought, heatwaves — or from longer-term shifts (chronic) — changing precipitation/temperature, sea-level rise, reduced water availability, biodiversity loss, soil-productivity changes.
climate-related transition risks
Risks from the effort to transition to a lower-carbon economy: policy, legal, technological, market and reputational risks. E.g. higher operating costs or asset impairment from new climate regulations.
climate-related opportunities
The potential positive effects arising from climate change for an entity — efforts to mitigate and adapt can create them.
climate resilience
The entity's capacity to adjust to climate-related changes, developments or uncertainties — managing risks and benefiting from opportunities. Includes both strategic and operational resilience.
climate-related transition plan
An aspect of the entity's overall strategy laying out its targets, actions or resources for transitioning toward a lower-carbon economy.
CO₂ equivalent (CO₂e)
The universal unit indicating the global warming potential of each greenhouse gas, expressed relative to one unit of carbon dioxide — a common basis for comparing gases.
global warming potential (GWP)
A factor describing the radiative-forcing impact (harm to the atmosphere) of one unit of a given greenhouse gas relative to one unit of CO₂.
greenhouse gases
The seven gases listed in the Kyoto Protocol: carbon dioxide (CO₂), methane (CH₄), nitrous oxide (N₂O), hydrofluorocarbons (HFCs), nitrogen trifluoride (NF₃), perfluorocarbons (PFCs) and sulphur hexafluoride (SF₆).
Scope 1 / Scope 2 / Scope 3 emissions
Scope 1: direct emissions from sources the entity owns or controls. Scope 2: indirect emissions from purchased/acquired electricity, steam, heating or cooling. Scope 3: other indirect emissions in the value chain (upstream & downstream).
financed emissions
The portion of an investee's or counterparty's gross emissions attributed to the loans and investments an entity makes to them — part of Scope 3 Category 15 (investments).
internal carbon price
A price the entity uses to assess the financial implications of emissions. Two common types: a shadow price (a notional cost used in decision-making) and an internal tax/fee (an actual charge on a business unit's emissions).
latest international agreement on climate change
The agreement by UN member states to combat climate change — i.e. the Paris Agreement (December 2015).
value chain
The full range of interactions, resources and relationships related to the entity's business model and external environment — from conception to delivery, consumption and end-of-life, upstream and downstream.
business model
The entity's system of transforming inputs into outputs and outcomes to fulfil its purposes and generate cash flows over short, medium and long term.
general purpose financial reports
Reports providing financial information useful to primary users for resource decisions — including the financial statements and sustainability-related financial disclosures.
primary users
Existing and potential investors, lenders and other creditors.
impracticable
Applying a requirement is impracticable when the entity cannot apply it after making every reasonable effort to do so.
carbon credit
An emissions unit issued by a carbon-crediting programme representing an emission reduction or removal; uniquely serialised, issued, tracked and cancelled in an electronic registry.

Deleted definition The term "disclosure topic" was deleted by the AASB, consistent with dropping industry-based disclosures.

Not-for-profit accommodation

For NFP entities, the terms "general purpose financial reports" and "primary users" take their meaning from the Framework for the Preparation and Presentation of Financial Statements (amended to March 2020) rather than the Appendix A definitions — because an NFP's users include parliaments, taxpayers, donors and service recipients, not just investors. ¶AusA1

Appendix C — effective date & transition

The on-ramp. An entity applies the standard for annual periods beginning on or after 1 January 2025; earlier application is permitted (disclose the fact if you do). The date of initial application is the start of the first period you apply it. ¶AusC1.1, ¶C2

The reliefs that make year one survivable:

1

No comparatives in year one

You need not provide disclosures for any period before the date of initial application — so no comparative information is required in your first year. ¶C3

2

Keep your old GHG method (one year)

If the year before you started you used a GHG measurement method other than the GHG Protocol, you may keep using it in year one. ¶C4(a)

3

Scope 3 relief in year one

In your first year you need not disclose Scope 3 emissions at all (including financed emissions). ¶C4(b)

If you use either ¶C4 relief, you may continue using it when presenting that information as comparatives in later periods. ¶C5

The logic of the reliefs Scope 3 and value-chain data are the hardest to gather. Giving everyone a one-year grace period on Scope 3 (and on comparatives) lets entities stand up the machinery without failing compliance on day one — a pragmatic "learn by doing" ramp.

Behind the Scenes — Why the AASB Chose This

The Basis for Conclusions isn't part of the standard — it's the AASB explaining why it made each call. Reading it turns you from someone who knows the rules into someone who understands the thinking behind them. We'll finish with a full-course recap.

What you'll learn
  • How AASB S2 came to exist (the law and the ISSB baseline)
  • The key design decisions and the reasoning behind each
  • The deliberate departures from IFRS S2 — and what's coming later
  • A consolidated recap of the whole standard

How we got here

The AASB issued AASB S2 to support the Government's decision to mandate climate disclosures for large businesses and financial institutions. The chain: investors demanded consistent, comparable info → in 2023 the Government confirmed mandatory disclosure aligned with the ISSB → the AASB used IFRS S1 and S2 as the baseline, modified for Australia → in September 2024 the enabling law passed. ¶BC2–BC4

After extensive consultation (Exposure Draft ED SR1, 117 comment letters, 289 survey responses, roundtables in nine cities), most stakeholders wanted minimal departure from IFRS S2. So the AASB adopted nearly all of IFRS S2, modifying only a handful of things. ¶BC12–BC15

The key decisions, decoded

📗

Voluntary S1, standalone S2

Since Australia tackled climate first, S1 (broad sustainability) was made voluntary and the needed general rules were placed in S2's Appendix D — so S2 works alone. ¶BC18–BC25

🏢

Consolidated-group choice

The Corporations Act lets a parent report for the group or just itself, so S2 ties disclosures to the same reporting entity as the financial statements. ¶BC26–BC27

🏭

Industry metrics dropped (for now)

Industry-based disclosures (SASB) were omitted as an interim measure; the AASB aims to finalise mandatory industry requirements by 2030. ¶BC28–BC30

🤝

Not-for-profit users

NFP terms refer to the Framework, recognising users like parliaments, taxpayers and donors. ¶BC31–BC35

📐

GHG Protocol over NGER

ED SR1 first proposed prioritising NGER Scheme methods; after feedback the AASB aligned with IFRS S2 and made the GHG Protocol the default (NGER methods still allowed where required by an authority). ¶BC36–BC39

🧪

Keeping NF₃

The AASB kept all seven Kyoto gases including nitrogen trifluoride (NF₃) — even though NGER doesn't list it — to stay aligned and future-proof (NF₃ is used making solar panels & semiconductors). ¶BC40–BC41

🌡️

CO₂e via latest IPCC

It used the latest IPCC assessment (AR6) for GWP values rather than AR5, again favouring IFRS S2 alignment. ¶BC42–BC44

🔌

Market-based Scope 2 not mandated

The AASB decided not to mandate market-based Scope 2 disclosure; entities may still provide it. Location-based remains required. ¶BC45–BC46

The throughline Almost every Australian decision points the same way: stay as close to IFRS S2 as possible. Why? Because Australian companies and investors operate in global markets — international comparability and interoperability (with frameworks like the European ESRS) is worth more than local tweaks. The big exception, industry-based metrics, is explicitly temporary.
