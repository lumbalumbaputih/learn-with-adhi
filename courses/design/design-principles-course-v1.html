Module 01 / 06

The Science of Seeing

You've seen thousands of well-designed things — and thousands of ugly ones. The difference usually isn't talent. It's knowledge of a handful of principles that work with the brain's hardwired visual system.

What you'll learn
  • How the brain actively constructs what we "see"
  • The 6 Gestalt laws and how to apply each one
  • Why processing fluency makes things beautiful
  • The Wundt curve: the sweet spot between boring and chaotic

The Brain as a Visual Machine

Vision isn't passive recording — it's active inference. The brain receives incomplete, ambiguous light signals and constructs a model of reality. It fills in gaps, makes predictions, and groups elements automatically, before conscious thought kicks in.

This is why design principles work: they aren't arbitrary rules. They're operating instructions for a specific piece of hardware — the human visual cortex. Learn how that hardware works and you can predict, with reasonable accuracy, how any viewer will respond to a composition.

  • ~30% of the brain's cortex is dedicated to visual processing
  • The brain processes images in ~13 milliseconds — before you're "aware" of having seen anything
  • 90% of the information we absorb is visual
  • The brain processes visuals 60,000× faster than text

Gestalt Psychology — The 6 Laws

In the 1920s, German psychologists discovered the brain doesn't perceive individual elements — it perceives relationships between elements. "Gestalt" means "form" or "unified whole" in German. Their six laws describe exactly how this grouping happens.

1. Proximity

Objects close together are perceived as a group, regardless of whether they look alike.

Demo — Proximity
Close spacing → 1 group
Wide spacing → 9 individuals
Same 9 dots. Only the spacing changed. Your brain does the rest.

Design application: Group related items together (form fields, menu items, card contents). Separate unrelated items with space. Never use arbitrary margins.

2. Similarity

Objects that share visual attributes — color, shape, size, texture — are perceived as related even when they're spatially apart.

Demo — Similarity
Two groups form — circles and squares — even though all are evenly spaced.

Design application: Consistent button styles signal "all these are interactive." Consistent font weights signal hierarchy. Color-coding groups data even when mixed together.

3. Continuation

The eye follows a path, curve, or line — even through interruptions. We prefer the path that requires the least deviation from a straight or smooth trajectory.

Demo — Continuation
⬤
The line appears to continue through the circle — the brain prefers a continuous path.

Design application: Reading flow, arrow direction, carousel indicators, progress bars. Use continuation to guide the eye through a layout deliberately.

4. Closure

The brain completes incomplete shapes. If enough information is present, we "see" the whole form even when parts are missing.

Demo — Closure
You see a circle
You see a square
Neither shape is complete — yet the brain fills in what's missing.

Design application: Logo design (negative space logos), icon design. Partial shapes save space and create visual intrigue.

5. Figure / Ground

The brain separates every visual field into a "figure" (the subject of attention) and a "ground" (the background). The same image can flip between the two.

Demo — Figure / Ground
Is it a white vase on a dark background — or two dark faces looking at each other?

Design application: Control which element reads as figure by adjusting contrast. Icons need a clear figure/ground relationship. Negative space logos deliberately play with ambiguity.

6. Common Fate

Objects moving (or appearing to move) in the same direction are perceived as a group — even when they don't share other attributes.

Design application: Animation and motion design. Elements that animate together appear related. This is why hover effects on a card group feel coherent, and why loading spinners signal "one system" even with multiple elements.

Processing Fluency

The hypothesis: We find beautiful what we can process easily.

When a design takes little mental effort to understand — clear hierarchy, familiar patterns, clean structure — the brain interprets that ease of processing as aesthetic pleasure. This isn't a conscious decision. It happens automatically.

  • First exposure effect: things become more familiar with repetition, and familiarity creates preference — which is why brand consistency works
  • Symmetry: symmetrical forms are faster to process (mirror the left half to read the right) — hence the near-universal preference for symmetrical faces
  • Clear hierarchy: when the structure of information is immediately obvious, viewers feel competent — and competence feels pleasant
But: pure fluency alone produces boring design. Too easy = too familiar = ignored. The brain needs some challenge to stay engaged. This leads us to…

The Wundt Curve — Finding the Sweet Spot

Named after Wilhelm Wundt (1874), this principle describes the relationship between complexity and preference as an inverted-U shape.

The Wundt Curve
Too simple
Sweet spot
Too complex
Preference
Preference rises with complexity — up to a point. Beyond that, overwhelm sets in and preference drops.

Design implication: Calibrate complexity to your audience. A child's app needs lower complexity than a professional tool. A luxury brand uses restraint — minimal complexity signals confidence. A game benefits from slightly higher complexity to maintain engagement.

Rule of thumb: Show only what needs to be there. When in doubt, remove an element. The design that remains beautiful after you've removed everything unnecessary is the sweet spot.

Module 02 / 06

Contrast — The Master Principle

If you had to internalize one principle above all others, it's this: contrast creates meaning. Without contrast, everything looks the same. When everything looks the same, nothing communicates.

What you'll learn
  • What contrast actually is (and isn't)
  • The 5 flavors of contrast and when to use each
  • How to build visual hierarchy using contrast alone
  • The "equal-weight trap" — the most common amateur mistake

What Is Contrast?

Contrast is simply difference between two or more elements. The greater the difference, the stronger the contrast. No contrast = no communication.

Every design decision you make is a contrast decision: bigger or smaller, darker or lighter, more space or less, heavier font or lighter. These choices determine what the viewer sees first, second, and last — or whether they bother looking at all.

The fundamental rule: If two elements are not the same, make them very different. Slight differences look like mistakes. Bold differences look intentional.

The 5 Flavors of Contrast

1. Value Contrast (Light vs. Dark)

The most fundamental contrast. Black text on white paper has maximum value contrast — that's why it's the default for reading. Value contrast controls legibility first, then emphasis.

Value contrast — same text, three levels
Hard to read — low contrast (poor)
Acceptable — medium contrast
Easy to read — high contrast (ideal for body text)
WCAG accessibility requires a minimum 4.5:1 contrast ratio for body text.

2. Size Contrast

The biggest element wins the most attention. Scale creates hierarchy — it signals to the viewer: "start reading here." Size contrast is the most direct way to express importance.

Size hierarchy
Article
The headline at 40px
Subheading at 16px, medium weight
Body text at 14px, regular weight — this is what you read once the headline has drawn you in. The size difference creates a clear reading order: title → subhead → body.

3. Weight Contrast (Bold vs. Light)

Weight lets you create emphasis within a single font family without changing size. A bold word in a regular-weight sentence stops the eye immediately.

Weight emphasis

The meeting is on Thursday at 3pm in the main conference room. Please bring your laptop.

Your eye went to "Thursday at 3pm" first. That's weight contrast at work.

4. Color Contrast

Warm vs. cool, saturated vs. muted, complementary colors placed side by side — all create contrast. A single highly saturated element on a muted background will immediately claim visual priority.

Color contrast — CTA button

Sign up for early access to the new product.

The orange button on the blue-tinted background creates strong color contrast — your eye goes there immediately.

5. Space Contrast (Dense vs. Sparse)

An element surrounded by whitespace becomes important — the space itself creates contrast. Dense, packed content reads as background; isolated elements read as figure.

Space creates emphasis
Company Name · Product A · Feature 1 · Feature 2 · About Us · Blog · Contact · Pricing · FAQ · Login · Sign Up · Terms · Privacy
Company Name
Same words. The right card feels premium and important because the space makes the name the focal point.

Visual Hierarchy

Hierarchy is the order in which the eye moves through a design. You direct that order with contrast. Every well-designed layout has at least 3 levels:

  • Level 1 (highest contrast): what they see first — main headline, hero image, primary CTA
  • Level 2 (medium contrast): what they see next — subheadings, key stats, secondary actions
  • Level 3 (lowest contrast): what they read after — body text, captions, labels
The equal-weight trap: A designer who makes everything large, bold, and colorful has designed a wall of noise. If everything is level 1, nothing is level 1. Hierarchy requires sacrifice — make some things less prominent so others can be more prominent.

Creating Focal Points

Every design needs one clear entry point — the first place the eye lands. Create it with your highest-contrast element: the biggest, darkest, most saturated, or most isolated element in the composition.

Multiple competing focal points create confusion. If your design has two elements of equal visual weight, the viewer doesn't know where to look first — and often doesn't look at all.

Test: Squint at your design until it blurs. The blurry version reveals your true focal point hierarchy — only the highest-contrast elements remain visible. Is that what you intended?

Module 03 / 06

Composition & Space

Before you pick a font or a color, you're arranging shapes in space. Composition is the skeleton of design — get it wrong and nothing else can save it.

What you'll learn
  • Why grids exist and how to use them invisibly
  • The rule of thirds and golden ratio — what they are and what they're not
  • How to use white space as an active design element
  • Balance, visual weight, and how eyes scan a layout

The Invisible Grid

A grid is a set of invisible lines that organizes elements into a coherent structure. The viewer never sees the grid — they only feel its absence when it's missing.

Column grids (typically 12 columns for web) give you a system to align elements consistently. Every element snaps to the grid's columns and gutters. This creates implicit rhythm: even without reading the content, the viewer senses structure and order.

  • Columns: vertical divisions of the space
  • Gutters: the consistent space between columns
  • Margins: the outer frame, equal on both sides
  • Baseline grid: horizontal lines for aligning text across columns
Even "rule-breaking" designs usually have a grid underneath — they just break it deliberately and knowingly. You need to know the rule to break it well.

Alignment

Every element should be visually connected to something else on the page. Random placement — even if it "looks fine" — creates subconscious unease in the viewer.

  • Left-aligned: most readable, clear natural flow for LTR languages
  • Centered: formal and balanced, but hard to maintain in long passages
  • Right-aligned: dynamic, works well for captions and labels on the right side
  • Justified: avoid on screen — creates uneven "rivers" of white space
The alignment test: If you can draw a strong vertical line through one edge of every element in a group, your alignment is working.

Rule of Thirds

Divide your composition into a 3×3 grid (9 equal rectangles). The four intersection points of those grid lines are called power points — the natural resting places for the human eye.

Rule of Thirds — power points
Placing the subject (red dot) at a power point creates dynamic tension. Dead center feels static; off-center feels alive.

The rule of thirds applies equally to photography, web layouts, slide design, and poster composition. It works because off-center placement creates tension — and tension creates visual interest.

The Golden Ratio (φ = 1.618)

The golden ratio is a mathematical proportion where the ratio of the whole to the larger part equals the ratio of the larger part to the smaller part. It appears throughout nature: nautilus shells, sunflower spirals, leaf arrangements, even proportions in the human face.

Golden ratio — 38% / 62% split
38.2%
61.8%
Dividing a space at ~62%/38% feels naturally harmonious — not arbitrary, not exactly equal.

In practice: use the golden ratio to decide column widths, size relationships between elements, and where to place primary focal points. A sidebar at ~38% of total width and main content at ~62% is a classically proportioned layout.

Honest note: The golden ratio is a useful tool, not a magic formula. Many beautiful designs don't use it explicitly. Use it as a starting point, not a constraint.

White Space — Not Empty, Active

White space (also called negative space) is not the absence of design — it's a design element. It has weight, direction, and meaning. Removing it doesn't make a design more informative; it makes it harder to read.

🫁

Breathing Room

Space prevents cognitive overload. Crowded layouts cause the brain to work harder to separate elements.

👑

Emphasis

Isolating an element gives it importance. Luxury brands use more space than budget brands — space signals value.

📦

Grouping

Space separates groups (Gestalt proximity). Consistent internal spacing unites; consistent external spacing separates.

🎵

Rhythm

Regular spacing creates visual rhythm — the same way silence between notes creates music.

Micro vs. macro whitespace: Micro is the spacing between letters, lines, and labels. Macro is the space between sections, around images, and in margins. Both matter equally.

Balance

Visual weight is how "heavy" an element feels: larger elements, darker elements, and more complex elements all carry more weight. Balance means distributing visual weight so the composition doesn't feel like it's tipping.

  • Symmetric balance: mirror image across a central axis. Feels stable, formal, calm. Classic use: logos, badges, formal layouts.
  • Asymmetric balance: different elements balance each other through contrast. Feels dynamic, modern, interesting. A large light object can balance a small dark one.
  • Radial balance: elements radiate from a center point. Circular, organic. Useful for icons, mandalas, hub-spoke diagrams.

How Eyes Scan — Z and F Patterns

Eye-tracking research shows predictable scanning patterns depending on content type:

Z

Z-Pattern

For content with visual elements and breaks: top-left → top-right → diagonal → bottom-left → bottom-right. Use for landing pages, ads, and posters.

F

F-Pattern

For text-heavy pages: read the first lines fully, then skim the left edge. Use for articles, emails, documentation.

Design implication: Put your most important information where the eye arrives first: top-left for Western viewers (LTR languages). The bottom-right of a layout is the least-viewed area — don't put critical content there.

Color Theory & Harmony

Color is the most emotional element of design. It creates mood before a single word is read, signals meaning before logic engages, and can make a trustworthy brand feel dangerous — or a dangerous product feel safe.

What you'll learn
  • The HSL model — the designer's way of thinking about color
  • Color temperature and why it creates emotional response
  • The 6 color harmonies and when to use each
  • The 60-30-10 rule for building balanced palettes
  • Color psychology and accessible color

The Color Wheel — HSL Demystified

Forget RGB (red/green/blue numbers). Designers think in HSL: Hue, Saturation, Lightness. This model maps directly to what you see and what you want to control.

🎨

Hue

The pure color — its position on the color wheel. Red, orange, yellow, green, blue, purple. Expressed in degrees: 0°=red, 120°=green, 240°=blue.

💧

Saturation

The intensity or purity of the color. 0% = gray (no color). 100% = pure vivid color. Lowering saturation makes colors more subtle and sophisticated.

☀️

Lightness

How light or dark. 0% = black. 50% = the pure hue. 100% = white. Adjusting lightness creates tints (lighter) and shades (darker).

Same hue (purple, 270°) — varying saturation and lightness
S:80 L:60
S:50 L:60
S:20 L:60
S:80 L:30
S:80 L:80
S:80 L:95
One hue generates an entire palette through HSL variation. This is how design systems create light/dark mode and component states from a single brand color.

Color Temperature

Colors have psychological temperature — even though no actual heat is involved. This is deeply ingrained from evolutionary associations: fire (warm/orange) meant warmth and food; sky and water (cool/blue) meant distance and calm.

Warm vs. Cool
Warm — Red, Orange, Yellow
Energetic · Urgent · Close · Advancing · Passionate
Cool — Blue, Green, Purple
Calm · Trustworthy · Distant · Receding · Professional
Temperature is relative — a muted orange next to a vivid red looks "cool." Always judge temperature in context.

The 6 Color Harmonies

Harmonies are color combinations derived from the color wheel's geometry. They're not rules — they're starting points that reliably produce coherent palettes.

Color harmonies — swatches
1. Monochromatic
One hue, varied in saturation and lightness. Highly unified, elegant, sophisticated. Can feel limited.
2. Analogous
Adjacent hues on the wheel (~30° apart). Natural, harmonious, like a sunset. Comfortable and approachable.
3. Complementary
Opposite hues on the wheel (180° apart). Maximum contrast. Bold and vibrant. One color must dominate — equal use creates visual vibration.
Blue dominates (3 swatches), orange is the accent (2 swatches). This ratio prevents visual clashing.
4. Split-Complementary
A color + the two hues adjacent to its complement. High contrast, less tension than pure complementary. More versatile.
5. Triadic
Three hues evenly spaced (120° apart). Vibrant and playful. Works well for children's brands and energetic UIs.
6. Tetradic (Square)
Four hues evenly spaced (90° apart). Rich and complex — hard to balance. Use one as dominant, others as accents at reduced saturation.

The 60-30-10 Rule

A practical formula for building a balanced color palette that doesn't overwhelm:

60-30-10 distribution
60% — Dominant (neutral)
30% — Secondary
10%
The 10% accent (yellow-orange) is where the brand energy lives. CTA buttons, highlights, key icons.
  • 60% dominant: backgrounds, primary surfaces — usually neutral (white, light gray, cream)
  • 30% secondary: sidebar, headers, cards — a branded color, muted
  • 10% accent: buttons, links, highlights — your most vibrant color

Color associations are partly universal (evolutionary) and partly cultural. These are Western/global defaults — always consider your audience.

Luxury, creativity, mystery. Beauty brands, premium, spiritual.

●

●

Sophistication, power, elegance, weight. Luxury, fashion, tech.

Accessible Color — WCAG Contrast Ratios

~8% of men and ~0.5% of women have some form of color vision deficiency. Accessible design doesn't just benefit them — it improves readability for everyone.

  • Normal text (under 18pt): minimum 4.5:1 contrast ratio (WCAG AA)
  • Large text (18pt+ or 14pt+ bold): minimum 3:1
  • UI components (buttons, icons, form borders): minimum 3:1
  • Never rely on color alone to convey information — add icons, labels, or patterns
Free tools: WebAIM Contrast Checker (webaim.org/resources/contrastchecker) and the Coolors Contrast Checker. Build accessibility in from the start — it's much harder to retrofit.

Module 05 / 06

Typography as Design

Type is the voice of design. The same words whispered vs. shouted communicate completely differently — typography does that for text. And since most design is mostly type, getting it right is non-negotiable.

What you'll learn
  • Anatomy of type — the terms that let you talk about it precisely
  • The 4 type categories and what each one communicates
  • How to choose and pair typefaces purposefully
  • Typographic hierarchy and the modular scale
  • Micro-typography: line-height, measure, spacing

Why Type Is 95% of Design

Most designed things — websites, apps, posters, packaging, reports — are predominantly text. Even image-heavy designs like Instagram feeds are framed by type (captions, usernames, buttons). Type isn't decoration added on top of design; type is the design in most cases.

Web designer Oliver Reichenstein said it in 2006: "Web design is 95% typography." That number hasn't changed. If your type is wrong, your design is wrong.

Anatomy of Type

Learning type anatomy gives you vocabulary to describe what you see — and to understand why some typefaces work at small sizes while others only work large.

Type anatomy — key terms
Hdpx
BASELINE
The baseline is the invisible line that letters sit on. Ascenders rise above the x-height (h, d); descenders fall below the baseline (p, y).

Baseline

The line all letters sit on. Descenders hang below it.

x-height

Height of lowercase letters (like 'x'). High x-height = better readability at small sizes.

Ascender

The part of letters that rises above x-height: b, d, f, h, k, l, t.

Descender

The part that drops below the baseline: g, j, p, q, y.

Serif

Decorative strokes at the ends of letter stems. Their presence/absence defines type category.

Leading

Vertical space between lines of text (CSS: line-height). Named after the lead strips used in metal type.

Tracking

Overall letter-spacing of a block of text. Add tracking to ALL CAPS; never to body text.

Kerning

Spacing adjustment between specific letter pairs (e.g., AV, WA). Built into good typefaces.

The 4 Type Categories

Serif

Serif

Traditional, authoritative, reliable. Best for long-form reading in print. Garamond, Times, Merriweather.

Sans

Sans-Serif

Modern, clean, digital-native. Best for screens and UI. Inter, Helvetica, Futura.

Script

Script

Personal, elegant, human. Headlines and logos only — never body text. Pacifico, Great Vibes.

Display

Display

Expressive, stylized for large sizes. Posters, headlines, logos. Not for body text.

Script fonts and body text: Script typefaces are designed for headings and decorative use at large sizes. They become illegible in small body text and should never be used there — this is the single most common mistake by non-designers.

Choosing a Typeface

Use this three-question framework:

  1. What personality does this design need? Traditional/trustworthy → serif. Modern/digital → sans-serif. Energetic/creative → display. Personal/elegant → script (heading only).
  2. How will it be used? Long body text → prioritize x-height and readability at 15–16px. Short headlines → can be more expressive and stylized.
  3. What's the context? Screen: sans-serifs generally render more cleanly at small sizes. Print: serifs guide the eye across longer lines. Both: test at the actual size and output medium.
The trap: Choosing a font because you "like it" rather than because it fits the content. The right typeface for a legal document is different from the right typeface for a birthday invitation — even if you personally prefer the birthday font.

Pairing Typefaces

The golden rule: contrast in style, harmony in feeling.

A pair works when the two typefaces are clearly different (so the viewer knows they're distinct) but feel like they belong in the same room (no personality clash). The classic pairing formula: one serif + one sans-serif.

  • Keep it to 2 typefaces. 3 is acceptable if one is used only for accent (e.g., code). More than 3 is always a mistake.
  • Ensure similar x-heights — fonts with very different x-heights feel wrong at the same size
  • Avoid pairing two serifs or two geometric sans-serifs — they compete without clear differentiation
  • Bad pair signal: both fonts feel like they're trying to be the star
  • Good pair signal: one leads, one supports
Classic pairings
Bricolage Grotesque
Paired with Inter for body text — this is the font combination used on this very site. Display heading with clean humanist body.
Georgia Heading
Paired with Inter body — transitional serif heading with modern sans body. Editorial and trustworthy.

Typographic Hierarchy

Type hierarchy is contrast applied to text. Use these tools to create clearly distinct levels:

Size

Biggest = most important. Use at least 3 clearly different sizes. Subtle size differences read as mistakes.

Weight

Bold vs. regular vs. light. Weight creates emphasis without changing size.

Color

Colored text draws the eye before black. Use sparingly — reserve for the highest-level accents.

Case

ALL CAPS for labels/overlines (with added tracking). Title Case for headings. Sentence case for body.

The modular scale: choose a base size (16px) and a ratio (1.25 = "Major Third"), then multiply repeatedly: 16 → 20 → 25 → 31 → 39 → 49px. Each step feels meaningfully different from the last. Tools like typescale.com generate these automatically.

Micro-Typography — Spacing and Rhythm

  • Line-height (leading): 1.4–1.6 for body text. 1.1–1.3 for headings. Too tight = cramped and hard to scan back. Too loose = text feels disconnected.
  • Measure (line length): 45–75 characters per line is the sweet spot. Shorter = choppy. Longer = eyes lose their place returning to the next line.
  • Letter-spacing (tracking): Add 0.05–0.15em tracking to ALL CAPS labels. Never add tracking to body text — it breaks the natural word rhythm.
  • Paragraph spacing: Use 0.75em–1em space between paragraphs (either via bottom margin or line spacing). Empty line = section break. Half-line = paragraph break.

5 Common Typography Mistakes

  1. Too many fonts — 2 is the target. Every additional font adds visual noise and requires justification.
  2. Justified text on screen — creates uneven "rivers" of white space between words. Use left-aligned for screen.
  3. Insufficient line-height — lines packed too closely reduce legibility and feel claustrophobic.
  4. Script fonts for body text — illegible at paragraph scale. Script = headings only, at large sizes.
  5. Oversized body text — 15–17px on screen is sufficient. Going larger out of insecurity just shortens the measure.

Module 06 / 06

Building Your Design Eye

Knowing principles is necessary but not sufficient. The second stage is training your eye — building the habit of seeing design not as a consumer, but as an analyst. This is a skill built through practice.

What you'll learn
  • The CRAP+CT framework — a complete critique checklist
  • A step-by-step process for analyzing any design
  • The daily exercise for building a design eye in 30 days
  • Recommended books, tools, and resources
  • Your personal design checklist

The CRAP+CT Framework

CRAP is a memorable acronym for the four core layout principles, coined by Robin Williams. I've extended it with Color and Type — the two remaining pillars — to give you a complete checklist for critiquing any design.

C

Contrast

Is there a clear visual hierarchy? Can you find the focal point in 3 seconds? Is the most important element the most prominent?

R

Repetition

Are fonts, colors, and sizes used consistently? Do repeated elements build a visual language the viewer can learn?

A

Alignment

Is every element aligned to something else? Are margins and gutters consistent? Does nothing feel arbitrarily placed?

P

Proximity

Are related items grouped together? Are unrelated items clearly separated? Does spatial grouping match semantic grouping?

C

Color

Is the palette harmonious? Does it follow 60-30-10? Do all text/background combinations meet WCAG contrast?

T

Typography

Is type hierarchy readable in 3 seconds? Are no more than 2 typefaces used? Are line-height, measure, and spacing comfortable?

How to Critique a Design

Use this 5-step process on any design — yours or someone else's:

  1. Look first, don't analyze. Give yourself 5 seconds of pure looking. What do you notice? What draws your eye? What doesn't?
  2. Find the focal point. Where does your eye land first? Is that where it should land given the design's purpose?
  3. Trace the reading path. Where does your eye go next? Can you follow a logical sequence through the content?
  4. Apply CRAP+CT systematically. Go through each letter. For each, ask: is this working? If not, what specifically is broken?
  5. Identify the one biggest fix. Don't try to fix everything. Find the single change that would provide the most improvement — usually a contrast or hierarchy issue.
The squint test: Squint your eyes until the design blurs. The blurry version reveals your true contrast hierarchy — only the highest-contrast elements remain visible. Does what remains match your intentions?

The Daily Design Exercise

One observation per day. Pick any designed thing — a product label, an app screen, a billboard, a book cover — and apply one principle from this course to analyze it. Write a single sentence. Do this for 30 days.

30-day observation prompts
Days 1–5: Hierarchy. Find 5 designs with a clear focal point and 5 without. Note what makes the difference.
Days 6–10: Color. On every website you visit, identify the harmony type being used (complementary, analogous, etc.).
Days 11–15: Typography. In every app you use, identify the typeface category and whether the pairing rule is being followed.
Days 16–20: White space. Find examples of designs that use space well (premium) and designs that pack everything in (budget). Note the emotional difference.
Days 21–25: Gestalt. Find logos or icons that use closure, figure/ground, or similarity. Analyze how the principle serves the brand.
