Why Aid So Often Fails

Hundreds of billions of dollars have flowed into poverty reduction over the past 70 years. Some of it worked. A surprising amount didn't — and understanding why is the starting point for everything that follows.

What you'll learn
  • The core logic problems with traditional aid programs
  • Why "earmarking" feels sensible but often backfires
  • How poverty shrinks cognitive bandwidth — and what that means for policy
  • The difference between helping people and treating people as capable agents

The Paternalism Problem

The default assumption behind most aid programs is that poor people need to be guided toward the right choices. Give them food, not cash — they might spend it on alcohol. Give them training, not wages — they might waste the money. Give them a plan, not capital — they might not know how to invest.

This assumption is almost never stated out loud, because it sounds awful when you say it. But it's embedded in the structure of most interventions: the earmarked voucher, the conditional cash transfer, the in-kind food basket, the prescribed curriculum. Each of these says, implicitly: we don't trust you to decide what you need.

The irony: the paternalism isn't just ethically uncomfortable — it's often economically inefficient. When you give someone a bed net instead of the cash to buy one, you've made a decision about their highest-value use of $10. You might be wrong. The person you're helping almost certainly knows more about their own situation than you do.

The Four Classic Failure Modes

🎯

Targeting errors

Complex eligibility criteria exclude many who qualify and include some who don't. Administrative costs eat into the budget. The deserving poor are often the hardest to reach.

🔒

Earmarking mismatch

In-kind goods (food, tools, bed nets) may not match actual need. A family that needs school fees doesn't benefit from surplus grain. Fungibility is a feature, not a bug.

📋

Conditionality overhead

Conditional cash transfers (CCTs) require compliance — school attendance, health check-ups. This shifts burden to the recipient and often excludes the most vulnerable.

💸

High delivery costs

Traditional programs spend 30–60 cents of every aid dollar on administration, logistics, and staff. GiveDirectly's overhead runs below 10 cents on the dollar.

Poverty as a Tax on Bandwidth

In 2013, economists Sendhil Mullainathan and psychologist Eldar Shafir published Scarcity: Why Having Too Little Means So Much. Their key finding: poverty isn't just a shortage of money. It's a shortage of mental capacity.

When your mind is consumed by an immediate problem — how to pay the rent, where the next meal comes from — your capacity for everything else shrinks. Planning horizons collapse. Willpower depletes. IQ-equivalent test performance drops by a measurable margin equivalent to losing a full night's sleep, simply from being primed to think about money stress.

What this means for policy: programs that add complexity, conditions, and paperwork to the lives of people already stretched thin by scarcity are fighting against their own purpose. The cognitive overhead of navigating a bureaucratic aid program is itself a cost that falls hardest on those least able to bear it.

The "Deserving Poor" Frame Is Backwards

A persistent instinct in aid design is to distinguish deserving from undeserving recipients — to build systems that reward the right behaviour and withhold resources from those who might misuse them. This produces systems that are expensive to operate, humiliating to navigate, and still leaky.

The evidence from cash transfer programs suggests a different frame: assume competence. Treat recipients as economic agents who understand their own constraints better than any external program designer does. The constraint isn't character — it's capital.

The Kenya Experiment

In 2014, a charity called GiveDirectly began giving unconditional cash to poor households in rural Kenya. By 2019, the experiment had grown large enough to run one of the most rigorous studies in development economics. Here's what they did and why the design matters.

What you'll learn
  • How GiveDirectly works and who gets the money
  • Why randomised controlled trials (RCTs) are the gold standard for this question
  • The specific design of the Egger et al. 2022 Econometrica study
  • Why 653 villages and 10,500 households matters for statistical power

GiveDirectly: The Basic Model

GiveDirectly was founded in 2009 by four Harvard and MIT economics PhD students — Paul Niehaus, Michael Faye, Rohit Wajarkur, and Jeremy Shapiro — who noticed something obvious in their research: virtually all aid programs assume something more complex than "give people money" will work better. But the evidence base for that assumption was thin.

Their model is simple to the point of being radical:

  1. Identify extremely poor households in sub-Saharan Africa using satellite imagery, census data, and community targeting (typically in the poorest 20% by asset ownership)
  2. Verify eligibility through mobile surveys and in-person checks
  3. Send cash directly to recipients via M-Pesa (Kenya's mobile money platform) — no intermediaries, no conditions
  4. Recipients decide how to spend it
M-Pesa as infrastructure: Kenya's mobile money system — launched by Safaricom in 2007 — is what makes this scale possible. By the time GiveDirectly launched, over 70% of Kenyan adults had M-Pesa accounts. Cash transfers that would have required physical distribution infrastructure became as simple as a text message.

Why RCTs Matter Here

Development economics has a long history of programs that seemed to work — until someone ran a proper controlled trial and found the effect disappeared or was much smaller than claimed. The randomised controlled trial (RCT), borrowed from clinical medicine, is the best tool we have for distinguishing "this happened after the program" from "this happened because of the program."

Randomised controlled trial design — the counterfactual logic
Random assignment ensures both groups are statistically identical before the transfer. Any outcome difference afterward is attributable to the cash, not pre-existing differences.

The Egger et al. 2022 Study

The landmark study — Dennis Egger, Johannes Haushofer, Edward Miguel, Paul Niehaus, and Michael Walker, published in Econometrica in 2022 — was large enough to answer a question smaller studies couldn't: what happens to the local economy, not just the individual recipient?

The study randomised at the village level — some villages received transfers, others didn't. This design was crucial: it let the researchers measure spillover effects on non-recipient households and businesses in the same local economy, something individual-level randomisation can't do.

Why $1,000? In the study villages, median annual household consumption was roughly $700. A $1,000 transfer — paid in installments — represented more than a year of typical household spending. Large enough to shift behavior, invest, and produce meaningful economic signal.

What They Measured

Beyond the obvious (what did recipients spend money on), the study tracked broader economic outcomes across both treatment and control villages: enterprise revenue, employment, asset prices, consumer prices (to detect inflation), and the incomes of non-recipient households who just happened to live near recipients.

The Multiplier: $1 In, $2.50 Out

The most striking finding from Kenya wasn't what recipients did with their money. It was what happened to the economy around them. Every dollar transferred generated roughly $2.50 in local economic activity — with near-zero inflation.

What you'll learn
  • What a fiscal multiplier is and why it's usually less than 1
  • How the local multiplier differs from the national one
  • The $2.50 finding: what it means and why it's higher than expected
  • Why near-zero inflation is the crucial accompanying result

What Is a Multiplier?

In macroeconomics, the "multiplier" describes how an initial injection of spending ripples through an economy. When a household spends $100, the recipient of that spending earns $100 — and then spends some of it in turn, generating further income for others, and so on. The total economic activity generated exceeds the original injection by the "multiplier" factor.

In rich-country macroeconomics, government fiscal multipliers are typically estimated between 0.6 and 1.5 — often below 1, meaning each dollar of government spending generates less than a dollar of additional GDP, partly because it crowds out private activity or leaks out of the local economy through imports.

How the local multiplier works: a simplified flow
The recipient buys grain from the merchant. The merchant pays the miller. The miller hires a worker. Each transaction generates income for the next actor. ~80% of the initial transfer stays in the local market.

Why 2.5x in Rural Kenya?

The Egger et al. result — ~$2.50 in local economic activity per $1 transferred — is substantially higher than typical government fiscal multipliers. Three structural factors explain this:

1. High marginal propensity to consume

In rich countries, an additional dollar of income for a wealthy household might be saved, invested abroad, or spent on imports. A poor household in rural Kenya has a near-100% marginal propensity to spend locally — they have immediate unmet needs and limited access to savings or investment products. Almost every dollar of the transfer becomes someone else's income quickly.

2. Local market constraints were already slack

The study villages had underutilised productive capacity. The miller had closed because there wasn't enough demand. The merchant stocked less because turnover was low. The trader made fewer trips because the market was thin. Injecting demand into a slack local economy can activate latent capacity without generating inflation — you're filling existing supply gaps rather than bidding up prices.

3. Near-zero leakage to imports

The goods and services poor Kenyan households buy — grain, cooking fuel, school fees, local repairs, livestock — are mostly locally produced. When spending is concentrated in a geographically defined local market, the money circulates within that market before leaking out. The study found roughly 80% of the initial transfer stayed within the local economic area through at least one additional round of spending.

The near-zero inflation finding is as important as the multiplier. A common objection to cash transfers is that injecting money into a poor, supply-constrained economy will just bid up prices, hurting non-recipients and eroding the real value of the transfer. The Kenya data showed essentially zero price inflation in treatment villages — confirming the slack-supply explanation and demolishing the most common economic objection to cash.

Non-Recipient Households Also Won

Perhaps the most surprising finding: households in treatment villages who did not receive a transfer saw their incomes rise anyway. Merchants earned more. Farm workers got hired. Local service providers had more customers. The transfer didn't just benefit the targeted poor — it raised living standards across the local economy by thickening the market.

This is the "capitalism triggered by force" effect. The quote from the screenshot you started with is economically precise: the transfer doesn't work because it's charity — it works because it injects demand-side capital into a market that was too thin to self-sustain. The mechanism is entirely standard market economics. The trigger is the transfer.

What People Actually Buy

The most common objection to cash transfers is also the most durable: "they'll waste it." It's worth taking seriously — and then looking at what actually happens when you measure it.

What you'll learn
  • What the data actually shows about spending patterns after cash transfers
  • Why the "wasted on alcohol and tobacco" assumption is empirically weak
  • How recipients make investment vs. consumption decisions
  • The dignity and agency argument alongside the economic one

The Data on Spending

Multiple studies across different cash transfer programs — Kenya, Mexico's Oportunidades, Brazil's Bolsa Família, Uganda, Ecuador, and elsewhere — have tracked how recipients spend the money. The pattern is remarkably consistent.

Typical cash transfer spending allocation (GiveDirectly Kenya recipients)
Approximate GiveDirectly Kenya recipient spending allocation. Food, productive assets, and shelter account for the vast majority. Alcohol and tobacco are statistically indistinguishable from zero in most studies. (Evans & Popova 2017; GiveDirectly internal data.)

The Alcohol & Tobacco Finding

Evans and Popova (2014, updated 2017) conducted a systematic review of 19 studies examining whether cash transfers increase spending on alcohol, tobacco, and gambling. Their finding was unambiguous: across almost all studies, in almost all contexts, cash transfers do not increase "temptation good" spending. Several studies found slight decreases, likely because recipients now had better alternatives.

Where does the assumption come from? The "they'll waste it on alcohol" fear appears to be projection from middle-class intuitions about what they themselves might do with a windfall, combined with moral frameworks that treat poverty as a character failure. It is not grounded in data. It persists because it's emotionally compelling and politically convenient for those who prefer more controlling aid designs.

Investment vs. Consumption

A key finding across multiple programs is that recipients distinguish between consumption and investment — often more shrewdly than external advisors expect. When a transfer is large enough to enable it, households frequently:

  • Purchase productive assets (livestock, small business inventory, tools) that generate future income
  • Repair or improve housing — which is both welfare and an asset
  • Pay down debt at high interest rates, effectively earning the interest rate as a risk-free return
  • Build savings buffers that reduce the scarcity-tax on cognitive bandwidth

These are textbook investment decisions. The constraint on making them before the transfer wasn't preference — it was capital. Poor people, in aggregate, are not unusually present-biased or financially unsophisticated. They are capital-constrained in ways that force suboptimal choices.

The Dignity Argument

Beyond the economic evidence, there's a case from human dignity that stands independently. Unconditional cash treats the recipient as a capable adult who understands their own situation. Every condition, restriction, and monitoring mechanism says the opposite. The psychological evidence suggests this matters: people with more control over their circumstances have better mental health outcomes, make better long-run decisions, and report higher life satisfaction — separate from the monetary value of the transfer itself.

The GiveDirectly recipients were asked what they would change about the program. The most common response: "nothing." The second most common: "give us more money." Neither group asked for conditions, guidance, or supervision.

Objections & Real Limits

The evidence for direct cash transfers is strong — but "strong" doesn't mean "without limit." There are real questions about scale, inflation, labour supply, and what cash simply cannot fix. Taking these seriously makes the case more defensible, not less.

What you'll learn
  • Why near-zero inflation at village scale might not hold nationally
  • The work disincentive question — and what the evidence actually shows
  • Conditional vs. unconditional: when conditions might legitimately add value
  • What structural poverty problems cash transfers cannot solve

Objection 1: Inflation at Scale

The Kenya study found near-zero inflation at the village level. But that result is specific to a context where: (a) supply capacity was slack, (b) the transfers were concentrated in a small share of the overall economy, and (c) most goods were imported into the village from a larger market.

Scale the program up to the national level — say, a universal basic income for all Kenyan households — and the dynamics change. If demand rises uniformly across the whole economy while supply is fixed in the short run, prices rise. The inflation zero-result is partly a function of the program being small relative to the economy it sits in.

The honest answer: we don't yet have a national-scale cash transfer large enough to test this rigorously. GiveDirectly's ongoing "basic income" pilot in Kenya covers around 20,000 people across hundreds of villages — large by research standards, but tiny relative to Kenya's 55 million population. The inflation dynamics at true national scale remain genuinely uncertain.

The Supply-Side Response

The pessimistic view assumes supply is fixed. The optimistic view: supply responds. If demand rises for grain, farmers plant more. If demand rises for construction services, more people train as builders. In the medium to long run, supply-side responses can absorb demand increases without sustained inflation. The Kenya evidence is consistent with this — no inflation over a multi-year follow-up period — but it's still only one context.

Objection 2: Work Disincentives

Standard economic theory predicts that giving people money should reduce their labour supply — the "income effect." If you have $1,000, you might work fewer hours because you need fewer dollars from labour.

The empirical evidence from cash transfer programs is mostly reassuring. Most studies find small or no reductions in labour supply. Some find increases — because the transfer provides enough capital to invest in a small business or farm that generates more income than the wage labour it replaced. The GiveDirectly Kenya data found recipients worked the same number of hours, but on better activities (their own enterprises rather than wage labour).

Labour supply effects across cash transfer programs (direction of finding)
Most studies find no significant labour supply reduction. The income effect predicted by theory is small or absent in practice — possibly because poor households are already working near-maximum hours and transfers enable quality shifts, not quantity reductions.

Objection 3: When Conditions Might Help

The case against conditions is strong for general welfare transfers. But there are situations where conditionality has a legitimate rationale:

  • Positive externalities: if a behaviour (childhood vaccination, school attendance) has benefits to society beyond the individual, a subsidy conditional on that behaviour can correct the under-provision. This is the logic behind Mexico's Oportunidades and Brazil's Bolsa Família.
  • Commitment devices: some recipients themselves prefer commitment structures — committing their own future resources to particular uses (savings programs, etc.) when present-bias might otherwise derail their intentions.
  • Political economy: unconditional transfers are much harder to defend politically than conditional ones. A condition (attend school, see a doctor) gives the program a visible story that makes it defensible against "they'll waste it" attacks. This isn't an economic argument, but it's a real constraint on what programs can be implemented.

What Cash Cannot Fix

Direct cash transfers address capital constraints. They don't address:

  • Structural discrimination: cash given to a marginalised group doesn't change the labour market or social structures that limit their opportunities
  • Missing markets: if there's no local health clinic, cash doesn't create one — it lets people travel to a clinic that may be far away
  • Public goods: roads, schools, clean water — these require coordinated investment that individual transfers can't produce
  • Political and legal security: recipients in conflict zones or without property rights can have cash-funded assets seized
  • Relative poverty: if a transfer raises everyone's income equally, it may reduce absolute poverty while leaving relative poverty unchanged
The right framing: cash transfers are not a complete theory of development. They are one highly cost-effective tool that works for a specific and important problem — capital constraints on poor households. Used alongside investments in public goods and institutional reform, they're powerful. Used as a substitute for those investments, they hit their limits quickly.

The Bigger Picture

Kenya isn't the only data point. From Finland to Stockton, California, to long-running experiments in Namibia and India, the cash transfer evidence base is growing. Here's what the broader picture shows — and what it means for how you think about poverty and development.

What you'll learn

  • The effective altruism framework and where GiveDirectly fits
  • The political economy of cash vs. conditional transfers
  • Concrete things you can do with this knowledge

UBI Experiments: A Global Map

🇫🇮

Finland (2017–2018)

2,000 unemployed people received €560/month unconditionally. Result: small positive effects on wellbeing and modest employment increases vs. control group. No significant labour supply reduction.

🇺🇸

Stockton SEED (2019–2021)

125 residents received $500/month. Recipients were more likely to move to full-time employment (from 28% to 40%), not less. Mental health improved. The "won't work" prediction failed.

🇰🇪

GiveDirectly Kenya Basic Income

Ongoing since 2017: ~20,000 people in 200+ villages receive 75 cents/day for 12 years. One of the longest-running UBI pilots anywhere. Early results consistent with the 2022 study.

🇮🇳

SEWA Pilot, India (2011–2012)

Rural Madhya Pradesh: unconditional cash improved nutrition, school attendance, livestock ownership, and business investment. Near-zero alcohol/tobacco increase. Sanitation improved.

Effective Altruism and the "Best Use of a Dollar" Question

The effective altruism (EA) movement — which seeks to identify the most cost-effective ways to improve welfare — has grappled seriously with GiveDirectly. The organisation is both a top-rated charity by GiveWell (which applies rigorous cost-effectiveness analysis) and a useful benchmark: if you're going to argue for a different charity or program, you need to argue it produces more welfare per dollar than just giving the money directly to a poor person.

This is a surprisingly high bar. Most programs don't clear it once you account for delivery overhead. The ones that do — deworming, vitamin A supplementation, certain malaria interventions — tend to address specific market failures that cash can't directly fix (e.g. you can't buy malaria prevention individually; it requires mosquito net distribution at scale).

GiveDirectly as a benchmark: "Does this program deliver more value per dollar than giving the money directly to the beneficiary?" should be the default question for evaluating any aid intervention. Most interventions have never seriously asked it. The ones that have, have mostly improved their programs as a result.

The Political Economy

The most interesting tension in this space isn't empirical — it's political. Cash transfers that work well are politically fragile. They can be portrayed as "handouts," stigmatised by both left (who prefer public services) and right (who prefer conditionality). Conditional cash transfers have proven more politically durable precisely because they have a visible accountability story: "we only pay people who do X."

Brazil's Bolsa Família — a conditional cash transfer that reached 14 million families and is widely credited with major poverty reduction — succeeded partly because its conditions made it politically defensible. The conditions (school attendance, health check-ups) had modest independent effect, but they may have been essential to the program's survival through multiple administrations. This is an uncomfortable tradeoff: some conditions reduce efficiency but increase political sustainability.

What You Can Do

Knowing this changes how you evaluate aid and giving. A few concrete implications:

  • If you donate to development charities, ask whether they have stronger evidence of impact than direct cash. GiveWell's top-rated charities clear this bar; most don't.
  • If you work in policy, the default question for any proposed intervention should be "does this beat the cash equivalent?" If it can't answer that question, it probably shouldn't be funded.
  • If you encounter the "they'll waste it" argument, you now have the systematic review to respond with: 19 studies, across multiple countries, found no evidence of increased alcohol or tobacco spending.
  • Think about what cash can't fix. Cash is one tool. Public goods, institutional reform, infrastructure — these are complementary, not alternatives. The case for cash doesn't require dismissing public investment.

Primary Research

Egger, Dennis, Johannes Haushofer, Edward Miguel, Paul Niehaus, and Michael Walker. "General Equilibrium Effects of Cash Transfers: Experimental Evidence from Kenya." Econometrica 90, no. 6 (2022): 2603–2643.
The landmark study at the core of this course. Randomised 653 Kenyan villages to receive $1,000 transfers to 10,500 households. Measured both individual outcomes and local general equilibrium effects (spillovers to non-recipients, price effects, enterprise impacts). Finds a ~$2.50 local multiplier and near-zero inflation. The village-level randomisation is the methodological advance that makes this more than a standard RCT. Caveat: single context (western Kenya), so external validity to other settings remains to be established.
→ DOI: 10.3982/ECTA17945
Evans, David K., and Anna Popova. "Cash Transfers and Temptation Goods." Economic Development and Cultural Change 65, no. 2 (2017): 189–221.
Systematic review of 19 quantitative studies examining whether cash transfers increase spending on alcohol, tobacco, and gambling ("temptation goods"). Finding: no statistically significant increases across the evidence base; several programs found modest decreases. This is the most rigorous meta-analytic answer to the "they'll waste it on alcohol" objection. Caveat: most studies rely on self-reported expenditure data, which may undercount temptation goods in contexts with social stigma around reporting.
→ DOI: 10.1086/686745
Banerjee, Abhijit, Paul Niehaus, and Tavneet Suri. "Universal Basic Income in the Developing World." Annual Review of Economics 11 (2019): 959–983.
Authoritative review of the UBI evidence base in low-income settings by three leading development economists. Covers both cash transfers and UBI-specific pilots. Synthesises the consumption, investment, labour supply, and wellbeing evidence. Useful framework for distinguishing what's known from what remains uncertain. Caveat: written before the 2022 Egger et al. general equilibrium result, so it doesn't include the local multiplier evidence.
→ DOI: 10.1146/annurev-economics-080218-030229

Cognitive & Behavioural Evidence

Mullainathan, Sendhil, and Eldar Shafir. Scarcity: Why Having Too Little Means So Much. New York: Times Books / Henry Holt, 2013.
The foundational book on "bandwidth tax" — the cognitive cost of scarcity. Presents the experimental evidence that financial worry consumes working memory and executive function, producing measurable impairments equivalent to losing a night's sleep or a meaningful drop in IQ. Not specific to cash transfers, but provides the psychological framework for why complex, conditional aid programs impose hidden costs on recipients. Caveat: some of the specific experimental results (particularly sugar cane farmer studies) have not fully replicated, though the core bandwidth concept has broader support.
→ Google Scholar

UBI / Rich-Country Pilots

Kangas, Olli, Signe Jauhiainen, Miska Simanainen, and Minna Ylikännö, eds. The Basic Income Experiment 2017–2018 in Finland: Final Report. Helsinki: Ministry of Social Affairs and Health, 2020.
Official final report of Finland's two-year UBI experiment. 2,000 unemployed citizens received €560/month unconditionally. Key findings: modest improvement in wellbeing and mental health; small positive employment effect (recipients more likely to work than control group by year 2). No significant reduction in labour supply. Caveat: participants were drawn only from the unemployed population, limiting generalisability to employed workers who might respond differently to an unconditional income.
→ Finnish Government Publications
West, Stacia, Amy Castro Baker, Sukhi Samra, and Donna Coltrera. "Preliminary Analysis: SEED's First Year." Stockton Economic Empowerment Demonstration, 2021.
Primary report from Stockton's $500/month guaranteed income pilot (SEED). 125 recipients vs. 200-person control group. Key findings: full-time employment rose from 28% to 40% among recipients; mental health and physical health improved; spending concentrated on necessities. Documents the counterintuitive result that unconditional income enabled more labour market participation, not less. Caveat: relatively small sample in a single US city with specific demographic and economic characteristics.
→ SEED Research Page

Development Economics & Policy Context

Banerjee, Abhijit V., and Esther Duflo. Poor Economics: A Radical Rethinking of the Way to Fight Global Poverty. New York: PublicAffairs, 2011.
The accessible synthesis of RCT-based development economics by the 2019 Nobel laureates. Not a cash-transfer book per se — it covers a wide range of interventions — but it's the essential background for understanding why RCTs transformed development economics and what the evidence base looked like before the cash transfer literature matured. Caveat: pre-dates most of the GiveDirectly evidence and doesn't engage with the general equilibrium multiplier question.

GiveWell's detailed cost-effectiveness analysis and charity evaluation of GiveDirectly. Includes delivery cost breakdowns, evidence review, and comparison to alternative top charities. Data quality note: The strongest evidence base (Kenya RCTs) comes from a single lower-income country context. Results may not generalise directly to different economic structures, governance environments, or cultural settings. The rich-country UBI pilots (Finland, Stockton) are encouraging but small-scale.
