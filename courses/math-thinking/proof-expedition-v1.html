Evidence Is Not Proof

In science, enough evidence makes a theory. In mathematics, a million confirming examples prove nothing. Here is the demonstration that converts everyone.

In 1772 Euler noticed that the formula n² + n + 41 seems to always spit out a prime number. Test it yourself — slide n and watch the streak grow:

Slide all the way right. It works for n = 1…39, then fails at n = 40 — because 40² + 40 + 41 = 41 × 41. Forty confirmations. Zero proof.

Field note · a complete proof Claim: odd + odd = even, always.
Proof: every odd number is 2k+1 for some integer k. Take any two: (2a+1) + (2b+1) = 2a+2b+2 = 2(a+b+1) — that's 2 × (an integer), the very definition of even. ∎

Notice the move: no examples were tested. Using letters instead of numbers covered every possible pair at once. That single trick is the engine of all mathematics.

⛺ Checkpoint
A claim has been verified by computer for the first 10 quintillion cases. Status?
This literally happened: the Pólya conjecture held for ~900 million cases, then failed. Evidence ≠ proof — that's the whole point of the mountain you're climbing.

The Truth Machine

Every proof ever written is built from four little words: and, or, not, if-then. Pull the levers and learn their exact meanings.

Lab · pull the levers
P = TRUE Q = TRUE
P = “it's raining” · Q = “I carry an umbrella”
P ∧ Q
and		P ∨ Q
or		¬P
not		P → Q
if-then		P ↔ Q
iff

The lever that trips everybody

Set P to FALSE and look at P → Q: it's true no matter what Q does. “If it rains, I carry an umbrella” promises nothing about dry days — so a dry day can never make it a lie. A conditional is false in exactly one world: promise made, promise broken.

Field note · the contrapositive twist “P → Q” and “¬Q → ¬P” are always identical claims. “If it rains, I carry an umbrella” = “If I'm umbrella-less, it isn't raining.” But the converse “Q → P” is a different claim entirely — confusing them is arguably humanity's most common reasoning error.
⛺ Checkpoint
“If a number is divisible by 4, it is even.” Which is guaranteed equally true?
Option 2 is the contrapositive — always equivalent. Option 1 is the converse, and false: 6 is even but not divisible by 4.

Sets: the LEGO Bricks of Mathematics

Numbers, functions, even infinity itself — all of modern maths is built from one primitive idea: a bag of things. Four operations combine the bags.

Below, A = multiples of 2 and B = multiples of 3 (from 1–20). Click an operation and watch which numbers light up:

Lab · set operations, live
Notice A ∩ B = the multiples of 6. Intersecting properties is literally how mathematicians define new concepts.

Why such a humble idea runs everything

“Even numbers” is a set. A function is a set of input→output pairs. The number 3 itself can be defined as a set. And Camp 2's logic maps perfectly onto sets: ∪ is or, ∩ is and, complement is not — two views of one machine.

Field note · the paradox that broke mathematics Consider “the set of all sets that don't contain themselves.” Does it contain itself? If yes → it shouldn't be in there. If no → it qualifies, so it should. Both answers explode. Bertrand Russell's 1901 puzzle forced mathematicians to rebuild the foundations of the entire subject.
⛺ Checkpoint
A = {1,2,3,4}, B = {3,4,5}. What is A ∩ B?
Intersection keeps only what's shared: 3 and 4. ({1,2,3,4,5} is the union; {1,2} is A − B.)

Camp 4 · the great trickbook ch. 2 & 5 — induction

The Infinite Domino Run

How can a finite human prove a statement about ALL infinitely many numbers? Two ingredients: the first domino falls, and every domino knocks over the next.

Lab · the domino principle
Sabotage = one broken inductive step. Everything after it stays standing — the proof is dead. That's why the “P(k) → P(k+1)” step must hold for EVERY k.

An induction you can SEE

Claim: 1 + 2 + … + n = n(n+1)/2. Stack the staircase, add its upside-down ghost twin, and a perfect n × (n+1) rectangle appears — so the staircase is exactly half of it:

Lab · the staircase identity
n = 5 
Legend says Gauss spotted this at age 8 when a teacher tried to keep the class busy adding 1 to 100. (Answer: 5050, in seconds.)
⛺ Checkpoint
A proof shows P(1) is true, and P(k) → P(k+1) — but only for k ≥ 5. What's actually proven?
Domino 1 falls, but the knocking only works from domino 5 on — and 2, 3, 4, 5 were never toppled. Broken chain, exactly where it matters.

Camp 5 · the toolboxbook ch. 4–6 — proof strategies

Four Ways Up Any Cliff

Mathematicians don't stare at a claim and hope. They reach for one of four standard attacks — and picking the right one is a learnable instinct.

① Direct: assume the “if”, march to the “then” (Camp 1's odd+odd proof).
② Contrapositive: prove ¬Q → ¬P instead — same claim, often a far gentler slope.
③ Contradiction: assume the claim is FALSE, derive an absurdity, conclude it's true.
④ Induction: the dominoes of Camp 4, for “all n” claims.

Field note · the most famous contradiction ever (Euclid, ~300 BC) Are the primes infinite? Suppose NOT — say the complete list is p₁ … pₙ. Let N = p₁p₂⋯pₙ + 1. Divide N by any prime on the list: remainder 1, every time. So N's prime factors are missing from the “complete” list. Contradiction — the primes never end. ∎

Strategy range — train your instincts

🎯 Pick your tool · 1 of 3
Claim: “If n² is even, then n is even.”
From n² = 2k it's awkward to reach n. The contrapositive is one line: n = 2m+1 → n² = 4m²+4m+1, odd. Done.
🎯 Pick your tool · 2 of 3
Claim: “√2 cannot be written as a fraction a/b.”
“IMPOSSIBLE” claims almost always want contradiction: a² = 2b² → a even → b even → not lowest terms. Absurd. ∎ This proof reportedly scandalized the Pythagoreans.
🎯 Pick your tool · 3 of 3
Claim: “Every postage amount ≥ 8¢ can be made from 3¢ and 5¢ stamps.”
“Every n ≥ 8” is the induction signature. The step: a pile with a 5¢? Swap it for two 3¢ (+1). No 5¢? Then it has three 3¢ — swap for two 5¢ (+1). Either way the dominoes keep falling.

Arithmetic on a Clock

On a 12-hour clock, 9 + 5 = 2 — and that's not a mistake, it's a whole new number system. It runs RSA encryption, ISBN check digits, and your credit card's checksum.

Gold = start at a; the purple trail walks b steps round the clock. Where you land = (a+b) mod n. Try shrinking the clock to 7 — that's the days of the week.

The machinery underneath: equivalence relations

Declaring 14 ≡ 2 (mod 12) means relating numbers that differ by a multiple of 12. A relation earns the title “equivalence” by passing three checks — reflexive (a~a), symmetric (a~b ⇒ b~a), transitive (a~b, b~c ⇒ a~c) — and the prize is huge: it slices the world into clean, non-overlapping classes, like the 12 hour-positions. “Same birthday”, “same remainder”, “same blood type”: each one instantly partitions everything.

Field note · imposters “Is a friend of” is symmetric but famously not transitive. “Is at most as tall as” is reflexive and transitive but not symmetric. Genuine equivalence relations are rarer than they look — that's why mathematicians run the three checks every time.
⛺ Checkpoint
Today is Wednesday. What day is it 100 days from now? (Hint: the week is a mod-7 clock.)
100 = 14×7 + 2, so 100 ≡ 2 (mod 7). Two days past Wednesday → Friday. You just used modular arithmetic in the wild.

Camp 7 · the night climbbook ch. 7 — functions & cardinality

Some Infinities Are Bigger Than Others

The wildest result on this whole mountain — and the proof needs nothing but the idea of pairing things up.

How do you compare sizes without counting? Pair things up perfectly — a bijection: everyone matched, no one doubled, no one left over. A shepherd with no number words still knows if sheep went missing: one pebble per sheep.

Hilbert's fully-booked hotel

A hotel with infinitely many rooms, every one occupied. A new guest arrives. Watch:

Lab · Hilbert's Hotel
“Full” doesn't mean “no vacancies” when you're infinite. Both announcements are bijections — and they prove ℕ is the same size as ℕ+1, and even the same size as HALF of itself.

Cantor pushed further: even the fractions ℚ — seemingly vastly more numerous — pair up perfectly with ℕ. All these sets share one size, called ℵ₀ (“aleph-nought”). So is every infinity the same? No.

Field note · Cantor's diagonal (1891) — contradiction at altitude Suppose someone hands you a complete numbered list of every decimal between 0 and 1. Build a saboteur number: make its 1st digit differ from entry #1's 1st digit, its 2nd differ from entry #2's 2nd… straight down the diagonal. The saboteur differs from every entry somewhere — it's not on the “complete” list. Contradiction (Camp 5!). No list can hold the reals. ℝ is a strictly larger infinity. ∎
⛺ Checkpoint
Which set is genuinely BIGGER than the counting numbers ℕ?
Evens (n ↔ 2n) and fractions both pair with ℕ — size ℵ₀. The diagonal argument shows the reals can't be listed. Infinity comes in sizes; mathematics proved it in one page.

Counting Without Counting

How many 5-card poker hands exist? You'll never list 2,598,960 of them — combinatorics counts them anyway, with two humble rules.

Rule of product: independent choices multiply (a 4-digit PIN: 10×10×10×10 = 10,000). Rule of sum: exclusive cases add. From these grows C(n,k) — the number of ways to choose k things from n. And every value lives in one triangle:

Why is each cell the sum of the two above? To choose k of n items: either you take item #n (then choose k−1 from the rest) or you don't (choose k from the rest). Add the cases — the triangle rule, proven in one sentence.
Field note · poker, properly counted Hands: C(52,5) = 2,598,960. Flushes: 4 suits × C(13,5) = 4 × 1,287 = 5,148 → about 0.2%. Choosing a team of 3 from 8 people? Not 8×7×6 = 336 — that counts line-ups. The same trio appears in 3! = 6 orders, so teams = 336 ÷ 6 = C(8,3) = 56. That division is the entire soul of “choose”. Casinos are built on this camp.
⛺ Final checkpoint
A 3-person team from 8 people. How many possible teams?
336 counts ordered line-ups; divide by the 3! = 6 orderings of each trio → 56. (8³ would allow picking the same person three times.)
