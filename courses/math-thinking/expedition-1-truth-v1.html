Day 1 · §1.1.1 Triangle Tanglehow do you KNOW it's true?

The 180° You Were Told To Believe

You've been told the angles of a triangle add to 180°. But how do you know? Your teacher said so? You measured a few? Drag the triangle below — then watch the actual proof appear.

Lab · drag the apex
Drag the red apex anywhere. The three angles change — their sum doesn't. That's evidence. Now click "Reveal the proof".

The proof: draw a line through the apex parallel to the base. The two base angles reappear at the top as alternate angles (the Z-pattern) — and together with the apex angle they form a straight line. A straight line is 180°. Done — for every triangle that could ever exist, in two sentences.

Field note · what just happened Measuring triangles gives you confidence. The parallel-line argument gives you certainty — it doesn't depend on which triangle you drew. The whole book is about learning to make this move yourself.
⛺ Checkpoint
Why is the parallel-line argument better than measuring 1,000 triangles?
Speed is nice but not the point: the argument never mentions a specific triangle, so its conclusion holds for all of them — infinitely many — forever.

Day 2 · §1.1.2 Prime Timeopen problems: where proof hasn't reached

Goldbach: True for Quadrillions, Proven for None

In 1742, Christian Goldbach guessed: every even number ≥ 4 is the sum of two primes. 283 years later, nobody has proven it — or found a counterexample. Be the tester:

Lab · the Goldbach machine
Even number:
Try 4, 36, 1000, 99998… Every even number you'll ever test works (it's been verified past 4×10¹⁸). And yet — Day 1 taught you why this proves nothing. The proof is still missing; finding it would make you instantly famous.

This is the strange life of mathematicians: a statement can be almost certainly true and still officially unknown. Notice the pattern in your results too: big even numbers have many prime pairs — which makes the missing proof even more maddening.

⛺ Checkpoint
What would it take to settle Goldbach FALSE?
Asymmetry of proof: confirming examples never finish the job, but ONE counterexample kills a universal claim instantly. That's why mathematicians hunt both directions at once.

Day 3 · §1.1.3 Irrational Irreverenceyour first full proof, step by step

√2 Is Not a Fraction — Walk the Proof

Legend says the Pythagoreans drowned Hippasus for proving this. Step through the same argument yourself — five clicks, total certainty at the end.

Lab · proof stepper
This is "proof by contradiction" — you'll meet it formally in Expedition 4. Assume the opposite, follow the logic, watch the assumption destroy itself.
Field note · why this mattered so much The Pythagoreans believed everything was ratios of whole numbers. This 5-step argument proves their worldview impossible — using nothing but even/odd. It's also the book's first demonstration that a proof can be short, elementary, and earth-shattering at the same time.
⛺ Checkpoint
The proof assumes √2 = a/b "in lowest terms". Why is that allowed?
6/4 → 3/2: keep dividing out common factors and the process must stop. So IF √2 were a fraction, a lowest-terms version would exist — and that's the version the proof then strangles.

Day 4 · §1.2 Exposition Exhibitionsymbols, style & spotting bad proofs

Write Right, Read Right

Mathematics is a written art. The book spends 20 pages on it because a proof that can't be read isn't a proof — and a confident-sounding argument can hide a fatal flaw.

The symbols you'll use everywhere

∀ "for all" · ∃ "there exists" · ∈ "is in" · ⇒ "implies" · ∎ "proof complete". One sentence, two ways:

Field note · same claim, two costumes Words: every even number bigger than 2 can be written as a sum of two primes.
Symbols: ∀n ∈ E, n > 2 ⇒ ∃p,q primes with n = p + q.
Symbols aren't for showing off — they remove ambiguity. But the book's rule is balance: use symbols for precision, words for flow. A wall of pure symbols is as unreadable as a wall of pure prose.

Spot the flaw — proofs that lie

Here's a "proof" that 2 = 1. Every line looks innocent. One is a crime:

Lab · find the illegal line
1  Let a = b (both nonzero)
2  Multiply both sides by a:  a² = ab
3  Subtract b²:  a² − b² = ab − b²
4  Factor:  (a+b)(a−b) = b(a−b)
5  Divide by (a−b):  a + b = b
6  Since a = b:  2b = b, so 2 = 1  ∎?!

Per the book, the main reason to write proofs in clear prose (not just symbols) is…
A proof's entire purpose is to let a skeptical reader check every step. Writing IS the mathematics — which is why this 698-page math book is mostly… sentences.

Day 5 · §1.4.1–1.4.3 Puzzle valley, part 1funny money & Gauss in the house

The Missing Dollar & the 8-Year-Old Who Beat Arithmetic

Puzzle valley begins. First: a story where $1 vanishes into thin air. Then: the pairing trick young Gauss used to humiliate his teacher's busywork.

The hotel's missing dollar

Three friends pay $30 for a room. It should've been $25, so the bellhop returns $5; they each take $1 back and tip him $2. Now: each friend paid $9 (= $27), plus the $2 tip… $29. Where's the missing dollar?

Lab · follow the money
The puzzle's "$27 + $2" is an accounting crime: the $2 tip is already INSIDE the $27. The lesson (the book's real point): a confidently-worded wrong calculation feels like math — until you demand that every step be justified. Same skill as Day 4's flaw-hunt.

Gauss in the house

A teacher orders the class to add 1+2+⋯+100. Eight-year-old Gauss answers in seconds: pair the ends. 1+100, 2+99, 3+98… every pair makes 101, and there are 50 pairs: 5050.

Lab · the pairing trick
n = 10 
n/2 pairs, each summing to n+1 → total n(n+1)/2. You met this formula at the highlights trek as a staircase; here is where it was born. (The book then asks: what if n is odd? Try to convince yourself the formula survives.)
⛺ Checkpoint
Sum 1 + 2 + ⋯ + 200, using Gauss's trick:
100 pairs × 201 = 20,100 — or n(n+1)/2 = 200×201/2. No adding machine required.

Day 6 · §1.4.4 Friend Trendsyour first impossibility proof — by playing

The Party Where Patterns Are Unavoidable

At any party of 6 people, there must be 3 mutual friends or 3 mutual strangers. Don't believe it? The lab below lets you try to defeat the theorem. You will lose — and the losing IS the lesson.

Lab · the Ramsey game — avoid a same-colour triangle

Click each connection to cycle:
grey → friends → enemies

Colour all the lines. Goal: NO triangle with 3 sides the same colour.
With 5 people there's a clever colouring that wins (hint: friends-pentagon, enemies-star). With 6, every single one of the 32,768 colourings contains a one-colour triangle — that's Ramsey's theorem, the gateway to a whole field of combinatorics.
Field note · why 6 always traps you Pick any person at the 6-party. They have 5 connections, so at least 3 are the same colour — say 3 friends. Now look at those 3 friends among themselves: if ANY pair of them are friends, they + you form a friend-triangle. If NO pair is, they form an enemy-triangle themselves. Trapped either way. ∎  (That's a complete proof — pigeonhole + case analysis, two tools you'll use forever.)
⛺ Checkpoint
Why does the argument need at least 3 same-coloured connections from the first person?
Split 5 things into 2 boxes: some box gets ⌈5/2⌉ = 3. The humble pigeonhole principle — you'll meet it properly in Expedition 8 — is doing the heavy lifting.

Day 7 · §1.4.5 The Full Monty Hallsummit day — intuition vs proof

Three Doors, One Car, Thousands of Fooled PhDs

A car behind one door, goats behind two. You pick a door; the host — who knows where the car is — opens a different door showing a goat, then offers a switch. In 1990 Marilyn vos Savant said "always switch" and ~10,000 readers, hundreds with PhDs, wrote in to call her wrong. She wasn't. Play first:

Field note · the proof in one breath Your first pick is the car with probability 1/3 — nothing the host does changes that, because he can ALWAYS show you a goat. So the car is behind "the other unopened door" with probability 2/3. Switching wins exactly when your first pick was wrong: 2/3 of the time. The host's inside knowledge is what your gut keeps ignoring — if he opened a random door (sometimes revealing the car), switching truly wouldn't help.
⛺ Final checkpoint
Why is "two doors left, so it's 50–50" wrong?
Equal-looking options aren't equally likely when one of them was filtered by someone who knows the answer. Your simulation above is the empirical witness; the 1/3-stays-1/3 argument is the proof. Intuition lost; proof won — the perfect end to Expedition 1.
