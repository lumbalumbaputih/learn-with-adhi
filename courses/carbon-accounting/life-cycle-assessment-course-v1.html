Module 01What is LCA?

Before any tool, any formula, any ISO standard — you need one clear mental picture of what LCA actually does. This module builds that picture.

TL;DR
LCA stands for Life Cycle Assessment. It is a method for measuring the environmental impact of a product, material, or service across its entire life — from extracting raw materials, through manufacturing, use, and finally disposal or recycling. The goal is to stop blaming only one part of the chain and see the whole thing.

The "from cradle to grave" idea

Imagine a plastic bottle of shampoo. Most people only think about the moment they throw it in the bin. But the real environmental story started months earlier — mining bauxite for the cap, drilling petroleum for the plastic, running energy-hungry factories, trucking it across countries, heating water every time you use it, and finally landfilling or recycling the empty container.

LCA forces you to account for all of those stages, not just the one that is convenient to look at. That is the core idea. Everything else in this course is just methodology for doing that accounting rigorously.

The five life cycle stages LCA tracks. Every arrow is a flow of energy, material, or waste.

Three things LCA is not

Not this

A simple carbon footprint calculator that only counts one gas (CO₂) at one stage.

A policy recommendation — LCA produces data, not decisions.

A marketing label ("eco-certified") — those often lack LCA rigour.

Actually this

A multi-impact method covering climate change, water use, toxicity, land use, and more.

A tool that gives decision-makers evidence to work with.

A scientifically structured process governed by ISO standards (14040 and 14044).

A quick analogy

Think of LCA like a full financial audit of a company — not just the profit at the end of the year, but every transaction, every supplier cost, every hidden liability. You can't know if the company is actually healthy by looking only at the final balance. Same with a product's environmental impact.

Module 02Why LCA matters

Knowing what LCA is isn't enough — you need to know where it fits in the real world and why practitioners actually use it.

TL;DR
LCA prevents "burden shifting" — fixing one environmental problem while quietly creating another. It is used by companies, governments, researchers, and NGOs to compare products, design better materials, shape policy, and back up environmental claims with evidence.

The burden-shifting problem

In the 1990s, several countries switched from plastic bags to paper bags for supermarkets, believing paper was obviously greener. But LCA studies showed that paper bags require significantly more energy and water to produce, and more transport weight per bag. Unless a paper bag is reused many times, its climate impact is actually higher than a plastic bag over its full life. Neither is ideal — but without LCA, you'd never know which trade-offs you were making.

This is the core reason LCA exists: to stop optimising one part of a system while ignoring what you're doing to the rest.

LCA in circular economy work

Since you're doing your Master's in environment and climate emergency under Roberto Minunno's supervision (circular economy, design for disassembly), this is directly relevant to you. LCA is how circular economy claims get validated. Saying "this building is designed for disassembly so it's better" is not evidence — an LCA comparing the end-of-life scenario of a conventional building vs. a disassemblable one is evidence.

Relevance to your research
Building deconstruction LCA studies quantify how much embodied carbon and material value is recovered through disassembly versus demolition. If you're writing about barriers and enablers of deconstruction in Australia, LCA results are a common source of quantitative evidence in the literature.

Types of LCA by scope

Cradle-to-grave

Full life cycle

Covers raw material extraction through end-of-life disposal. The most complete picture. Used for consumer product comparisons and policy.

Cradle-to-gate

Up to factory exit

Stops at the point a product leaves the manufacturer. Common in B2B settings where the downstream use is unknown. Often used for material EPDs.

Cradle-to-cradle

Circular loop

Assumes materials are recovered and re-enter the production cycle. Used in circular economy design. Harder to model because it requires data on actual recovery rates.

Gate-to-gate

One process only

Covers only a single step in the supply chain. Common when a manufacturer only wants to assess their own factory operations.

Module 03The 4-phase framework (ISO 14040/14044)

LCA has an internationally agreed structure. Two ISO standards — 14040 (principles) and 14044 (requirements) — define four phases that every credible LCA must follow.

TL;DR
ISO 14040/14044 divides an LCA into four phases: (1) Goal and Scope, (2) Life Cycle Inventory, (3) Life Cycle Impact Assessment, and (4) Interpretation. They're not strictly linear — the standard explicitly says iteration between phases is expected and necessary.

Why ISO standards matter

Without a standard, every organisation could define LCA however they liked. You couldn't compare studies, and companies could cherry-pick the methodology that made their product look best. ISO 14040 (published 2006, revised 2021) and ISO 14044 establish the rules of the game so that an LCA done in Australia can be meaningfully compared to one done in Germany.

In practice, you don't need to memorise the ISO documents. You need to understand the framework they define well enough to apply it.

The four ISO 14040 phases and their iterative relationship

The four phases at a glance

Phase 1

Goal and Scope Definition

You decide why you're doing the LCA, what product or system it covers, where the system boundaries are drawn, and what the functional unit is (the unit of comparison). This is the most important phase — getting it wrong makes the rest of the study meaningless.

Phase 2

Life Cycle Inventory (LCI)

You collect data on every input (energy, water, raw materials) and every output (products, emissions, waste) for every process within your system boundary. This is the most time-consuming phase. Data can come from your own measurements, industry databases, or published literature.

Phase 3

Life Cycle Impact Assessment (LCIA)

You translate the raw inventory data (e.g., "X kg of CO₂, Y litres of water, Z kg of phosphate") into environmental impact categories (climate change, eutrophication, water scarcity, etc.) using characterisation factors from established impact assessment methods like ReCiPe or CML.

Phase 4

Interpretation

You analyse the results, check their consistency with your goal, identify the life cycle stages or processes that drive the most impact (hotspots), run sensitivity and uncertainty analyses, and draw conclusions. This phase feeds back into all others if issues are found.

Common beginner mistake
Treating the four phases as a strict one-way sequence. In reality, ISO 14044 requires iteration. If your impact assessment in Phase 3 reveals that a supplier's process you ignored is actually a major hotspot, you go back to Phase 2 to collect that data properly.

Module 04Phase 1 — Goal & Scope Definition

This is the most consequential phase. Every choice you make here determines what your LCA can and cannot tell you. Spend more time here than you think you need to.

TL;DR
Phase 1 requires you to define: why you're doing the study, who it's for, what system you're studying (system boundary), what one unit of that system you'll compare against (functional unit), and what data quality you need. Mess any of these up and the study is either misleading or useless.

1. The goal statement

Your goal statement must answer four questions, and it must be written before any data collection begins:

  • Intended application: What decision will this LCA inform? (e.g., "Compare the environmental impact of concrete vs. cross-laminated timber frames for a 6-storey residential building in Perth")
  • Reasons for the study: Why now? Internal product development, EPD certification, academic research, policy submission?
  • Intended audience: Internal team only, a regulator, the public? This determines how much detail and transparency is required.
  • Comparative assertion: If you're comparing two products and will make the results public, ISO 14044 imposes additional requirements including critical review by an external panel.

2. The functional unit — the most critical concept in LCA

The functional unit is the reference unit to which all inputs and outputs in your study are normalised. It defines what the system is supposed to do, not just what it is.

Example — paint

If you're comparing water-based vs. solvent-based paint, a bad functional unit is "1 litre of paint" — because the two paints may have different coverage rates and different lifespans before repainting is needed. A good functional unit is "painting 1 m² of interior wall surface with adequate coverage for 10 years." Now you're comparing what both paints actually do.

Warning
Choosing a functional unit that favours one product over another is one of the most common ways LCA studies are manipulated, intentionally or not. Always define the functional unit in terms of the service delivered, not the physical quantity of the product.

3. System boundary

The system boundary defines what processes are inside your LCA and what are outside it. There is no universal correct boundary — it depends on your goal — but you must document it clearly and defend your decisions.

Boundary decisionWhat to consider
Which life stages to includeRaw material, manufacturing, distribution, use, end-of-life. You must justify any stages you exclude.
GeographyAre you using global average data or location-specific data? Electricity grids vary enormously by country.
Cut-off criteriaVery small flows (typically <1% of total mass or energy) can be excluded, but you must document this and ensure excluded flows don't account for >5% total.
Capital goodsDo you include the factory building and machinery that makes the product? Often excluded in attributional LCA, included in some consequential LCA.
Co-productsIf a process produces multiple outputs (e.g., a refinery produces petrol, diesel, and lubricants), how do you allocate the environmental burden between them?

4. Allocation

Allocation is the tricky problem of dividing environmental burden between co-products from a shared process. ISO 14044 recommends avoiding allocation where possible (by expanding the system or subdividing processes), but when it's unavoidable, you can allocate by mass, energy content, or economic value.

Example — milk and cheese

A dairy farm produces milk and manure. If you're doing an LCA of cheese, you need to decide how much of the farm's emissions to assign to the milk vs. the manure (which is used as fertiliser). Allocating by mass gives a different answer to allocating by economic value. Document which you chose and why.

5. Data quality requirements

State upfront what quality of data you need — geographic representativeness, temporal representativeness (how recent), technological representativeness (does the data match the actual process you're studying), and completeness. This becomes a benchmark to evaluate your data against in Phase 2.

A researcher wants to compare reusable ceramic mugs vs. disposable paper cups for a café. She defines the functional unit as "1 ceramic mug" vs. "1 paper cup." What is wrong with this?

Module 05Phase 2 — Life Cycle Inventory (LCI)

The inventory is the data engine of an LCA. This is where you quantify every flow of material and energy into and out of your system. It is painstaking, but the quality of your results is only as good as the quality of your inventory.

TL;DR
The LCI maps all inputs (raw materials, energy, water) and outputs (products, by-products, emissions to air, water, and land) for every process within your system boundary. Data comes from primary sources (your own measurements), secondary sources (databases), or literature. You then validate and aggregate it into a ready-for-impact-assessment dataset.

What you're actually collecting

Every process in your LCA is modelled as a box with quantified inputs and outputs

Where data comes from

Source typeDescriptionPros / Cons
Primary dataCollected directly from the process: metering energy use, weighing materials, measuring emissionsMost accurate for your specific case. Expensive and time-consuming to collect.
LCA databasesecoinvent (most comprehensive, paid), GaBi databases, ELCD (free, EU), AusLCI (Australian specific)Fast and standardised. May not perfectly match your actual process or location.
EPDsEnvironmental Product Declarations from manufacturers — contain verified LCI data for specific productsDirectly relevant to real products. Only covers the product, not your full system.
LiteraturePeer-reviewed studies with reported inventory dataOften freely available. Temporal and geographic representativeness can be a concern.

The LCI process step by step

  • Draw the process flow diagram. Map every unit process within your system boundary and draw the flows between them. Do this on paper first. It forces you to see what's connected to what before you touch any numbers.
  • Identify data needs. For each process box, list every input and output you need to quantify. Be specific: not just "energy" but "electricity from the South West Interconnected System (SWIS) in WA."
  • Collect data. Start with primary data for your most important processes (usually the ones that drive the most material or energy flow). Use databases for background processes (e.g., the upstream supply chain of a commodity like steel).
  • Normalise to the functional unit. All data must be expressed per your functional unit. If your functional unit is "1 tonne of structural steel delivered to site," then every input and output gets divided or scaled to that reference.
  • Handle allocation. For any multi-output processes, apply the allocation approach you defined in Phase 1.
  • Validate and check mass balance. The total mass of inputs should roughly equal total mass of outputs (some is lost to emissions, but large discrepancies indicate missing data or errors).
  • Document data sources and quality. For each data point, record where it came from, when it was produced, and how representative it is of your system. This is not optional — it's required by ISO 14044.

Key LCA databases to know

For Australian practitioners
AusLCI is the Australian Life Cycle Inventory Database — free, maintained by the Australian Life Cycle Assessment Society (ALCAS). It includes Australian-specific electricity grid data, construction materials, and transport. Always prefer AusLCI data over European databases when studying Australian systems.
Global standard
ecoinvent (v3.x) is the most widely used LCA background database globally. It covers ~18,000 processes across agriculture, energy, transport, manufacturing, waste treatment, and more. Requires a paid licence but is available free through most LCA software for students.

Module 06Phase 3 — Life Cycle Impact Assessment (LCIA)

Your inventory is a long list of raw flows: kilograms of CO₂, litres of water, grams of methane, milligrams of cadmium. LCIA translates this into environmental impact scores that humans can actually interpret and compare.

TL;DR
LCIA converts inventory flows into impact category indicators using characterisation factors. The process has three mandatory steps (classification, characterisation) and two optional ones (normalisation, weighting). The result is a profile of impacts across categories like climate change, water depletion, and toxicity.

The LCIA steps

  • Classification. Assign each inventory flow to the impact categories it contributes to. CO₂ and methane both go to "climate change." Phosphate runoff goes to "eutrophication." Some flows contribute to multiple categories (e.g., NOₓ affects both climate change and acidification).
  • Characterisation. Multiply each flow by its characterisation factor to produce an impact score. CO₂ has a Global Warming Potential (GWP) of 1. Methane (CH₄) has a GWP of 28 over 100 years. So 1 kg of methane = 28 kg CO₂ equivalent (CO₂e). This converts all flows within a category into a single comparable unit.
  • Normalisation (optional). Divide your impact scores by a reference value (e.g., the average European citizen's annual impact) to understand the relative magnitude of your result. Makes categories comparable to each other in scale.
  • Weighting (optional, not allowed in comparative public studies per ISO 14044). Apply weights to different impact categories based on their perceived importance. Controversial because it involves value judgements. Use with caution and always report unweighted results too.

LCIA methods — which one to use?

Characterisation factors come from published LCIA methods. You don't calculate them yourself — you select a method and your LCA software applies the factors. The most common methods are:

ReCiPe 2016

Most widely used globally

Covers 18 midpoint categories and 3 endpoint categories (human health, ecosystem quality, resource scarcity). Available in two perspectives: Hierarchist (recommended default), Individualist, Egalitarian. Used in most LCA software by default.

CML-IA

Conservative, midpoint-only

Developed by Leiden University. Older and more conservative — only calculates midpoint indicators, not endpoint. Often required by EPD programme operators and used in construction sector LCA.

ILCD / EF 3.1

EU standard for product footprints

The European Commission's recommended method under the Product Environmental Footprint (PEF) framework. 16 impact categories. Increasingly required for EU market access and green claims substantiation.

Midpoint vs. endpoint — what's the difference?

Midpoint

Measures the problem in physical/chemical terms, closer to the emission. Example: kg CO₂ equivalent tells you about radiative forcing — the mechanism of warming.

More scientifically certain. Less intuitive for non-experts. Preferred in academic and EPD contexts.

Endpoint

Translates midpoint results into damage to "areas of protection" — human health (DALYs lost), ecosystem quality (species lost), resource scarcity ($).

More intuitive for decision-makers. Less scientifically certain because the damage modelling involves more assumptions.

An LCA reports that Product A causes 150 kg CO₂e and Product B causes 120 kg CO₂e of climate change impact, but Product A causes less eutrophication and less freshwater depletion than Product B. Which product is "better"?

Module 07Phase 4 — Interpretation

Interpretation is where your results become useful. It is not just writing a summary — it is a structured analytical process that ISO 14044 requires you to do before drawing any conclusions.

TL;DR
Interpretation has three required elements: (1) identify significant issues (hotspots), (2) evaluate the results for completeness, sensitivity, and consistency, and (3) draw conclusions and limitations. This phase can and should send you back to earlier phases if problems are found.

Step 1 — Identify significant issues (hotspot analysis)

A hotspot is a life cycle stage, process, or flow that contributes disproportionately to one or more impact categories. Finding hotspots tells you where environmental improvement efforts will have the biggest effect.

Example — building LCA

A building LCA might find that the concrete slab and structural steel together account for 65% of embodied carbon (a hotspot in production stage) while the use-phase heating accounts for 70% of total lifecycle energy. The conclusion: for short-lived buildings, tackle embodied carbon first; for long-lived buildings, operational energy matters more. Without hotspot analysis you'd treat all stages equally and misallocate effort.

Step 2 — Evaluate results

ISO 14044 requires three types of evaluation before you can claim your results are reliable:

Completeness check

Is all necessary information present?

Go through each life cycle stage systematically and verify that no significant processes, flows, or impact categories are missing. If gaps exist, document them and assess whether they affect the conclusions.

Sensitivity analysis

How much do your results depend on uncertain choices?

Change one assumption at a time — the allocation method, a data value with high uncertainty, the LCIA method — and see how much the results change. If swapping a key assumption flips your conclusion, your conclusion is not robust. Report this honestly.

Consistency check

Were your methods applied consistently?

If you're comparing two products, were data sources of the same quality for both? Were system boundaries defined the same way? Inconsistency between compared systems is a major source of bias in comparative LCA.

Step 3 — Conclusions, recommendations, limitations

Your conclusions must be directly traceable to your results — don't overstate what the data supports. For every conclusion, identify the corresponding limitation. Standard reporting includes:

  • State which product or scenario performs better on which impact categories
  • Identify trade-offs clearly — do not collapse them into a single "winner" without explicit justification
  • State the scope of validity — e.g., "These results apply to the Australian context using 2024 grid data; results would differ with a renewable energy grid"
  • Recommend where further data collection would most improve result reliability
  • State what the results cannot be used to conclude — this is as important as what they can

Critical review (for public comparative studies)

If your LCA compares two products and the results will be made public, ISO 14044 requires an independent critical review by a qualified external reviewer (or a review panel). This reviewer checks that your goal and scope were appropriate, your methods were consistent with ISO requirements, your data was appropriate and reasonable, and your interpretations were consistent with the results.

Note for academic work
In a Master's thesis or research paper, the "critical review" function is partly fulfilled by your supervisors and peer reviewers. But you should still apply the completeness, sensitivity, and consistency checks yourself before submitting — reviewers will catch if you haven't.
You've run a sensitivity analysis and found that if you change the allocation method from mass-based to economic-value-based, the ranking between Product A and Product B reverses — Product B goes from worse to better on climate change. What does this mean for your conclusions?

Module 09Common Mistakes & Pitfalls

Most LCA errors are made in Phase 1 and only discovered in Phase 3 or 4. This module is a checklist of the problems that trip up beginners — and some experienced practitioners too.

TL;DR
The most dangerous mistakes are: poorly defined functional units, system boundaries that conveniently exclude inconvenient stages, using geographically inappropriate data, ignoring uncertainty, and overstating conclusions beyond what the data supports. All of these are avoidable with discipline in Phase 1.

Mistake 1 — Biased functional unit

Defining the functional unit in a way that favours the product you want to win. Example: comparing an LED bulb to an incandescent using "1 bulb" instead of "providing 800 lumens of light for 1,000 hours." The LED lasts 25× longer and uses a fraction of the electricity — the functional unit must capture the service, not the object.

Mistake 2 — Convenient system boundary cuts

Excluding life stages or processes that would increase your product's impact. Common examples: excluding end-of-life because it's "uncertain," excluding use-phase energy because you want to focus on production, or excluding transport because the distances are hard to find. Every exclusion must be documented, and its potential effect on results must be assessed.

Mistake 3 — Wrong geography

Using European or global average electricity data for an Australian study. Australian electricity is more carbon-intensive on average than European but varies significantly by state. WA's SWIS grid, the NEM in eastern states, and off-grid diesel systems have entirely different emission factors. Using the wrong grid can swing your climate change results by 50% or more.

Mistake 4 — Using old data

Electricity grids are changing fast — a study using 2015 Australian grid data will significantly overestimate climate impact compared to 2024 data because renewables have grown substantially. ISO 14044 requires temporal representativeness — your data should be recent enough to represent the current system, or you must clearly state the temporal scope.

Mistake 5 — Not checking data quality

Treating all numbers from databases as equally reliable. Some ecoinvent datasets are based on direct measurement; others are estimated from decades-old studies. Check the data quality indicators in your database entries and flag low-quality data sources — they may be candidates for sensitivity analysis.

Mistake 6 — Single-issue thinking

Optimising only for climate change and ignoring other categories. A product with lower CO₂ but higher water toxicity, land use, or heavy metal pollution is not unambiguously better. Report all relevant impact categories and discuss trade-offs explicitly.

Mistake 7 — Confusing LCA types

Attributional LCA

Models the current, average supply chain as it exists. Answers: "What environmental burden is attributable to this product today?" Most common. Uses average market mixes.

Consequential LCA

Models the marginal changes that would result from a decision. Answers: "What happens to the environment if we switch from A to B?" More complex, uses marginal suppliers, appropriate for policy analysis.

Beginners often don't realise there are two fundamentally different LCA paradigms. The choice affects which datasets you use, how you handle allocation, and what conclusions are valid. Be explicit about which approach your study uses.

Mistake 8 — Overclaiming conclusions

Writing "Product A is environmentally superior to Product B" when the data shows Product A is better on three categories and worse on two, and the difference on the key category is within the uncertainty range. LCA supports nuanced, conditional conclusions — not marketing slogans. If your report's conclusion sounds like an ad, rewrite it.

Red flag sentence in any LCA report
"This product has been proven environmentally friendly using LCA." This is almost certainly an overstatement. LCA produces comparative results with specific scope and limitations — it does not certify absolute environmental friendliness.
A company releases an LCA showing their new packaging uses 30% less climate change impact than the old packaging. But the study only covers production (cradle-to-gate) and the new packaging uses much more water in manufacturing. Which TWO mistakes is this study likely making?

Module 10Your First LCA — A Step-by-Step Walkthrough

Theory done. Now let's walk through a real, simple LCA from start to finish. We'll compare two options for carrying groceries: a single-use plastic bag vs. a reusable cotton tote bag. Classic example. Deliberately simple. Every concept from the course applies.

TL;DR
Follow all four ISO phases in order. The cotton tote produces far more impact per bag, but reuse flips the comparison. The answer depends entirely on your functional unit — which is why Phase 1 is everything.

Phase 1 — Goal and Scope

Goal statement

Intended application: Compare the environmental impact of two grocery bag options to inform a consumer awareness campaign.

Intended audience: General public and sustainability officers at a supermarket chain in Australia.

Reasons: A supermarket is deciding which bag type to promote as the "greener" option.

Comparative assertion: Yes, results will be public — critical review is required.

Functional unit

"Carrying one week of groceries for one person for one year." This normalises both options to the same service. A cotton tote used weekly for a year = 52 uses. A single-use plastic bag used once per shopping trip = 52 bags. Now we're comparing apples to apples.

System boundary

Cradle-to-grave. Include: raw material production (cotton farming or petroleum refining), manufacturing (weaving or extrusion), distribution (transport to store), use phase (washing for cotton, single use for plastic), end-of-life (landfill for plastic, or continued reuse for cotton). Exclude: retail store energy (shared across both, negligible difference).

On climate change, the cotton tote wins over one year of use — but only because we assumed 52 washes. If the tote is used only 10 times before being lost or discarded, its climate impact per trip is 2.2 + 0.5 = 2.7 kg CO₂e vs. 1.6 kg CO₂e for plastic. Reuse frequency is the critical variable.

The cotton tote's per-unit climate impact drops with every reuse. It only beats plastic after ~20 uses on climate change — but may remain worse on water use regardless.

Phase 4 — Interpretation

Hotspot analysis

Cotton farming dominates the tote's impact across most categories — particularly water use, land use, and pesticide-related ecotoxicity. For the plastic bag, the end-of-life (landfill, marine litter potential) and oil extraction are the key hotspots.

Sensitivity analysis

The most sensitive variable is the number of times the cotton tote is reused. At 20 uses, break-even on climate change. At 50+ uses, the tote is clearly better on climate. But the water use advantage for plastic persists regardless of reuse count. This trade-off is the central finding.

Conclusion (what you would actually write)

"The cotton tote bag produces lower climate change impact than single-use plastic bags when used more than approximately 20 times, but it consistently produces higher freshwater depletion and land use impacts regardless of reuse frequency. There is no single 'environmentally superior' option across all impact categories. The choice depends on which environmental impact the decision-maker prioritises. Both options can be improved: for the cotton tote, switching to organic cotton reduces pesticide ecotoxicity; for the plastic bag, ensuring proper recycling reduces end-of-life burden. These results are based on simplified illustrative data and should not substitute a full peer-reviewed LCA for public comparative claims."

What to do next

  • Practice with a spreadsheet LCA first. Before touching any software, manually model a 3-process system (e.g., producing 1 kg of bread: wheat farming → milling → baking) in Excel with inputs, outputs, and one impact category. It will teach you more than three hours of clicking in SimaPro.
  • Download openLCA and connect AusLCI. Free, professional, and directly relevant to Australian work. The GreenDelta YouTube channel has beginner tutorials. Do one of them end-to-end.
  • Read one published LCA in your field. Find a peer-reviewed LCA paper on building materials or circular economy in the last 5 years. Read the methodology section carefully — you now have the vocabulary to understand it.
  • Identify what LCA questions arise in your own Master's research. If you're comparing deconstruction vs. demolition scenarios, what would be your functional unit? What life cycle stages would you include? What data sources are available in Australia? Answering these is Phase 1 of your own potential LCA.
  • Connect with ALCAS. The Australian Life Cycle Assessment Society runs free webinars, publishes the AusLCI database, and has an active network of practitioners and academics in Perth/WA. Introduce yourself as a Curtin postgrad — the community is helpful.
