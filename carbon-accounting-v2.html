Module 1.1

What Even Is Carbon Accounting?

Imagine your electricity bill. Every month, someone reads your meter, records how many kilowatt-hours you used, and sends you a number. You didn't generate the electricity yourself — but you're accountable for how much you consumed.

Carbon accounting works the same way, but for greenhouse gases. Instead of kilowatt-hours, you're measuring tonnes of CO₂-equivalent (tCO₂e). Instead of one meter, you have hundreds of "meters" across your entire business — fuel tanks, electricity sockets, delivery trucks, flights, supply chains.

Analogy

Think of your personal bank statement. Every transaction is logged — coffee, rent, groceries. Carbon accounting is like building a "bank statement" for a company's emissions. Every activity that produces greenhouse gases gets logged, categorised, and totalled up.

The output? A carbon footprint — a single number (in tCO₂e) that tells a company "this is how much you contributed to climate change this year." That number drives regulation, investor decisions, and strategy.

Module 1.2

Until recently, only large energy companies cared about carbon numbers. That changed fast. Here's the chain reaction:

Real-World Context · 2026

Regulations: The EU's CSRD (Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive) now requires ~50,000 companies to report emissions. The SEC in the US has tightened climate disclosure rules for public companies. Australia's climate reporting regime kicked in for large reporters.

Investors: ESG-focused investments are projected at $33.9 trillion. Investors won't put money in what they can't measure.

Talent gap: 63% of companies report being understaffed in this area. Demand vastly outpaces supply.

The punchline: if you learn to measure emissions accurately, you become one of the most sought-after professionals in sustainability. The GHG Protocol — which we'll learn next — is the "GAAP of carbon." It's the universal language.

Module 1.3

The GHG Protocol — Your Rulebook

The Greenhouse Gas Protocol is the world's most widely used standard for measuring emissions. Think of it as the "accounting standards" (like GAAP or IFRS) but for carbon. Over 92% of Fortune 500 companies use it.

It was created by the World Resources Institute (WRI) and the World Business Council for Sustainable Development (WBCSD). It defines:

It covers seven greenhouse gases, but for simplicity, everything gets converted into one unit: tonnes of CO₂-equivalent (tCO₂e). Methane is ~80× more potent than CO₂ over 20 years, so 1 tonne of methane = ~28 tCO₂e (over 100 years). This conversion is called the Global Warming Potential (GWP).

Analogy

It's like converting all currencies to USD so you can add them up. You wouldn't add yen and euros directly. GWP lets you add methane, nitrous oxide, and CO₂ into one total.

Module 1.5

The Basic Calculation

Every emissions calculation in carbon accounting follows one core formula:

The Formula

Activity Data × Emission Factor = tCO₂e

Activity Data: How much of something you did (e.g., 10,000 kWh of electricity, 5,000 litres of diesel)

Emission Factor: How much CO₂e each unit produces (e.g., 0.82 kgCO₂e per kWh in Australia for grid electricity)

Analogy

It's like calculating your grocery bill. "Quantity × Price = Total." Activity data is the quantity, emission factor is the price, and tCO₂e is your total bill. The "price list" (emission factors) comes from government databases.

Module 2.1

Organisational Boundaries — Who Counts What?

Before measuring a single tonne of CO₂, you need to draw a line around what's "yours." The GHG Protocol gives you two approaches:

Two Approaches

Equity Share: You report emissions proportional to your ownership. If you own 30% of a joint venture, you report 30% of its emissions.

Operational Control: If you control the operations, you report 100% of emissions — even if you only own 30%. This is the most commonly used approach because it's cleaner.

Analogy

Imagine a shared house. Equity share = you split the electricity bill based on how many rooms each person owns. Operational control = whoever controls the thermostat pays the full bill. Most companies prefer "whoever holds the thermostat" because it's simpler to manage.

Module 2.2

Scope 2 Deep Dive: Location vs. Market-Based

This is one of the trickiest (and most practical) distinctions you'll encounter. The GHG Protocol requires companies to report Scope 2 in two ways:

The Two Methods

Location-based: Uses the average grid emission factor where you physically are. "The grid in NSW emits 0.68 kgCO₂e per kWh, so that's what we report."

Market-based: Uses the emission factor from your specific energy contract. If you signed a Power Purchase Agreement (PPA) with a wind farm, your market-based factor might be near zero.

Analogy

It's like measuring your diet by what's available at the supermarket (location-based) vs. what you actually put in your trolley (market-based). If you deliberately choose organic, your "market-based" footprint is different from the average shopper.

This matters because companies can reduce their market-based Scope 2 to near-zero by buying renewable energy certificates (RECs) or signing PPAs — even while the grid they plug into is still coal-heavy. Critics call this "greenwashing." Defenders call it "driving demand for renewables." You'll encounter this debate constantly.

Module 3.1

The Reporting Landscape — ISSB, CSRD, and SEC

Carbon accounting doesn't exist in a vacuum. It feeds into mandatory disclosure frameworks that are reshaping corporate reporting worldwide. Here are the three you need to know:

The Big Three Frameworks · 2026

ISSB (IFRS S1 & S2): Global baseline standards from the International Sustainability Standards Board. S2 specifically covers climate-related disclosures. Now mandatory in multiple jurisdictions including Australia and the UK. Designed to link sustainability data to financial value creation.

EU CSRD: The Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive — requires ~50,000 EU-connected companies to disclose using European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS). Requires third-party assurance (audit). Far more prescriptive than ISSB.

SEC Climate Rules (US): Public companies must include material climate risks in annual filings. More limited in scope than CSRD but significant for US-listed companies.

Analogy

Think of these like tax codes in different countries. The underlying maths (carbon accounting) is the same everywhere — just like profit is profit. But how you file, what you disclose, and who audits it depends on which jurisdiction your company operates in. A multinational might need to comply with all three.

Module 3.2

Base Year, Recalculation & Tracking Reductions

Companies don't just measure once — they set a base year and track progress against it. This is where the "accounting" part really earns its name.

How It Works

Base year: The reference year against which reductions are measured (e.g., "We'll reduce 42% from our 2020 baseline by 2030").

Recalculation trigger: If the company acquires or divests a business, changes methodology, or discovers a significant error, you must recalculate the base year so comparisons remain valid.

Threshold: Most companies set a significance threshold (e.g., 5%). Changes below that threshold don't trigger recalculation.

Analogy

It's like adjusting for inflation in financial comparisons. Saying "revenue grew 20% since 2020" is meaningless if you acquired three companies in that time. You need to restate the baseline so you're comparing like with like. Same logic applies to emissions.

Module 3.3

Scope 3 Calculation Methods

Scope 3 is where you earn your salary. The GHG Protocol offers several calculation approaches, each with trade-offs between accuracy and effort:

Visual · Data Hierarchy for Scope 3
🎯 Supplier-specific data — Actual emissions from your supplier
📦 Hybrid method — Mix of supplier data + industry averages
📊 Average-data method — Industry average EF × quantity purchased
💰 Spend-based method — $ spent × spend emission factor (least accurate)

↑ Most accurate     ↓ Least accurate (but easiest to start with)

Most companies start with spend-based for their first inventory (because they already have financial data), then progressively improve to supplier-specific data over 2–3 years. Your job is to build a roadmap for this data maturity journey.

Real Example · Spend-Based Calculation

A company spent $2 million on IT equipment. The EEIO (Environmentally Extended Input-Output) database says the "computer equipment" sector emits ~0.35 kgCO₂e per dollar spent.

$2,000,000 × 0.35 kgCO₂e/$ = 700 tCO₂e

Rough? Yes. Better than nothing? Absolutely. You'd then prioritise getting supplier-specific data from your top 5 IT vendors to refine this.

Module 3.4

Assurance & Verification — Getting Audited

Just like financial statements need an audit, carbon inventories increasingly need third-party assurance. The CSRD requires it. ISSB-aligned regimes are moving toward it.

There are two levels:

Assurance Levels

Limited assurance: "Nothing came to our attention that suggests material misstatement." It's a lighter review — the auditor checks your methodology and samples your data. Think of it like a spot-check tax audit.

Reasonable assurance: "We are confident the report is fairly stated." This is a full audit, like a financial audit. It's where the market is heading but most companies start with limited.

What auditors look for: documented methodology, clear boundary definitions, consistent emission factor sources, evidence trails for activity data, and proper base year recalculation policies. If you can produce an inventory that survives an audit, you're at a professional level.

Module 3.5

Science-Based Targets (SBTi)

Once you have an inventory, the next question is: "How much do we need to reduce?" The Science Based Targets initiative (SBTi) answers this by defining reduction pathways aligned with limiting warming to 1.5°C.

Measure baseline
→

→
Submit for validation

Report progress annually
What SBTi Requires

Near-term targets: 5–10 year horizon. Must cover Scope 1 & 2, and Scope 3 if it's >40% of total.

Net-zero targets: Long-term (by 2050). Requires reducing at least 90% of emissions, with only residual emissions offset.

Key rule: Carbon offsets cannot count toward near-term target progress. You must actually reduce, not just buy credits.
