MODULE 01 / 05

The Global Textile Industry

An introduction to how textiles are made, the scale of the fashion industry, and why understanding its systems is essential for anyone engaging with sustainability, policy, or everyday consumption.

LEARNING OBJECTIVES
  • Describe the structure of the global apparel supply chain from fibre to disposal
  • Distinguish between natural and synthetic textile fibre types and their trade-offs
  • Explain the business model of fast fashion and how it differs from traditional production
  • Quantify the global scale of the textile industry using key industry figures

Scale and Scope

The global textile and apparel industry generates approximately USD 1.5–2.5 trillion in annual revenue, employs more than 300 million people across the supply chain, and produces an estimated 100 billion garments per year — roughly 13 items for every person on Earth. These numbers place fashion among the most economically significant industries in the world, yet its environmental and social costs remain largely externalised from the price tag on a garment.

The industry operates across a complex geography. Raw fibre production is concentrated in South and Central Asia (cotton, silk) and the petrochemical sector (synthetics). Spinning, weaving, dyeing, and garment assembly are heavily concentrated in China, Bangladesh, Vietnam, India, and Cambodia, where labour costs remain low. Brands and retail are concentrated in Europe and North America. The result is a supply chain that can span a dozen countries before a finished garment reaches a consumer.

Fibre Types: Natural vs Synthetic

Every textile begins with a fibre. Fibres fall into two broad categories: natural fibres (plant-based or animal-based) and synthetic fibres (petroleum-derived). Each carries a distinct environmental and social profile.

FibreTypeShare of ProductionKey Issues
PolyesterSynthetic~52%Microplastic shedding, fossil fuel dependent, poor biodegradability
CottonNatural (plant)~24%High water use, pesticide-intensive, land degradation
Other synthetics (nylon, acrylic)Synthetic~12%Similar to polyester; nylon also has high N₂O emissions in production
WoolNatural (animal)~1%Land use, methane from sheep, but biodegradable and durable
Silk, linen, hemp, othersNatural~11%Generally lower impact but niche scale

Polyester's dominance is a relatively recent phenomenon driven by its low cost of production and its versatility. In 1980, polyester comprised less than 20% of fibre production. By 2020, it surpassed 50%. This shift has profound environmental implications, particularly around microplastic pollution (covered in Module 2).

The Fast Fashion Business Model

Traditional fashion operated on two seasons per year — spring/summer and autumn/winter. Brands would design collections months in advance, manufacture in large batches, and sell at relatively stable prices. Fast fashion disrupted this model fundamentally.

Pioneered by retailers such as Zara, H&M, and later Shein, fast fashion compresses the design-to-shelf cycle to as little as two weeks and operates on 52 or more micro-seasons per year. The business model depends on low unit prices, rapid trend turnover, and high volume. A t-shirt that retails for AUD 10 can only be manufactured at that price point if wages and environmental standards are compressed throughout the supply chain.

Key concept: Fast fashion externalises costs. The environmental damage from cotton irrigation, the health impacts on workers in dye factories, and the cost of landfilling garments are borne by communities and governments — not by the brand or the consumer at the point of sale.

Ultra-fast fashion, exemplified by Shein (which reportedly adds 2,000–10,000 new styles per day), represents an extreme acceleration of this model made possible by on-demand digital manufacturing and algorithmic demand forecasting.

The Anatomy of a Garment's Price

Understanding why exploitation persists in fashion supply chains requires understanding how the price of a garment is actually constructed. Most consumers assume that a $10 fast-fashion t-shirt is cheap because of efficient manufacturing. The reality is more uncomfortable: it is cheap because every cost along the supply chain has been compressed to its minimum, with labour bearing the most severe compression.

The most striking feature of this breakdown is that the person who physically assembled the garment — cutting fabric, operating a sewing machine, attaching buttons, pressing seams — receives approximately 3 cents of every dollar the consumer pays. The retail margin alone is often larger than the entire cost of production. This is not an accident or a market failure; it is the deliberate outcome of a business model that concentrates pricing power at the brand and retail end of the chain, far from the workers who bear the physical cost of production.

Why does the retail margin dominate? Brands price garments based on perceived value — the brand name, aspirational marketing, store experience — not on production cost. A fast-fashion item priced at $15 could sell for $10 and still be profitable. The decision to price higher and compress labour costs further is a strategic choice to maximise margin, not a necessity.

Environmental Impact

Textiles are among the most resource-intensive goods humans produce. This module quantifies the industry's footprint across water, carbon, chemical pollution, and solid waste — grounding environmental claims in data.

LEARNING OBJECTIVES
  • Quantify the water consumption associated with key textile fibres
  • Explain the mechanisms through which textiles contribute to greenhouse gas emissions
  • Describe the microplastic pollution pathway from synthetic garments to ocean ecosystems
  • Assess the scale of textile waste and its disposal consequences

Water: The Hidden Cost of Cotton

Water is perhaps the most stark of the textile industry's environmental footprints. Cotton is a thirsty crop: producing 1 kilogram of cotton fibre requires an estimated 10,000–20,000 litres of water when accounting for both irrigation and rainfall. In practical terms, a single cotton t-shirt consumes approximately 2,700 litres — equivalent to what one person drinks over 2.5 years.

10,000 Lwater for one pair of jeans (field to retail)
20%of global freshwater pollution from textile dyeing
FIGURE 2.1 — WATER FOOTPRINT COMPARISON (LITRES)

The consequences of unsustainable irrigation for cotton cultivation are not merely theoretical. The Aral Sea in Central Asia — once one of the four largest lakes in the world — has shrunk to less than 10% of its original volume since the 1960s, largely due to Soviet-era irrigation schemes to support cotton agriculture in Uzbekistan and Kazakhstan. The resulting ecological catastrophe displaced fishing communities, exposed toxic salts and pesticides on the former lakebed, and generated severe public health consequences in surrounding regions.

Chemical pollution in dyeing: The dyeing and finishing stage uses approximately 72 toxic chemicals, many of which are discharged into waterways in countries with weak regulatory enforcement. The Citarum River in West Java, Indonesia — draining a major textile manufacturing region — has been documented as one of the most polluted rivers on Earth, with concentrations of lead, cadmium, and arsenic far exceeding safe limits.

Carbon Emissions

The fashion industry is responsible for approximately 1.2 billion tonnes of CO₂ equivalent per year — more than the combined emissions from international aviation and maritime shipping (UNEP, 2019). This makes fashion one of the most carbon-intensive sectors in the global economy.

Emissions are distributed across the supply chain:

Supply Chain StageKey Emission SourcesApproximate Share
Fibre productionLand use, fertilisers (N₂O), synthetic fibre refining15–20%
Yarn & fabric productionEnergy for spinning, weaving, knitting machinery20–25%
Dyeing & finishingHigh heat processes; coal-powered factories in Asia35–40%
Transport & retailGlobal shipping, air freight for fast fashion5–10%
Consumer useWashing, tumble drying10–15%
End of lifeLandfill methane, incineration5–10%

A particularly significant driver is the energy mix of manufacturing countries. China and Bangladesh — which together account for the majority of global garment production — rely heavily on coal-fired electricity for factory operations. Until those energy systems decarbonise, the emissions intensity of textile production will remain high regardless of brand commitments.

Microplastics: Invisible Pollution

Synthetic textiles — polyester, nylon, acrylic — shed microscopic fibres during washing. A single wash of a polyester fleece jacket can release 700,000 individual fibres. These fibres pass through wastewater treatment systems largely uncaptured and enter rivers and oceans, where they persist indefinitely.

The International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN) estimates that synthetic textiles are responsible for 35% of primary microplastics released into the world's oceans — the largest single source. Microplastics have been documented in the deepest ocean trenches, in Arctic sea ice, in the lungs of seabirds, and in human blood. Their long-term effects on ecosystems and human health remain an active area of scientific research, but the trajectory of evidence is concerning.

Textile Waste

The fashion industry generates approximately 92 million tonnes of textile waste per year globally. The vast majority is landfilled or incinerated; less than 1% of clothing is recycled into new clothing fibres. In Australia specifically, roughly 800,000 tonnes of clothing and textiles are sent to landfill annually — approximately 31 kilograms per person per year.

The Atacama Desert, Chile has become one of the world's most visible symbols of fast fashion's waste problem. Thousands of tonnes of unsold or returned fast-fashion garments — shipped to Chile's Iquique free-trade zone and unable to be re-exported — are dumped in the desert, forming mountains of textile waste visible from satellite imagery.

MODULE 03 / 05

Social & Human Impact

Behind every garment is a person who made it. This module examines the labour conditions, wage structures, and systemic inequalities embedded in global textile supply chains, and why they persist.

LEARNING OBJECTIVES
  • Describe the demographic profile of the global garment workforce
  • Analyse the wage gap between living wage and actual pay in garment manufacturing countries
  • Explain the significance of the Rana Plaza disaster as a turning point in supply chain governance
  • Identify structural reasons why labour exploitation persists in fast fashion supply chains

Who Makes Our Clothes?

An estimated 75 million people work directly in garment manufacturing globally, with hundreds of millions more engaged in upstream processes — fibre farming, ginning, spinning, weaving. The industry is highly gendered: approximately 80% of garment workers are women, most of them young women from rural areas who have migrated to urban factory districts. For many, garment work offers a first formal-sector job and access to income independent of family structures — but under conditions that frequently violate basic labour rights.

80%of garment workers are women
~$3/dayaverage daily wage in Bangladesh's garment sector

Gender, Power, and the Garment Workforce

The feminisation of garment labour is not incidental — it is structural. In countries such as Bangladesh, Cambodia, and Vietnam, the preference for hiring women reflects a combination of factors that make women's labour cheaper and more controllable than men's under existing social conditions: lower reservation wages (women often accept lower pay than men for equivalent work), higher compliance with supervisory authority in contexts where women have limited labour market alternatives, and weaker unionisation in feminised workforces where social norms suppress collective organising.

Sociologist Naila Kabeer's (2000) research in Bangladesh documented the contradictory position of garment workers: factory work provides women with income, physical mobility, and a degree of independence from household patriarchal control that was previously unavailable to them. At the same time, the conditions under which that work is performed — forced overtime, verbal abuse, physical insecurity, poverty wages — constitute a structural form of gender-based exploitation. Kabeer described this as "the power of agency within structures of constraint."

Reproductive labour compounds the economic disadvantage. Women garment workers disproportionately carry unpaid domestic and childcare responsibilities outside working hours. When brands demand extended overtime to meet order deadlines, the burden falls unevenly on women workers who must then also manage household obligations. Maternity leave is frequently denied in practice, and pregnancy is a documented basis for dismissal in factories that lack effective enforcement of labour protections.

The case of Myanmar (2021–present): Following the military coup in Myanmar, global brands faced acute pressure to continue sourcing (abandoning workers) or exit (depriving workers of income). Most of the 700,000+ garment workers in Myanmar are women. This case illustrates how geopolitical events in producing countries create impossible choices when supply chains lack resilience and transparency — and workers, predominantly women, absorb the cost of corporate risk management decisions.

The Wage Gap

In major garment-producing countries, the gap between statutory minimum wages and estimated living wages is significant. A living wage is defined as the remuneration sufficient to afford a decent standard of living for a worker and their family, including food, housing, education, healthcare, and a modest discretionary income. Most garment minimum wages fall far short of this benchmark.

Why do wages stay low? Brands routinely pit supplier factories against one another in competitive tendering, squeezing margins to the point where labour costs must be minimised to win contracts. Factories facing thin margins resist wage increases, and workers — many of whom lack formal union rights or face violent suppression of organising — have limited bargaining power.

Rana Plaza: A Turning Point

On 24 April 2013, the Rana Plaza building in Savar, Bangladesh — housing five garment factories supplying major international brands — collapsed, killing 1,134 workers and injuring more than 2,500. It remains the deadliest industrial accident in the history of the garment industry.

Cracks had been visible in the building the day before the collapse. Bank and shop tenants on the lower floors evacuated immediately. Garment workers on the upper floors were ordered to return to their machines — under threat of losing a month's wages. The collapse was not a structural surprise; it was a foreseeable consequence of ignored building safety regulations, structural modifications made without permits, and the absence of effective accountability in the supply chain.

The Bangladesh Accord on Fire and Building Safety (2013) — signed by over 200 international brands following Rana Plaza — established a legally binding framework for factory inspections, remediation, and worker complaint mechanisms in Bangladesh. Subsequent iterations have expanded its scope. It is widely regarded as one of the most effective examples of binding supply chain accountability in the fashion industry.

Structural Drivers of Exploitation

Labour exploitation in garment manufacturing is not primarily the product of individual bad actors. It is structurally embedded in the political economy of the industry:

Buyer power concentration: A small number of mega-brands control enormous purchasing volumes and set the terms of trade for thousands of small-to-medium suppliers, who have little leverage to negotiate.

Supply chain opacity: Brands frequently subcontract to factories they have not audited, creating layers of deniability. A 2021 study found that fewer than 40% of global brands could identify all their second-tier suppliers.

Race to the bottom: As wages in one country rise, brands shift sourcing to lower-cost locations — from South Korea to China, to Bangladesh, now to Ethiopia and Myanmar. Workers in each country bear the cost of this mobility.

Weak regulatory enforcement: In many garment-producing countries, labour laws that exist on paper are poorly enforced due to underfunded inspection systems, corruption, and the economic importance of the industry to export earnings.

Consumer Behaviour

Consumption is the demand signal that drives the industry. This module examines the psychological, economic, and cultural mechanisms that shape how people buy, wear, and discard clothing — and how individual choices aggregate into systemic outcomes.

LEARNING OBJECTIVES
  • Apply the cost-per-wear framework to evaluate clothing purchasing decisions
  • Identify psychological drivers of overconsumption in fashion
  • Recognise and critically assess common forms of greenwashing
  • Distinguish between individual-level and structural-level responsibility

How Much Do We Actually Consume?

Global per-capita clothing consumption has roughly doubled over the past 20 years, driven by falling real prices and faster trend cycles. In Australia, the average person buys approximately 27 kilograms of new clothing annually — the second-highest per capita in the world after the United States. The average garment is estimated to be worn just 7 times before being discarded.

27 kgaverage new clothing purchased per Australian per year
7×average number of times a garment is worn before disposal

These statistics point to a structural mismatch between the resources embedded in garment production and the utility extracted from those garments. A cotton t-shirt that required 2,700 litres of water to produce and is worn three times before being thrown away represents a profound inefficiency from an ecological standpoint.

The Cost-Per-Wear Framework

Cost-per-wear (CPW) is a practical tool for evaluating the true economic value of a clothing purchase:

Cost-per-wear = Purchase price ÷ Number of times worn

A $200 jacket worn 150 times has a CPW of $1.33. A $20 fast-fashion jacket worn 4 times has a CPW of $5.00. The "cheap" garment costs nearly four times as much per use — while generating far more waste and consuming more resources per unit of utility delivered.

CPW is not merely an economic abstraction. It embeds a durability argument: higher-quality garments made from better materials, constructed to withstand repeated washing and physical use, deliver better value over time. The problem is that the fashion industry's pricing signal — cheap at point of sale — actively undermines rational long-term purchasing by making low-quality items appear affordable.

Psychological Drivers of Overconsumption

Consumer behaviour in fashion is shaped by psychological mechanisms that brands actively exploit:

Identity expression and self-concept: Clothing is a primary vehicle for identity construction and social signalling. Research in consumer psychology consistently finds that clothing purchases are often driven by aspirational self-image rather than functional need — what clothing scholar Susan Kaiser (2012) describes as the "fashion-identity nexus."

Social comparison and FOMO: Social media platforms have dramatically amplified social comparison in clothing. The "outfit of the day" culture on Instagram and TikTok, combined with algorithmic content delivery, creates persistent exposure to new styles and implicit pressure to participate in trend cycles. Fast-fashion brands co-produce this culture through influencer partnerships.

Hedonic shopping and retail therapy: Fashion shopping triggers dopamine release and is commonly used as an emotional regulation strategy. The fast-fashion model deliberately structures the shopping experience around novelty — new arrivals daily, limited-time sales — to maximise hedonic triggers.

Planned obsolescence through trend engineering: Unlike a mobile phone that becomes technically obsolete, a garment remains physically functional long after fashion trends have moved on. Fast fashion manufactures social obsolescence by ensuring that last season's item feels dated — even if it has years of functional life remaining.

Greenwashing: What to Watch For

As consumer environmental awareness has grown, brands have responded with sustainability marketing that is frequently misleading. Greenwashing — making environmental claims that overstate or misrepresent a product's sustainability — is pervasive in fashion.

Greenwashing TacticExampleWhy it Misleads
"Conscious Collection"H&M Conscious lineOften 1–2% of total production; rest of business unchanged
Vague "eco-friendly" claims"Made sustainably"No standard definition; no third-party verification
Recycled content framing"Made from recycled plastic bottles"Still sheds microplastics; does not address overproduction
Carbon offset schemes"Carbon neutral shipping"Offsets do not reduce emissions; quality and permanence vary widely

Red flag: When a brand prominently advertises a small sustainable line while simultaneously expanding total production volume, the net environmental impact is likely negative regardless of the "eco" collection. Look for systemic commitments — supply chain transparency reports, science-based emissions targets, and third-party certifications from credible bodies such as GOTS, bluesign, or the Higg Index.

Individual vs Structural Responsibility

A persistent tension in sustainability discourse concerns the degree to which environmental harm is attributable to individual consumer choices versus systemic factors. This debate is particularly sharp in fashion.

The "individual responsibility" framing — heavily promoted by the fossil fuel and fast-fashion industries — argues that consumer choices drive outcomes, and therefore the solution lies in more conscious individual purchasing. This framing is convenient for industry: it shifts moral responsibility onto consumers while leaving the structural incentives — cheap labour, externalised environmental costs, subsidised waste disposal — intact.

The alternative structural view argues that individual consumers make decisions within heavily constrained choice architectures: fast fashion is cheap because its costs are externalised; sustainable alternatives are expensive because they internalise what fast fashion does not; and low-income consumers often have no realistic access to higher-cost ethical alternatives. In this view, the primary policy levers are regulatory and fiscal — extended producer responsibility, mandatory labelling, minimum durability standards, and import duties calibrated to labour and environmental standards.

The most defensible position acknowledges both: individual choices matter at aggregate scale, and structural change is necessary for system-level transition. The two are not mutually exclusive.

Circular Economy & Solutions

The linear "take-make-dispose" model of fashion is not inevitable. This module examines the circular economy framework as applied to textiles, the business models emerging within it, and the policy landscape shaping systemic change.

LEARNING OBJECTIVES
  • Define circular economy principles and apply them to the textile sector
  • Evaluate circular business models including rental, resale, repair, and take-back schemes
  • Describe key policy mechanisms driving sustainable textile systems
  • Assess emerging material and technology solutions in the context of systemic change

From Linear to Circular

The conventional fashion supply chain follows a linear model: raw materials are extracted, transformed into products, sold, used briefly, and disposed of. Value is extracted once. At the end of the product's life — often premature — it becomes waste.

The circular economy (CE) framework, developed most comprehensively by the Ellen MacArthur Foundation in collaboration with McKinsey and IDEO, proposes an alternative: systems in which materials and products circulate at their highest value for as long as possible. In fashion, this means designing garments to last; enabling repair, resale, and rental; and ensuring that, at true end-of-life, fibre-to-fibre recycling is technically and economically feasible.

FIGURE 5.1 — CIRCULAR TEXTILE ECONOMY (SIMPLIFIED)

Circular Business Models in Practice

The circular economy in fashion is not theoretical. A growing range of business models is operationalising circular principles at different price points and market segments:

Clothing rental: Platforms such as Rent the Runway (US), GlamCorner (AU), and By Rotation (UK) allow consumers to access fashion without ownership. Rental models are particularly effective for occasion wear — garments that would otherwise be worn once. The model works by maximising utilisation rate per garment.

Resale and peer-to-peer platforms: Depop, Vestiaire Collective, eBay, ThredUp, and Facebook Marketplace facilitate secondary market transactions. The secondhand clothing market is growing at roughly 15–20% per year — faster than the primary market. In 2023, the global secondhand apparel market was valued at approximately USD 200 billion.

Brand-run take-back schemes: Patagonia's Worn Wear programme, H&M's garment collection, and Levi's SecondHand platform take back used garments for resale, repair, or recycling. These models vary substantially in quality and genuine circularity — a used garment resold is more circular than one sent to a recycling contractor who landfills 80% of intake.

Repair and care services: The Restory (UK), Patagonia's repair centres, and Nudie Jeans' free-repair-for-life policy extend garment lifespans by making repair economically rational for consumers. Nudie Jeans has documented that repaired jeans generate 75% lower CO₂ than producing an equivalent new pair.

Design for Longevity

Circular fashion begins at the design stage. The key principles are:

Durability: Stronger seams, quality hardware, colourfast dyes, and fabric weights appropriate to end use. Garments designed to last 10 years have 10× the use-phase before environmental burden is repeated.

Mono-material construction: Blended fabrics (e.g., polyester-cotton) are extremely difficult to recycle because the fibres cannot be separated economically with current technology. Designing in single-fibre compositions enables end-of-life circularity.

Repairability: Accessible seams, available spare buttons and zips, standardised components, and repair documentation (including QR codes linking to instructional videos).

Policy Landscape

Individual and corporate action operates within a policy context that shapes what is economically viable. Several significant policy developments are reshaping the textile sector:

PolicyJurisdictionKey Provisions
EU Strategy for Sustainable and Circular Textiles (2022)European UnionMandatory durability standards; digital product passports; EPR; ban on destruction of unsold garments (from 2024 for large companies)
Ecodesign for Sustainable Products Regulation (ESPR)European UnionMinimum durability, repairability, and recyclability requirements for textiles sold in the EU

The EU framework is the most comprehensive globally and is likely to function as a de facto global standard: brands selling into the EU market will need to comply, and it is economically irrational to maintain separate product lines for EU and non-EU markets. This is the so-called "Brussels effect" — EU regulation becoming a de facto global standard by virtue of market size.

Technology and Emerging Materials

Chemical recycling: Mechanical recycling of textiles (shredding and re-spinning) degrades fibre quality rapidly. Chemical recycling — breaking fibres down to their molecular constituents and repolymerising — promises closed-loop recycling for synthetic fibres. Companies such as Renewlane, Ambercycle, and Worn Again are developing commercial-scale processes, though scale remains limited.

Bio-based and biodegradable materials: Materials such as Mylo (mycelium leather), Piñatex (pineapple leaf fibre), and Tencel (lyocell from sustainably sourced wood pulp) offer alternatives to both conventional synthetics and resource-intensive natural fibres. Their scalability and genuine environmental benefits relative to conventional alternatives require careful life-cycle assessment.

Digital product passports: The EU's ESPR mandates digital product passports for textiles — QR codes or similar mechanisms that carry full lifecycle information: fibre composition, country of manufacture, care instructions, repair resources, and recycling pathways. This data infrastructure supports both consumer decision-making and end-of-life sorting.

Key takeaway: Technology alone will not resolve the structural problems of the textile industry. Chemical recycling of polyester is only useful if the volume of polyester entering the recycling stream does not exceed processing capacity — which requires reducing overproduction, not just improving end-of-life management. Systemic solutions require changing incentive structures, not just developing better technologies for managing the consequences of unchanged behaviour.

Australia-Specific Policy Context

Australia's policy environment for sustainable textiles has historically lagged the European Union, but several significant developments have emerged in recent years.

National Clothing Product Stewardship Scheme (NTCPS) and Seamless: The Seamless scheme, launched in late 2023, is Australia's national clothing stewardship initiative operated by the Australian Fashion Council (AFC). Brands, retailers, and importers pay a levy (AUD 0.04 per unit) into a fund that finances collection infrastructure, sorting technology, and fibre-to-fibre recycling R&D. As of 2025, participation remains voluntary — a significant limitation given that voluntary schemes consistently capture only the brands already most committed to sustainability. The scheme is subject to a mandatory trigger review if voluntary targets are not met.

National Waste Policy Action Plan 2019: The broader waste policy context includes national targets for 80% of solid waste diverted from landfill by 2030, which encompasses textiles. State-level implementation varies considerably — Victoria has the most developed infrastructure through Sustainability Victoria's Recycling Victoria strategy, while Western Australia's capacity lags.

Modern Slavery Act 2018 (Cth): Companies with annual consolidated revenue above AUD 100 million must submit annual modern slavery statements disclosing risks of modern slavery in their operations and supply chains, and actions taken to address them. The Act covers garment supply chains. Research by the Australian Council of Superannuation Investors (ACSI) has found that disclosure quality varies widely, with many statements identifying risks without specifying material remediation actions.

Product stewardship — emerging mandatory instruments: The Australian Government's Recycling and Waste Reduction Act 2020 provides the legislative framework for mandatory product stewardship schemes. Textiles are listed as a priority product, with government retaining the power to make participation mandatory if the voluntary scheme underperforms. Whether that trigger is pulled will depend substantially on political will and industry lobbying dynamics.

Where Australia sits globally: Relative to the EU, Australia is significantly behind on mandatory durability standards, digital product passports, and binding extended producer responsibility. Australia's per-capita textile consumption is among the world's highest, but its policy framework is substantially weaker than the market-scale regulatory pressure the EU is generating. For Australian researchers and policymakers, the EU framework provides both a model and an external pressure point — Australian brands exporting to EU markets will be required to comply with EU regulations regardless of domestic requirements.

Key Australian data point: Clean Up Australia estimates Australians send approximately 800,000 tonnes of clothing and textiles to landfill each year — roughly 31 kg per person per year, equivalent to 137 average t-shirts per person annually entering landfill. This is among the highest per-capita rates in the OECD and provides a baseline against which any future stewardship scheme outcomes should be measured.

Case Study 1: Patagonia

Patagonia is the most frequently cited example of a fashion brand whose sustainability commitments are substantive rather than cosmetic. Founded by Yvon Chouinard in 1973, the company has made environmental mission central to its identity and governance.

Supply chain transparency: Patagonia publishes a detailed supplier list including tier 2 and tier 3 suppliers — a standard that fewer than 10% of global apparel brands meet. Its Footprint Chronicles tool allows consumers to trace specific product supply chains to individual factories, with data on labour, environmental, and social performance at each facility.

Wage commitments: Patagonia is a certified Fair Trade brand for a significant portion of its line, meaning workers at Fair Trade-certified facilities receive a premium above the unit price that is managed democratically by worker committees. The company does not universally guarantee living wages across its supply chain, which is an honest limitation given supply chain complexity — but its FairTrade partnerships and supplier code of conduct are among the more substantive in the industry.

Environmental targets: Patagonia committed to being carbon neutral across its entire supply chain by 2025 — a more aggressive scope than most competitors who exclude Scope 3 (supply chain) emissions. It uses recycled materials across a substantial proportion of its product line and has been an early adopter of bluesign-certified fabrics.

Circular initiatives: The Worn Wear programme is arguably the most credible brand-operated circular initiative in fashion. Patagonia employs full-time repair staff, operates mobile repair trucks, and has published guides encouraging customers to repair rather than replace. In 2022, the company transferred ownership to a trust whose profits are directed to environmental causes — removing shareholder growth pressure from the governance structure.

Business model verdict: Patagonia makes high-quality, expensive, durable outdoor gear. Its premium price point funds higher supply chain standards and its durability reduces replacement frequency. The business model is more compatible with genuine sustainability than fast fashion. The key critique is scale: Patagonia's model works because it is a niche premium brand. Scaling its approach to mass-market volumes has not been demonstrated.

Case Study 2: H&M

H&M is the world's second-largest fashion retailer and the most studied example of the tension between fast-fashion scale and sustainability marketing.

Supply chain transparency: H&M publishes its supplier list — a relatively progressive step among fast-fashion peers — but has faced persistent criticism for the gap between disclosed tier 1 suppliers (direct garment factories) and the tier 2 and tier 3 suppliers (fabric mills, dye houses) where the most severe environmental and labour abuses typically occur.

Wage commitments: H&M's "Fair Living Wage" roadmap, launched in 2013, committed to achieving living wages for all workers in strategic supplier factories. A 2023 audit by the Clean Clothes Campaign found that wages at H&M's strategic suppliers had improved but remained below estimated living wage benchmarks in Bangladesh and Cambodia. The company's own reporting acknowledges the gap without committing to a firm timeline for closing it.

Environmental targets: H&M has set science-based emissions reduction targets and reports annually against them. The "Conscious Collection" — its flagship sustainable line — has been the subject of greenwashing litigation. In 2023, the Advertising Standards Authority in the UK upheld a complaint against H&M's sustainability marketing as misleading, finding that claims about the environmental credentials of "conscious" products could not be substantiated.

Circular initiatives: H&M's in-store garment collection programme has operated since 2013, collecting over 140,000 tonnes of garments. However, investigative reporting (including by the Swedish TV programme Uppdrag granskning) found that a substantial proportion of collected garments were landfilled or incinerated in receiving countries, and that the volume collected represents a tiny fraction of H&M's annual production output.

Business model verdict: H&M's fundamental business model — selling large volumes of low-price, trend-driven garments at high margins — is structurally incompatible with a meaningful reduction in environmental impact. Sustainability initiatives exist at the margin of the business without altering its core logic. Until production volumes are capped or reduced, incremental improvements in material sourcing are outpaced by growth.

Case Study 3: Shein

Shein represents the extreme endpoint of fast-fashion logic — an ultra-fast-fashion model operating almost entirely online, with a production system that bypasses many of the norms of traditional fashion retail.

Supply chain transparency: As of 2025, Shein discloses very limited supply chain information. A 2023 investigation by Channel 4 (UK) and subsequent reports found evidence of suppliers paying workers as little as USD 3 per 500-piece order — effectively 0.6 cents per garment. Shein contested specific figures but has not published audited supplier data.

Wage commitments: Shein has no publicly disclosed living wage commitment for its supply chain. Its 2023 ESG report acknowledged labour risk without providing verifiable data on actual wage levels or improvement timelines.

Environmental targets: In 2023, Shein announced a commitment to reduce absolute greenhouse gas emissions by 25% by 2030 from a 2021 baseline. Environmental analysts noted that this target applies to Scope 1 and 2 emissions only — excluding supply chain emissions, which constitute the overwhelming majority of Shein's environmental footprint.

Circular initiatives: Shein has launched a "Shein Exchange" resale platform for used Shein garments. Given that Shein's garments are made to low durability standards (partly because they are priced very low), the practical lifespan of garments available on the resale platform is limited. The platform has been criticised as a reputation management exercise rather than a genuine circular initiative.

Business model verdict: Shein's model — algorithmic trend identification, on-demand micro-batch manufacturing, near-zero product development cost, and aggressive pricing — produces the maximum volume of garments at the minimum durable quality, at scale. Its core business logic is the antithesis of a circular economy. Sustainability claims exist as marketing noise without structural content. Shein's planned IPO (as of 2025) would impose some disclosure requirements, but the fundamental business model is unlikely to change under public market pressure in the short term.

A note on data quality in fashion sustainability research: Many widely cited statistics in fashion sustainability — including some used in this course — originate from grey literature (industry reports, NGO publications) rather than peer-reviewed science. Where possible, figures have been corroborated across multiple sources. Students conducting original research should engage directly with primary data sources and be alert to methodological assumptions embedded in lifecycle assessment figures, which vary substantially depending on system boundaries, geographic assumptions, and allocation methods.
